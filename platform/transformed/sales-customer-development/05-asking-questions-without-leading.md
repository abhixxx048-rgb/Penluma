You ask a customer, "Wouldn't this save you a ton of time?" They smile and say, "Yeah, totally." You feel the rush. You go home, build the thing for six months, and launch to silence.

Here is what actually happened in that room: you didn't learn anything. You fished for a "yes," and a polite person handed you one. The question was rigged from the start.

This is the single most expensive mistake founders make in sales conversations. The good news is that it's also one of the easiest to fix once you see it.

## Why this matters

A sales conversation is really just a conversation made of questions. The quality of your questions decides the quality of the truth you get back. And truth is the only thing worth building a business on.

If your questions are leading, every "yes" you collect is worthless. You'll feel validated and build the wrong product, price it wrong, and target the wrong people. The data was poisoned before you wrote a single line of code.

Here is the relief, especially if you dread selling: asking good questions has nothing to do with being smooth or charming. It's about being genuinely **curious**. If you actually want to understand someone's problem, you're already most of the way there.

> **The core idea:** Your job in a sales or discovery conversation is to *learn the truth*, not to hear "yes." A leading question gives you a fake yes. A neutral question gives you real information.

## The two building blocks: open and closed questions

Every question you ask is one of two types. Learn the difference and you control the whole conversation.

An **open question** can't be answered in one word. It invites a story. It usually starts with *what, how, why, tell me, walk me through,* or *describe*.

> "How do you handle reprints when a customer rejects a proof?"

A **closed question** gets a "yes," "no," or a single fact. It starts with *do, did, is, are, have, how many,* or *when*.

> "Do you offer reprints?"

Both are useful. Open questions *open up* the conversation and get people talking. Closed questions *pin down* a fact once you need it: "So you printed 4,000 flyers last month, correct?"

The beginner mistake is asking mostly closed questions. That turns a conversation into a quiz, and a quiz teaches you almost nothing.

**Aim for roughly 80% listening, 20% talking.** If you're talking more than your customer, you're pitching, not discovering. Start wide and open; reach for closed questions only to confirm a detail.

## The poison: leading questions

A **leading question** quietly tells the other person, "Here is what I'm hoping you'll say." Most people are kind. They'll give you the answer you fished for just to be agreeable. You leave thrilled and completely misinformed.

This is the heart of Rob Fitzpatrick's book *The Mom Test*. The title comes from a simple truth: if you ask your mom whether your business idea is good, she'll say yes, because she loves you. The fix is to ask questions so neutral that even your mom couldn't lie to you.

Fitzpatrick's three rules:

1. Talk about **their life**, not your idea.
2. Ask about **specific things in the past**, not opinions about the future.
3. **Listen more, talk less.**

The trap to watch for is pitching disguised as a question. "Wouldn't it be great if there was a tool that did X?" is not research. It's a compliment trap. Everyone says "sure, sounds great," nobody pays, and you build the wrong thing for half a year.

### Before and after: turning leading questions neutral

Read these out loud. Notice how each "after" stops fishing and starts learning.

| Leading (poisoned) | Neutral (clean) |
|---|---|
| "Do you think this would save you time?" | "How long does that task take you today?" |
| "Wouldn't it be useful to automate invoices?" | "Walk me through how you send invoices now." |
| "Is pricing a real headache for you?" | "Tell me about the last time you priced a custom job." |
| "Would you pay $50 a month for this?" | "What do you spend on this problem today, in tools or time?" |
| "You'd love a faster checkout, right?" | "What happened the last time a customer abandoned their cart?" |
| "Don't you hate doing this manually?" | "What's the most frustrating part of your week?" |

See the pattern? The clean versions ask about **the actual past**: "the last time," "today," "now." The past is fact. The future is a daydream, and people are wildly optimistic about what they'll do and pay "someday."

Think of it like questioning a witness. A leading question is "You saw the blue car run the light, didn't you?" Any decent lawyer objects, because you planted the answer. A good discovery call is a fair interview: "What did you see?" Let them tell you. Their unprompted answer is the only evidence that counts.

## The funnel: move from wide to narrow

Don't fire questions at random. Move like a funnel. Start **wide** to find where the energy is, then go **narrow** into the juicy part. This mirrors how Neil Rackham's *SPIN Selling* works, built on research across more than 35,000 sales calls: you ask in a deliberate order instead of blurting your pitch.

- **Wide:** "Tell me about how you run the print shop." *(Find a topic where they light up.)*
- **Narrower:** "You mentioned proofs are a pain. Tell me more." *(Zoom into the one juicy problem.)*
- **Narrow:** "Walk me through the last proof that got rejected." *(Get the specific, recent, real story.)*

Once you've found a real problem, SPIN gives you four question types to layer in:

1. **Situation** — context. "How many orders do you handle a week?" Ask only a few of these; don't interrogate.
2. **Problem** — difficulties. "Where does that process break down?"
3. **Implication** — the cost of the problem. "When proofs get rejected, what does that do to your delivery dates?" This is the powerful one. It helps the person *feel* the size of the pain in their own words.
4. **Need-payoff** — the value of fixing it. "If that never happened again, what would change for you?" Notice that *they* describe the value. It isn't your sales pitch; it's their conclusion.

## The most underrated skill: silence

After you ask a real question, **stop talking**. Count to five in your head. The pause feels excruciating to you, but to the other person it's just thinking time. Given a few seconds of quiet, most people keep going and tell you the deeper, truer thing they were holding back.

The mistake is rescuing the silence. You ask a great question, they pause, you panic and add, "...like, is it the cost, or the time, or...?" You just handed them easy answers and buried the real one. Ask, then zip it.

Here's how it plays out:

> **You:** "What's the hardest part of fulfilling a rush order?"
>
> **Them:** "Honestly... the timing, I guess." *(pause — you say nothing for four seconds)*
>
> **Them:** "...okay, the real problem is I have no idea where the job is on the floor. I have to physically walk over and ask Dave. Twice a day. It drives me insane."

That second answer is the gold. It only surfaced because you stayed quiet.

## Follow-up probes: how to dig deeper

You rarely get the truth on the first answer. You get it on the third. Keep a handful of gentle **probes** ready. A probe is a short phrase that asks for more without changing the subject.

- "Tell me more about that."
- "What happened next?"
- "Why was that hard?"
- "Walk me through the last time that happened."
- "What did you do about it?"
- "How often does that come up?"
- "What did that cost you, in time, money, or stress?"

That fourth one, "What did you do about it?", is sneakily important. If they did nothing, the pain may be small. Real pain makes people act.

Listen hard for **the last time they tried to fix it themselves**: a clumsy spreadsheet, a hired temp, a half-built script. That's proof the pain is real and that budget exists. People only hack around problems that genuinely hurt.

## The 5 Whys: reaching the root cause

When you hit a problem, ask "why" up to five times, each "why" aimed at the previous answer. This technique comes from Sakichi Toyoda at Toyota, used to find the *root* cause instead of the symptom. Watch it work in a sales conversation:

> "We lose customers after the first order." → **Why?**
> "They say reordering is annoying." → **Why?**
> "They have to re-upload their artwork every time." → **Why?**
> "We don't save their files anywhere they can reach." → **Why?**
> "Our system has no customer account area." → **Why?**
> "We never built one because we didn't think it mattered."

Root cause found. The "churn problem" is really a "no saved-design library" problem, which is something you can actually build and sell.

Use this gently. Five blunt "whys" in a row feels like an interrogation. Soften them: "Huh, why's that?", "And what's behind that?", "What causes that to happen?"

## Common misconceptions

**"Closed questions are bad."** No. They're precise tools for confirming a fact you already need. The problem is *only* asking closed questions, which strips out all the story.

**"A 'yes' means you've validated the idea."** A "yes" to a leading question means nothing. Validation is when someone shows you the workaround they built, the money they already spend, or the introduction they make to a colleague with the same pain.

**"Asking about money is rude or premature."** Asking what someone *would* pay is a fantasy question. Asking what they *already* spend on the problem is just understanding their world. Spending is fact; willingness is fiction.

**"Good questioners are naturally smooth talkers."** The opposite. The best questioners talk least. They're curious, patient, and comfortable with silence.

## Two question shapes to avoid

**Double-barreled questions** jam two questions into one: "Do you struggle with pricing and inventory?" The person answers one and ignores the other, and you can't tell which. Ask one thing at a time. Split it: "Tell me about pricing." Then, later, "Now, inventory. How's that going?"

**Jargon questions** are stuffed with your insider words: "How do you handle your fulfillment-pipeline throughput?" Your customer hears noise. Use *their* words: "What happens from when an order comes in to when it ships?"

## Talking about money without flinching

Founders freeze on money questions. The trick is to anchor against money they **already spend**, not money they might spend someday. Try these:

- "How are you solving this today, and what does that cost you?"
- "Is there a budget for this already, or would you have to create one?"
- "Who would need to sign off on a purchase like this?"
- "What would have to be true for this to be worth paying for?"
- "When something like this costs you a customer, what's that worth to you?"

Frame any price against the pain, not against your costs. "You said a rejected proof costs you a full day and an angry customer. How many of those do you get a month?" Now any number you name sits right next to a number *they* gave you.

## How to use this: your call playbook

1. **Open wide.** Start with a broad, easy question about their day. "Walk me through a normal day running the shop."
2. **Follow the energy.** When they light up about a topic, zoom in there. Ignore your script.
3. **Stay in the past.** Swap every "would you" for a "what happened the last time." The past is fact.
4. **Ask one plain question at a time.** No double-barrels, no jargon. Use their words.
5. **Then shut up.** Count to five. Let the deeper answer arrive.
6. **Probe for the third answer.** "Tell me more." "What did you do about it?" "What did that cost you?"
7. **Hunt for the workaround.** Find the spreadsheet, the temp, the script. That's proof of real pain and real budget.
8. **Anchor money to spending, not willingness.** Ask what they pay today, never what they'd pay tomorrow.

### A copy-and-paste question bank

Keep this open during calls. Pick a few; don't run the whole list like a script.

1. "Walk me through a normal day running the shop."
2. "What's the most frustrating part of that?"
3. "Tell me about the last time that went wrong."
4. "What did you do about it?"
5. "How often does that happen?"
6. "What does it cost you when it does, in time, money, or stress?"
7. "How are you solving it today?"
8. "What have you already tried? Why didn't it stick?"
9. "If this problem vanished tomorrow, what would change for you?"
10. "Who else deals with this? Could you introduce me?"

## Conclusion

If you remember one thing, make it this: **the past tells the truth, the future tells you what people wish were true.** Rewrite every leading "would you" into a neutral "what happened the last time," and your conversations stop flattering you and start informing you.

That's the difference between building what people clap for and building what people pay for.
