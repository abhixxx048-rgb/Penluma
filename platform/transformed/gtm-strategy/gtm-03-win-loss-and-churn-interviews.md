A study tracked 723 people who cancelled a software subscription and compared what they *said* at cancellation with the real reason they left. The two matched only 27% of the time.

Read that again. Nearly three out of four times, the reason a customer hands you is not the reason they actually left. And the single most popular fake reason is the one founders trust most: "it was too expensive."

If you build your next feature, cut your price, or rewrite your pitch based on what people say on the way out, you are steering by a broken compass. The good news is the real reason is almost never hidden. It is usually sitting one short phone call away.

## Why this matters

Early on, you don't have a marketing problem or a sales problem. You have a **truth problem**.

Every customer who signs up, walks away, or cancels is running a free experiment on whether your product is worth paying for. Right now, most of that signal is evaporating unrecorded. Someone decides you're not worth it, clicks cancel, and the single most valuable piece of information your company could own disappears forever.

The most expensive mistake an early founder makes is building the next thing based on a *guess* about why the last five deals died, when the actual reason was a 20-minute conversation away.

Win/loss and churn interviews are the discipline of catching that signal while it's still warm, before a busy customer collapses a messy, multi-cause story into one tidy, ego-protecting headline.

And here's the part that should make you feel lucky rather than behind: when you're small, you have so few customers that you can personally talk to *every single one*. That isn't a weakness of being early. It's the biggest research advantage you will ever have, and most founders never use it.

## The reason people don't tell you the truth

It's not that customers are lying to be cruel. It's that two very human things happen.

**First, rationalization.** A decision to leave is messy. There was friction, then confusion, then a bad week, then a competitor's email, then a quiet "this isn't worth it." Remembering all of that is hard work. So the brain does what brains do: it compresses the whole tangle into one clean story that also happens to protect the ego. "Too expensive" sounds smarter than "I never figured out how to set it up." "No time" sounds better than "I lost interest."

**Second, social smoothness.** Telling a real person "your onboarding made me feel stupid" is uncomfortable. "It was a budget thing" lets everyone off the hook politely.

So the stated reason isn't a lie exactly. It's a *label* the customer reaches for so the conversation can end. Your job is to gently get underneath the label.

## The four groups you should learn from

Most founders track "wins" and "losses" and stop there. That misses where early-stage products actually bleed. There are really four groups worth learning from, and each teaches a different lesson.

### Won customers

People who signed up *and* actually got value. They teach you what's working in your pitch and who your real ideal customer is. Founders skip them ("we already won, move on") and never learn the repeatable reason they win.

### Lost to a competitor

They evaluated you and chose a named alternative. This reveals genuine feature or positioning gaps. Note: people rarely volunteer the competitor's name unless you ask carefully.

### Lost to "no decision"

They engaged, then did nothing. They stayed with their old spreadsheet, their old email workflow, their status quo. This is the quiet killer most founders mislabel as "not interested" and throw away.

Research from *The Jolt Effect* (Matthew Dixon and Ted McKenna) found that **40 to 60% of B2B deals die to "no decision," not to a competitor.** For a product sold to busy, non-technical buyers, "I'm too swamped to switch" will likely dominate your loss column. That should reshape your roadmap far more than any rival's feature list.

### Churned and never-activated customers

Two flavors here, and the second is sneaky:

- **Churned:** they paid, then cancelled. This teaches you why value didn't *hold*.
- **Never activated:** they signed up and then went dark. Never finished setup, never went live, never came back. This is almost always the biggest leak at an early company, and it's *invisible in your revenue reports* because these people never paid. It doesn't feel like a loss, so nobody studies it.

If you study only wins and losses, you will be blind to the two groups that are probably draining you most: no-decision losses and never-activated signups. Both are activation problems, not feature problems.

## The skill that changes everything: laddering

This is the single most important technique in the whole discipline, and it's simple.

**Laddering** means you take whatever the customer says, and instead of accepting it, you ask what it *meant*. Then you ask what *that* meant. You repeat, gently, 5 to 7 times, until you hit something at the level of a real business consequence or a real feeling. The first answer is the label. The truth is usually three to five questions below it.

Here's a real churn example. A customer said the reason they left was *"the reporting wasn't flexible enough."* A founder who takes that at face value spends three months building a fancy report builder. But laddered, it went like this:

- Which report? *Territory filtering.*
- How often did you need it? *Every week, for a leadership meeting.*
- What happened when it didn't work? *"It made me look bad in front of my boss."*

The real reason they churned was **professional credibility**, not reporting flexibility. No survey on Earth surfaces that. And the fix is completely different from "build a better report builder."

### Watch "too expensive" fall apart

Here's how a typical "it was too expensive" loss unravels when you refuse the first answer:

- *"It was just too expensive for us right now."*
- **Too expensive compared to what?** → *"My old site is like $30 a month, this felt like a lot more."*
- **When you pictured paying more, what did you expect to get back?** → *"Honestly I wasn't sure. I never really got it set up, so I couldn't see how it'd make me money."*
- **What stopped you from getting set up?** → *"I started, but configuring one item took me an hour and I have 200 of them. I didn't have the time."*
- **If setup had taken five minutes per item, would the price still feel too high?** → *"No. If it was actually working for me I'd happily pay it."*

That was never a price loss. "Too expensive" was a polite proxy for "I never reached value because setup is too heavy." If you'd believed the survey answer, you'd have cut your price (wrecking your economics) and *still* lost the next ten customers to the same setup wall.

### The magic question

Notice the move in the second-to-last step: **"If that one thing were fixed, would the rest have been enough?"** This single isolating question tells you whether you found the real blocker or just another symptom. Keep it in your back pocket for every call.

A few laddering prompts worth memorizing:

- "What do you mean by ___?"
- "Tell me more about that."
- "What happened right before that?"
- "What did that cost you, or how did that feel?"
- "Walk me through the last time that happened, step by step."
- And the most powerful one of all: **silence.** Ask, then stop talking. Count to three. People fill the gap with the real answer.

## Common misconceptions

**"Price is our biggest churn driver."** Usually false. In the 723-customer study, about a third of churners blamed price, but only about one in three of *those* turned out to have a genuine price problem once probed. Most "price" complaints are [value problems wearing a price mask](/blog/business-financial-literacy/08-pricing-fundamentals-cost-plus-vs-value-vs-competitive). If you discount in response, you destroy your margins and fix nothing.

**"We lose deals to competitors."** Often false. You more likely lose to *nothing at all*, to [the customer's own inertia](/blog/psychology-of-decisions/07-the-big-biases-ii-loss-aversion-sunk-cost-confirmation-overconfidence). That's a different and more winnable problem.

**"A cancellation survey tells us why people leave."** No. A survey catches everyone, which is useful, but a churning customer is mentally checked out and clicking the fastest plausible option. The survey is a *recruiting tool* for [real interviews](/blog/sales-customer-development/04-the-mom-test-talk-to-customers-without-fooling-yourself), not the answer itself.

**"The loudest feature request is the most important one."** Dangerous. The customer who wants a specific feature will describe it in vivid detail, so it *feels* urgent. But that loud minority can be far smaller than the silent majority who quietly never activated. Count before you build.

## How to use this: your weekly routine

You don't need a research firm. You need a spreadsheet, a calendar link, and the discipline to do one call a week. Here's the playbook.

1. **Build the list today.** Pull every account from the last four weeks into a spreadsheet and tag each one: Won, Lost, Churned, or Never-activated. This step alone often shocks founders about where the real leak is.

2. **Interview inside the window.** Talk to people 7 to 14 days after they leave. Sooner and you get raw venting with no insight. Later and rationalization has hardened into "price" or "no time." For churn, 14 days is your hard deadline.

3. **Run the interview yourself, for now.** As the founder with no sales team, you're both neutral *and* credible. People will tell you things they'd never write in a survey. Hand it off only when you can no longer keep up.

4. **Do not defend the product. Ever.** The instant you say "well, actually we *do* have that," candor dies. Your only job in the room is to understand, never to sell, fix, or rebut. Ask, shut up, count to three.

5. **Ladder past the first answer.** Refuse "too expensive" and "no time" at face value. Go 5 to 7 levels down. Ask the magic question: "If that one thing were fixed, would the rest have been enough?"

6. **Ask for the top three reasons, not one.** The real dealbreaker is often reason number two. Keep calls under 30 minutes and under a dozen questions.

7. **Tag every call against a fixed list within 10 minutes of hanging up.** Especially: did "price" survive the laddering, or did it collapse into "never saw value"? Split those two. They lead to opposite decisions.

8. **Recruit generously.** Invite about four times the number of calls you want (want 5, ask 20), follow up a couple of times with varied subject lines, and offer a modest thank-you gift. Don't overpay; gratuitous incentives attract people who'll say anything.

9. **Count monthly.** Once you have a stack of tagged calls, compute the simple ratios: what share of losses were "no decision," how many trials actually went live, what fraction of churn is genuinely fixable. Even with eight calls per group, the dominant pattern jumps out.

A practical anchor: put a recurring 30-minute block on your calendar every Monday called "Talk to a dead deal." Protect it like a customer meeting. One call a week is about 50 a year, which is full saturation across all four groups, at the cost of a few gift cards.

### One number worth watching above all

Track your **activation rate**: the share of signups that actually [reached first value](/blog/product-sense-empathy/09-reducing-friction-flows-steps-progressive-disclosure) (got set up, went live, did the core thing). If that number is 25%, then 75% of every dollar you spend on acquisition is leaking out *before* anyone sees value. No feature you ship will fix a leaky bucket. The never-activated interview is your highest-leverage research, precisely because nobody else is doing it.

## Conclusion

The one thing to remember: **the reason a customer gives you is a label, not the truth, and the truth is three questions deeper.** Refuse the first answer, ladder down to the real consequence, and you'll discover that most of what looks like a price problem or a feature gap is actually an activation problem in disguise.

Do this while you're small enough to call everyone. That window closes fast.

And here's the thread to pull next. Once you've separated *real* price objections from fake ones, you'll have something most founders only guess at: honest evidence of what customers will actually pay. That's the raw material for [pricing research](/blog/how-to-make-money/14-pricing-value-capture) that doesn't rely on asking people the one question they're worst at answering: "how much would you pay?"
