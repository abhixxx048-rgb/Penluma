**This document explains the key numbers a software business uses to know if it is making money per customer - and which ones actually matter for a small, early-stage business like this one. It matters because getting these numbers wrong leads to bad decisions: spending too much to get customers, or thinking the business is healthy when it is not.**

**The main parts explained simply:**

- **How much it costs to get one customer (CAC)** - This is every rupee/dollar you spend to bring in one new store owner: your time, tools, writing, ads, everything. The document warns that many businesses lie to themselves by only counting ad spend and forgetting to count their own working hours. The honest number is often 2–4 times bigger than it first looks.

- **How much a customer is worth over their life (LTV)** - This is the total profit you expect to earn from one customer before they leave. The document explains that this number is very easy to get wrong, especially when customers leave quickly (as small businesses often do). Treat it as a rough guess, not a fact.

- **The 3:1 rule (LTV vs cost to get a customer)** - A popular saying is that a customer should be worth at least 3 times what it cost to get them. The document explains this rule was made for big, mature companies and is often misused by early startups. Do not use it as a pass/fail test at an early stage.

- **How many months until a customer pays you back (CAC Payback)** - This is the most useful number for a small, cash-tight business. It simply asks: how many months of profit from this customer does it take to cover what I spent to get them? Under 12 months is healthy; over 18 months is a warning sign. Always check whether customers are actually staying that long.

- **Magic Number and Rule of 40** - Two metrics used by large, well-funded companies to measure growth vs. profit. The document is clear: ignore these completely until the business reaches roughly $5M+ in annual revenue. Building reports for them now is wasted time.

- **Common myths to ignore** - Several widely repeated rules are simply wrong or only apply to big companies. The document lists them so you can spot and dismiss them when they appear in articles or pitch decks.

**What to do with this:** Track one number first - how many months it takes a new store to pay back what it cost to get them. Make sure that number includes your own time, not just cash spent. Everything else can wait.
