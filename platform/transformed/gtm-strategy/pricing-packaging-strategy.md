Three prices on a page can quietly decide whether your product grows or stalls. Get the structure right and most buyers happily pick the plan you wanted them to pick. Get it wrong and you either scare people off or hand away revenue you earned. The strange part is that the math is rarely the hard bit. The psychology is.

This is the playbook behind the most common pricing pattern in software: **Good-Better-Best**. You will learn why three tiers beat five, how to choose what you charge for, and why pricing *below* a weaker competitor is often a mistake.

## Why this matters

Pricing is the highest-leverage decision you rarely revisit. A landing page tweak might lift conversion a few percent. The right tier structure changes how much every single customer pays, forever, and which ones you attract in the first place.

Most teams treat pricing as an afterthought, a number they pick once and defend out of habit. Meanwhile, **about 43% of SaaS companies underprice their product**, according to research from OpenView. That is not a rounding error. It is nearly half the market quietly leaving money on the table because pricing felt scary to get wrong.

The good news: you do not need a pricing consultant or a spreadsheet from another galaxy. You need a clear structure, the right unit to charge on, and the discipline to test it on your own customers instead of borrowing someone else's numbers.

## The core idea: three tiers, one hero

Good-Better-Best is exactly what it sounds like. You offer three named plans:

- **Good** — a lower entry tier for the smallest or most price-sensitive buyer.
- **Better** — the hero in the middle, the plan most customers *should* buy.
- **Best** — a premium anchor that makes the middle feel like a smart deal.

This is not a fringe tactic. In OpenView's study of 104 SaaS companies, **72 of them (about 69%) used some form of Good-Better-Best packaging** — including names like Slack and DocuSign. When two-thirds of a market converges on the same shape, it is usually because it works.

### Why three, and not five

It is tempting to add more tiers to "cover every customer." Resist it. Past three or four options, buyers freeze. Faced with too many choices, many people make no choice at all — and a hesitant buyer is a lost buyer.

Three tiers also unlock a quiet bit of psychology called the **decoy effect** (sometimes called the compromise effect). When a middle option sits between a cheaper-but-thinner plan and a pricier anchor, it starts to look like the rational, grown-up choice. People dislike feeling cheap, and they dislike overspending, so they reach for the comfortable middle.

The classic illustration comes from behavioral economist Dan Ariely's retelling of a magazine subscription offer. People were given three choices:

1. Web only — $59
2. Print only — $125
3. Print **and** web — $125

Almost nobody wanted print-only at the same price as the bundle. So why include it? Because it made the $125 combo look obviously superior. When that "useless" middle option was removed, far more people chose the cheap web-only plan, and total revenue dropped. The decoy was not there to sell. It was there to make the target option shine.

Your premium "Best" tier plays the same role. Many customers will never buy it. It still earns its keep by making your hero middle tier feel like the reasonable pick.

A practitioner rule of thumb is to aim for **roughly 60 to 70% of paying customers landing on the middle tier**. Treat that as a target to instrument and check, not a guaranteed law of nature.

## Choose what you charge for: the value metric

Before you set any prices, decide the unit you charge on. This is your **value metric**, and it matters more than the dollar amounts.

A good value metric does three things:

1. **It is easy to understand.** The buyer can explain it to their boss in one sentence.
2. **It tracks the value they receive.** As they get more out of the product, they pay a little more.
3. **It grows with the customer.** Their success becomes your expansion revenue, automatically.

Picking the wrong metric quietly sabotages everything. Consider a tool sold to small-shop owners who *are* the only person using it. Charging **per seat** punishes the solo operator for being solo — they have one seat and feel nickel-and-dimed. Charging a **percentage of their sales** feels like a tax on their own hard work, and they resent every invoice.

A better metric for that buyer is often **per location or per storefront** — one real-world thing they already count and understand. The unit should map to something the customer can picture and predict.

### Pure usage pricing is not the modern default — hybrid is

There is a popular myth that everyone is racing toward pay-as-you-go, metered billing. The data tells a more nuanced story. Usage-based pricing adoption rose for a few years, then actually *dipped* — and most "usage-based" companies are really **hybrid**: a predictable base subscription plus a light usage layer on top.

That hybrid shape exists for a reason. A pure metered bill is terrifying to a non-technical buyer. They cannot forecast it, they fear bill-shock, and an unpredictable invoice erodes trust. A **steady base price plus a gentle usage allowance** gives them a number they can budget around, with room to grow into.

So the structure to reach for is: a clear monthly price per tier, plus a soft allowance on whatever usage metric fits (orders, projects, contacts, whatever the customer already tracks).

## Don't underprice a stronger product

Here is the trap that catches nearly half the market. You build something more capable than a competitor, then you price it *below* their comparable plan, telling yourself you are being "competitive" and avoiding overcharging.

This logic is self-defeating. You cannot claim "we do more than them" and "we cost less than them" as evidence of *correct* pricing. Lower price signals lower value. A more capable product priced under a weaker rival's hero tier is exactly how you join the 43% who underprice.

Think about it from the buyer's side. If your product does strictly more — more features, more value, fewer headaches — and costs less, the buyer's instinct is not "what a deal." It is "what is wrong with it?"

The disciplined move is to **anchor against what competitors actually charge, then price to your value, not below it.** If a rival's most popular plan is $244 a month for a narrower product, a richer product can credibly sit at or above that number. If real willingness-to-pay research with customers supports going higher, go higher. For a stronger product, the bigger risk is almost always pricing too low, not too high.

One caution: competitor prices change often. Use them as anchors for your reasoning, not as fixed inputs you copy.

## The expansion engine: a soft cap that never blocks a sale

Your tiers are not just three static boxes. The space between them is your growth engine. The cleanest way to drive customers upward is a **soft usage allowance** — and the word *soft* is doing all the work.

Here is the principle: **never block a live sale.** Imagine a shop owner mid-transaction who suddenly sees "you've exceeded your plan" and loses the order. That is a catastrophe — bill-shock and a broken business moment in the same instant. They will remember it, and they will leave.

Instead, the allowance nudges:

1. **Soft cap, never a hard cutoff.** When a customer passes their limit, the order still goes through. Nothing breaks.
2. **Plain-language nudges, not surprise charges.** Around 80% of the allowance, a calm banner appears: *"Great month — you've used 240 of your 300 included orders. Shops growing like yours usually move up for more headroom."*
3. **Upgrade only on explicit consent.** You never silently start charging overage. The customer chooses to upgrade or opts in to small overage charges. The default is no surprise on the invoice.
4. **If you offer overage, keep it legible and never punitive.** Price it so that upgrading is always the better deal. The nudge stays honest: the message is "you're growing, let's get you set up," not "gotcha."
5. **Meter the one number they already track.** Orders, not API calls or abstract "credits." The metric should be something a non-technical owner can forecast in their head.

This is what turns expansion into a natural byproduct of the customer's *own success* — the upgrade arrives exactly when their willingness to pay is highest, because business is good.

## Be honest about retention math

A quick reality check that saves a lot of pain. **Net revenue retention (NRR)** measures how much an existing group of customers grows or shrinks in spending over time, after upgrades, downgrades, and cancellations. Above 100% means your existing base expands on its own.

Ambitious SaaS decks love to promise 120% NRR. For a low-priced, small-business customer base, that is usually fantasy. The harsh data: only a tiny fraction of companies with very low revenue per account ever exceed 100% NRR, and the median for small-business SaaS sits around 97%, per ChartMogul's research.

If your average price is modest, a realistic, honest target is **roughly 100 to 105% NRR** — reached through the soft-allowance upgrade path and genuine add-ons, not by pretending aggressive expansion will rescue a low-price base. Set the achievable number. Your roadmap and your investors will both thank you.

## Common misconceptions

**"More tiers means more customers covered."** Reality: more tiers means more paralysis. Three options convert better than six.

**"The premium tier is a failure if few people buy it."** Reality: the anchor tier earns its keep by making the middle tier look reasonable. Low sales on it can be exactly right.

**"Charging less than competitors is the safe choice."** Reality: for a stronger product, underpricing is the dangerous choice. It signals low value and starves your growth.

**"Everyone is moving to pure usage-based pricing."** Reality: most "usage-based" companies are hybrid. A predictable base plus a light usage layer beats scary metered billing for most buyers.

**"A free plan is always good for growth."** Reality: if you carry real cost to serve each customer, a permanent free plan can bleed you. A time-boxed trial that gracefully downgrades often works better than free-forever.

## How to use this

Here is a concrete sequence to put this into practice:

1. **Pick your value metric first.** Choose the one unit your customer already counts and that grows with them. Avoid seats if the buyer is a solo operator, and avoid revenue-share if it feels like a tax.
2. **Build three tiers, name them in your customer's language.** "Solo," "Growing," "Multi-Location" beats "Standard / Pro / Enterprise." Make the middle tier your obvious hero.
3. **Anchor against real competitor prices, then price to your value.** If you do more, do not sit below a weaker rival's hero plan. Verify those competitor prices at decision time, because they shift.
4. **Add a soft usage allowance, never a hard wall.** Nudge in plain language at 80% and 100%. Let upgrades happen on consent. Never block a live transaction.
5. **Offer annual billing at "two months free."** Annual lock-in is one of the strongest defenses against small-business churn. Show it as the recommended option.
6. **Design the pricing page for clarity.** Three transparent tiers, the middle one badged "Most Popular," plain outcome-led descriptions, a trust signal (customer count or star rating) near the price, and a clean mobile layout.
7. **Set an honest NRR target and instrument everything.** Track your tier mix. If 60 to 70% are not landing on the hero tier, fix the page layout and framing before you touch the prices.
8. **Test on your own funnel.** Borrowed lift percentages from someone else's blog are directional at best. Run your own A/B tests on price anchors, the "Most Popular" badge, and your nudge copy.

A note on running a free trial alongside three tiers: let the trial show off your value (give people real capability so they reach an "aha" moment fast), but reveal the full three-tier choice at the upgrade moment, ideally with the customer's own usage mapped to the tier that fits. The trial sells the *value*. The pricing page sells the *choice*. They work in sequence, not in conflict.

## Conclusion

If you remember one thing, remember this: **pricing is not a number, it is a structure.** Three tiers, a hero in the middle, a value metric your customer actually understands, and a soft allowance that grows with their success — that is the machine. The dollar amounts are just dials you tune on top of it.

The deeper lesson is that good pricing respects how people actually decide. They avoid extremes, they fear surprise bills, and they read a low price as a low-quality signal. Build for those instincts and your pricing page starts working *for* you.

So here is the thread worth pulling next: if the structure decides *how much* people pay, what decides *whether they ever start paying at all*? That is the story of trials, onboarding, and the first "aha" moment — the conversion funnel that feeds every tier you just built.
