A bus wrapped bumper to bumper in glossy vinyl and a shampoo label stuck to a bottle look like the same craft from a distance. They are not. One was sprayed by a digital printer that has never made the same print twice. The other was stamped half a million times by a spinning rubber plate. Mix those two worlds up in a quote, and you either bankrupt a sign shop or quote a snack-bag run that no factory will ever take.

These are the two big "other" worlds of print: **wide-format inkjet** for big, unique things, and **flexography** for mass-produced packaging. They almost never compete for the same job. Knowing which is which lets you price, preflight, and produce each one correctly.

## Why this matters

If you build software for print shops, sell signage, or just order it, this is the line that decides everything downstream: the machine, the ink, the material, the turnaround, and most of all the **price shape**.

Wide-format is priced by area, like buying carpet by the square foot. Flexo is priced by setup plus run length, like tooling up a factory line. Quote one with the other's math and the number comes out absurd.

Get the distinction right and you can answer the only question a customer actually cares about: "What's the cheapest, best way to make *my* thing?"

Here is the whole idea in one breath:

- **Wide-format inkjet** is a big digital printer that sprays ink, the same basic idea as your home printer, but 1.5 to 8-plus feet wide (grand-format goes past 100 inches, up to billboards). **No plates**, so every single print can be different at no extra cost.
- **Flexography (flexo)** is a high-speed roll-fed press that uses a soft, flexible rubbery **plate** with a raised image, like a giant ink stamp on a spinning cylinder. It needs a plate per design per color, so it only makes financial sense at tens of thousands of copies.

Think of it this way. Wide-format is a **photocopier**: push a button, get one of whatever you want, change it freely. Flexo is a **cookie cutter**: expensive to make the cutter, but once you have it, stamping out 500,000 identical cookies is dirt cheap per cookie.

## Wide-format inkjet: the big-and-flexible world

Wide-format is **digital**. Artwork goes from a file, through software that turns it into dots, straight to a printhead that sweeps back and forth spraying microscopic droplets. No plate to make means it shines on short runs, one-offs, and anything where every piece is unique.

The usual jobs:

- **Banners** in vinyl or fabric, indoor and outdoor.
- **Posters and point-of-sale displays**, like the standing cardboard display by the checkout.
- **Vehicle wraps**, printed vinyl that covers a car, van, or bus.
- **Rigid signage** such as lobby signs, real-estate signs, and directional signs on stiff board.
- **Wall, window, and floor graphics**, backlit displays, canvas, and **soft signage** (printed fabric for trade-show booths).

### The four inks, and why the choice matters most

The single biggest decision in wide-format is **which ink**, because the ink decides what you can print on, how long it survives outdoors, how fast it dries, and whether it is safe to run indoors. ("Chemistry" here just means what the ink is made of and how it sticks and dries.) Four families dominate.

**Eco-solvent** carries pigment in a mild, low-odor solvent, a liquid that slightly "bites" into the surface. That bite keys the ink into uncoated vinyl and makes it waterproof and scratch-resistant with no primer. It is the workhorse of the sign trade: cheaper machines and ink, around 3 years outdoors unlaminated and 5-plus laminated. The catch is **dwell time**, the drying and off-gassing wait before you can laminate, which slows finishing.

**Latex** is roughly 60 to 70 percent water plus tiny plastic particles and pigment. Onboard heaters drive off the water and melt the latex into a film, so prints come out **fully dry and odorless**, ready to finish immediately. It is the [greenest common option](/blog/print-production-craft/13-sustainability-eco-printing), often certified safe for hospitals and schools with no special ventilation, and it prints on everything from plain paper to wallpaper to textiles. Downsides: the heat can wrinkle thin heat-sensitive films, and the hardware costs more.

**UV-cure** is a liquid ink hardened into solid plastic the instant UV lamps hit it. Because it sits **on top** of the surface instead of soaking in, it prints on almost anything: glass, metal, wood, acrylic, foamboard, even leather, rigid or flexible, often with no primer. It dries instantly and can lay down white ink, gloss varnish, and raised textures. The catch: cured UV ink is rigid, so on a deep stretched curve, like a vehicle-wrap recess, it can **crack**. Modern UV-LED lamps removed most of the old heat and ozone trouble.

**Dye-sublimation** uses heat to turn solid dye into a gas that bonds *into* polyester fibers. The color becomes part of the cloth, so the result is soft, vivid, washable, and impossible to crack or peel. This is how soft signage is made: tension-fabric booth walls, flags, and backdrops that fold into a small case.

(There is also older "true solvent," toughest outdoors at 5 to 7 years but smelly and being phased out, plus **aqueous** water-based inks for indoor fine-art and photo prints that need lamination to survive outside.)

| Factor | Eco-solvent | Latex | UV-cure | Dye-sub |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Sticks without primer on | Vinyl | Broad range | Almost anything | Polyester only |
| Outdoor life | 3 yr / 5+ laminated | 3-5 yr | Good (rigid) | Indoor / soft signage |
| Ready to finish | Needs dwell time | Immediate | Instant | After heat press |
| Odor / eco-safety | Some odor | Best (low-VOC) | Low (UV-LED) | Low |
| Stretch / wrap curves | Flexes well | Flexes well | Can crack on deep stretch | Very flexible (fabric) |
| Hardware / ink cost | Lowest | Higher | Higher | Specialized |

Pick ink by three questions: **what surface** (UV is widest), **how long outdoors** (solvent and cast-wrap last longest), and **how green or fast** (latex is cleanest, UV is the fastest dry). There is no single best ink, only best-for-this-job.

### Roll-to-roll, flatbed, or hybrid

The printer's body style decides whether you can print a stiff board directly or have to glue a print onto one.

**Roll-to-roll** unwinds a roll of flexible media, runs it under a printhead that sweeps side to side, then rewinds or cuts at the far end. The *media* moves; the head moves across it. Great for banners and anything that bends around rollers, with high throughput and a lower entry price. The downside: for a rigid sign you must print on adhesive vinyl and then **mount** it onto a board, adding labor, material, and the risk of bubbles.

**Flatbed** lays the material flat and still on a vacuum bed while the printhead travels over it. Almost always UV ink. It prints **directly** onto rigid sheets, including foamboard, acrylic, aluminum composite, wood, and glass, with no mounting step, and at higher resolution because nothing moves. Downsides: higher cost, and the bed size caps the sheet size.

**Hybrid** is one UV machine with both a flat bed and roll-feed attachments, so it handles rigid *and* roll media. More flexible, but usually not best-in-class at either. Popular when a shop cannot afford two machines.

### Substrates, the real materials

"Substrate" just means the stuff you print on. Wide-format has its own vocabulary worth knowing, because your product catalog will list these by name.

| Material | What it is | Typical use |
|---|---|---|
| 13 oz scrim vinyl | Polyester mesh in PVC; standard banner weight | General outdoor banners |
| 18 oz vinyl | Heavier, often double-sided | Long-term / blockout banners |
| Mesh banner (8-11 oz) | Open weave, ~30-37% airflow | Windy fence / building banners |
| Foamboard / Gatorboard | Foam between paper liners | Indoor short-term rigid signs |
| Coroplast | Fluted polypropylene | Yard / real-estate / political signs |
| ACM / Dibond | Aluminum skins over a plastic core | Durable outdoor rigid signs |
| Acrylic / PVC (Sintra) | Premium rigid panels | Lobby and retail signage |
| Canvas, backlit, perf film | Specialty media | Fine-art, lightboxes, window graphics |

**Cast versus calendered vinyl** earns its own callout, because getting it wrong is the classic vehicle-wrap disaster. Both are vinyl, but made differently:

| | Calendered | Cast |
|---|---|---|
| How it's made | Rolled and squeezed like dough | Poured as liquid, then cured |
| Thickness | ~2.5-4 mil (thicker) | ~2 mil (thin) |
| "Memory" | Wants to shrink back and lift | No shrink memory; conforms |
| Life | ~1-5 yr | 5-7 yr |
| Use | Flat / simple curves, cheaper | Full wraps, deep curves |

The **overlaminate** is a clear protective film applied over the print. The rule: it must be equal or higher grade than the base film, or it shortens the whole system's life. A cast print needs a cast laminate.

A real example: a full **vehicle wrap** is 2-mil cast vinyl printed eco-solvent or latex, covered with matching cast laminate, then heat-formed into the door rivets and recesses. It lasts 5 to 7 years and peels off cleanly. The same job in cheap calendered film lifts off the curves within weeks.

The big idea here: in wide-format, **ink plus substrate plus laminate are a system** rated to work together. Mix grades, like cheap vinyl under a wrong laminate, and the graphic fails early.

## Flexography: mass-producing packaging and labels

Flexo is a completely different beast: **high-speed rotary relief printing**. Unpacking that:

- **Relief** means the image is *raised* on the plate, like a rubber stamp. Only the raised parts touch the material and leave ink.
- **Rotary** means the plate is wrapped around a spinning cylinder, so it runs fast and continuously.
- **Web** means a continuous roll of material fed through the press, rather than separate sheets.

Flexo is **analog**: you make a physical plate for every design and every color. That upfront tooling cost is exactly why flexo only pays off on **long runs**. But at scale it is the cheapest, fastest way on earth to print packaging. It dominates snack bags, pouches, shrink sleeves, product labels, and cardboard boxes.

### How a flexo print unit works

Every color is its own station, and the web threads through them one after another. Inside one station, ink follows a precise path:

1. An **ink chamber** with a **doctor blade** (a thin metal scraper) feeds ink to the anilox.
2. The **anilox roller**, an engraved metal roller covered in millions of microscopic cells, each holding a precise, metered amount of ink. The doctor blade wipes off the excess so every pass lays an identical thin film. The anilox is the heart of ink control.
3. The anilox inks only the **raised areas** of the **plate**, mounted on the plate cylinder.
4. The **impression cylinder** presses the web against the plate, transferring the image.
5. Inline **drying** (hot air, infrared, or UV) sets the ink before the next color, then often inline finishing: die-cut, laminate, slit, and rewind, all in one pass.

Flexo inks are **low-viscosity**, meaning runny and fast-drying, in solvent-based, water-based (preferred for food and paper), or UV-curable forms.

### The two parts that decide quality

Two specs come up constantly when print pros talk flexo:

- **Anilox specs** are measured in **LPI** (cell lines per inch, roughly 200 to 1200-plus) and **cell volume in BCM** (billion cubic microns of ink per square inch). High line count plus low volume gives a fine, light laydown for delicate highlights. Low line count plus high volume gives a heavy laydown for solid color, white, and coatings. Shops keep separate aniloxes for solids versus screens.
- **Plates** are made of **photopolymer**, a light-hardening plastic. UV light shines through a digital mask; the unexposed areas wash out, leaving the raised relief. Plates are described by **durometer** (hardness) and thickness, and modern "flat-top dot" plates hold tiny highlight dots more reliably. A soft mounting tape under the plate cushions it and changes how much the dots spread.

### Substrates and applications

- **Flexible film** (BOPP, PE, PET, laminates) for snack bags, pouches, and shrink sleeves.
- **Paper and labelstock** for peel-and-stick labels, the number-one flexo market.
- **Corrugated and cartonboard** for shipping boxes and folding cartons.
- **Foil, metallized film, cellophane, kraft**, plus tags, tickets, and even wallpaper.

### Printing corrugated boxes

Cardboard is a special case, because its rough, fluted surface limits how fine the printing can be. Three methods trade quality against cost:

| Method | How | Quality / use |
|---|---|---|
| **Post-print flexo** | Print directly onto the finished box | Cheapest, lower detail; logos on shipping boxes |
| **Preprint flexo** | Print the liner roll before gluing to fluting | Better quality, very high volume |
| **Litho-lamination** | Offset-print a paper label, laminate onto corrugated | Highest (photographic), but high minimums |

So a plain kraft shipping box gets a one or two color post-print logo. A glossy retail display box on the store shelf is [litho-laminated](/blog/print-production-craft/03-offset-lithography-the-workhorse-of-commercial-print) for photo quality. Seasonal or on-demand boxes go digital.

### The run-length economics

This is the decision a quote engine must get right. Flexo has high setup cost but low per-unit cost. Digital has near-zero setup but higher per-unit cost. So they cross over at a **break-even** quantity.

| | Flexo (analog) | Digital label press |
|---|---|---|
| Setup / plates | ~$200-$400 per color; 30-60 min makeready | No plates; ~5-10 min changeover |
| Setup waste | Higher per changeover | Low short-run waste |
| Speed | High (~100-200 m/min) | Lower (~20-60 m/min) |
| Variable data | No | Yes: names, lot codes, versions |
| Sweet spot | One design at ~50k-500k+ | Many designs at ~1-5k each |

The practical break-even is roughly **2,000 to 5,000 labels per design**, shifting with color count, plate cost, and changeover time.

For example, a snack-bag **shrink sleeve** runs on an 8-station UV flexo press on BOPP film, with CMYK plus two spot colors plus a white backer plus matte varnish, reverse-printed and laminated, for hundreds of thousands of units at a tiny cost each. A craft startup ordering 800 labels across six flavors should [go digital](/blog/print-production-craft/04-digital-printing-toner-inkjet-variable-data) instead.

## Common misconceptions

**"One ink is simply the best."** No. The widest-sticking ink (UV) cracks on deep wrap curves. The longest-lasting solvent ink is being phased out for being smelly. Every ink is best only for a specific job.

**"Cast and calendered vinyl are basically the same."** They are not. Calendered film has shrink "memory" and lifts off curves within weeks. Cast film conforms and lasts years. Putting calendered film on a curved wrap is the classic disaster.

**"Flexo is just old-fashioned printing."** It is the most cost-effective high-volume packaging process on earth. Below a few thousand units it loses to digital, but above that, nothing touches its per-unit cost.

**"Digital can do everything flexo does, cheaper."** Only on short runs. Once you cross the [break-even quantity](/blog/business-financial-literacy/10-break-even-margins-profitability), flexo's amortized plate cost makes it dramatically cheaper per unit, and far faster.

**"Bigger prints just need bigger files."** A far-viewed billboard tolerates much lower resolution than a close-up checkout display. Resolution is about viewing distance, not raw size.

## How to use this

If you are speccing, quoting, or building software for these jobs, work through this in order:

1. **Classify the job first.** Big and unique, or small-unit and mass-produced? That single fork sets the whole path: wide-format versus flexo or digital.
2. **For signage, pick ink by surface, lifespan, and safety**, in that order. UV for odd rigid materials, eco-solvent or latex for vinyl and wraps, dye-sub for fabric.
3. **Match vinyl to the shape.** Flat or gentle curves can use cheaper calendered film. Deep curves and full wraps demand cast vinyl with a matching cast laminate.
4. **Treat ink, substrate, and laminate as one rated system.** Never pair a premium print with a budget laminate.
5. **For labels and packaging, run the break-even math.** Below roughly 2,000 to 5,000 units per design, or for any variable data, quote digital. Above it, quote flexo.
6. **Price by the right shape.** Wide-format is area-based: price per square foot times material times ink mode times finishing. Flexo is setup plus run length: a plate charge per color, a per-thousand price, and a minimum order.
7. **Preflight in plain words.** Tell customers "[add 0.25 inch of bleed](/blog/print-production-craft/07-color-files-prepress-cmyk-pantone-bleed-proofs-preflight)," not "trim box mismatch." Auto-check resolution, color space, fonts, bleed, and spot-color count.
8. **Bake the recommendation into the tool.** Let the software steer the customer to digital below break-even and flexo above it, so the store owner never needs to know any of this craft.

## Conclusion

Here is the one thing to carry away: wide-format and flexo sit at opposite ends of print. One is big-and-unique, priced by area, digital, no plates. The other is small-and-mass-produced, priced by setup, analog, plate-driven. Nail the **pricing shape**, the **material-to-profile match**, and the **plain-language preflight**, and you can quote either for someone who has never touched a press.

But notice the quiet third option that kept appearing in the middle: the **digital label press**, bridging the gap below flexo's break-even. That in-between zone, where short runs meet variable data and "print 50 versions of 1,000 each" suddenly makes sense, is where the most interesting economics in modern packaging are being rewritten. That is the next rabbit hole worth following.
