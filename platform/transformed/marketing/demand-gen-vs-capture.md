Right now, about 95 out of every 100 people who could buy from you are not looking. They are not searching, not comparing, not reading reviews. They are simply living their day, unaware that a better way exists.

Most marketing teams spend almost all their money fighting over the other 5%.

That single imbalance explains why so many good products stall. And it points to the most useful idea in modern marketing: there are two completely different ways to find customers, and you need both running at the same time.

## Why this matters

If you only chase people who are already shopping, you are competing for a tiny, crowded pool. Everyone with a credit card and an ad account is bidding for the same handful of high-intent searchers. Prices go up, margins go down, and the moment you stop paying, the customers stop coming.

The bigger, quieter opportunity is the 95% who haven't realized they have a problem yet, or don't know a solution like yours exists. Reach them early, and you become the name they think of first when they finally do start looking. That's a moat competitors can't outbid.

Understanding the difference between these two motions, and how to split your effort between them, is the difference between renting attention and owning it.

## The two engines, explained plainly

There are two ways to grow demand, and they work in opposite directions in time.

**Demand capture** means catching people who already know they have a problem and are actively looking for a fix. They are "in-market." Your job is to be found and to win the comparison. Think of someone Googling "best accounting software for freelancers" with their wallet half out. They've decided to buy; the only question is from whom.

**Demand generation** means creating awareness, education, and preference *before* someone is shopping, so that when they eventually go looking, your name is the first one that surfaces. You aren't catching existing demand. You're creating it.

A clean way to remember the split, popularized by marketer Chris Walker: build preference before buyers are in-market, capture high-intent demand efficiently, then expand the relationship after the sale.

### The Tesla analogy

Tesla spent roughly a decade *creating* demand for electric cars. Millions of people who had zero interest in an EV ten years ago now want one. None of that came from a search ad. It came from years of stories, test drives, opinion, and word-of-mouth that slowly rewired what people wanted. That is demand generation.

A Google search for "Tesla Model 3 price" is demand capture. The wanting already happened. The search is just the final step.

### The 95-5 rule, the one number to remember

Here's the research that makes all of this concrete. Work from Professor John Dawes at the **Ehrenberg-Bass Institute**, amplified by the LinkedIn B2B Institute, found that at any given moment only about **5% of business buyers are in-market**. The other **95% are out-of-market**.

The logic is simple. Imagine a business that reviews a given purchase once every five years. In any single year, only about 20% are even considering a change. In any single quarter, it's closer to 5%.

So pure capture tactics, no matter how good, can only ever reach that 5% slice at once. If you ignore the 95%, you've quietly capped your own ceiling. The winning move is to raise the odds that *your* name is the one that comes to mind when someone finally decides it's time.

## A real example: software for local print shops

Abstract ideas stick better with a story, so here's one.

Imagine a startup selling simple "web-to-print" software, an online storefront that lets a local print shop take orders, design products, and price jobs online without hiring a developer. The buyer is a non-technical shop owner who runs everything by phone, email, and walk-in.

That owner is the textbook out-of-market customer. Most have never typed "web-to-print software" into Google, for two reasons:

- They don't know the category exists.
- They assume "selling online" means hiring a developer for a small fortune.

If this startup builds only a capture machine, it ends up fighting big incumbents over the tiny pool of already-convinced searchers. The far larger, less-contested prize is *creating the realization*: "You can run your own online print store yourself, this week, no developer needed."

That reframe turns a non-problem into an urgent one. And that is demand generation doing its job. Notice the segments hiding inside one market:

- **The frustrated DIYer** is in-market. They already use a clunky tool and are searching for alternatives. **Capture them.**
- **The "we need a website" shop** is on the edge. They're Googling vaguely but don't know the category name. **Capture and generate.**
- **The "online is for big companies" shop** is fully out-of-market. They believe selling online is an expensive developer project. **Generate.**
- **The growth-curious owner** watches competitors nervously but hasn't connected that worry to a software fix. **Generate.**

Same market, four very different people, two very different jobs.

## How to build a capture engine

Capture is the fast, high-return engine. Because it targets people already evaluating options, well-built bottom-of-funnel pages can show results in 30 to 60 days. Here's the priority order most teams should follow.

1. **Comparison and "alternative" pages.** The single highest-leverage capture asset. Honest pages like "Us vs. Competitor X" or "A simpler alternative to Y for small businesses." People search these with their wallet out, ready to choose.
2. **Bottom-of-funnel keyword pages.** Target your solution plus buying intent: "online ordering system for X," "best tool for Y." Give each a dedicated, conversion-focused page with a demo button, screenshots, and a clear "go live fast" promise.
3. **A pricing page that ranks.** Tons of people search "[product] pricing." A clear, mostly public pricing page captures those searches and pre-qualifies buyers before they ever talk to you.
4. **Paid search on the same terms.** A small, tightly-scoped budget on only your 10 to 20 highest-intent keywords. Resist the urge to broaden. In-market volume is thin; spend only where intent is unmistakable.
5. **Directories and review sites.** Get listed where buyers validate choices (G2, Capterra, and the like) and gather real reviews. These pages rank for your category and carry trust you can't write yourself.
6. **Retargeting.** Quietly follow up with people who visited your demo page but didn't convert. Cheap, and they've already shown intent.

## How to build a generation engine

This engine is harder and slower, but far more durable. For a focused, vertical business, demand creation runs on three things, not search ads: **founder credibility, community, and education.** The audience isn't searching, so you have to go to where they already are.

It also spreads through what's called **dark social**, the word-of-mouth, group shares, and DMs that never show up in your analytics. You won't see it in a dashboard, but it's often the real engine.

Here's what it looks like in practice:

- **Show up in the communities your buyers already trust.** Industry Facebook groups, forums, subreddits, and Slack channels where they troubleshoot daily problems. The rule is simple: help genuinely, don't pitch. Answer real questions for weeks before you ever mention your product. This is where preference is built.
- **Attend the events and associations they care about.** Trade shows, regional meetups, industry groups. A single sponsored local gathering often beats a pile of broad ads, because you're in the room with the exact people you want.
- **Publish a clear point of view.** A named, human face, often the founder, posting short, opinionated, plain-language content. Things like "How a two-person shop took its first online order in a weekend" or "You don't need a developer to sell online." Teach the realization; don't sell the feature.
- **Show, don't tell.** Sixty-to-ninety-second screen recordings that prove it works: "Watch me build this in four minutes." Non-technical buyers believe what they can see working.
- **Do founder-led outreach.** Early on, the founder personally calling, visiting, and *listening* is itself demand generation. Every conversation plants the idea and produces raw material for content.

The message that creates demand is never your category jargon. It's the customer's own words, reframing something they'd accepted as normal into a problem worth solving now.

## How to split your effort

A famous benchmark, the 60/40 brand-to-performance split, gets quoted constantly. But it was derived from large, established companies. For an early-stage business still proving that people will pay, copying it is a trap.

The stage-appropriate guidance is different. Early on, weight heavily toward capture, because capture gives you the fastest proof that the business actually works.

A practical split for an early-stage company over the next year or so:

- **Capture: about 60-70%.** Fast proof, lowest cost per customer, and it validates your messaging and ideal customer. Build the comparison pages, bottom-of-funnel pages, and directory presence first.
- **Generation: about 25-30%.** Crucially, this is mostly *founder time, not cash.* Community participation, point-of-view content, demos. It compounds slowly, so start now precisely because it's slow to mature.
- **Tooling and experiments: about 5-10%.** Analytics, a simple CRM, a "How did you hear about us?" field, and small test budgets.

As you saturate the thin 5% pool, gradually shift the mix toward generation. The early years buy proof; the later years buy a moat.

## Common misconceptions

**"If it's not in the dashboard, it isn't working."** Wrong, and expensive. Analytics credit the last click, so they're heavily biased toward capture. They're blind to the community comment or founder post that actually created the demand. Never kill a generation program because the dashboard shows zero conversions. By design, it won't show them.

**"Brand benchmarks like 60/40 apply to everyone."** They were built for big companies that already have proven economics. At the early stage, leaning that hard into awareness burns your runway before you've earned the right to spend it.

**"Generation should pay off this quarter."** It compounds over quarters and years. Tesla's took the better part of a decade. The whole point of starting now is that it's slow.

**"I can pitch in communities if I'm clever about it."** You can't. Communities eject sellers fast. Help first, for months, before anyone clicks a link.

**"More keywords, more growth."** In-market volume is genuinely thin. Broadening paid search past unmistakable intent just spends money on people who weren't going to buy.

## How to use this, starting this week

Here's a concrete order of operations. None of it requires a big budget.

1. **Add a "How did you hear about us?" field** to every demo request and sign-up today. One hour of work, and it's your single best read on whether generation is working. Self-reported attribution beats your dashboard.
2. **Ship three comparison pages.** Versus your main competitor, an "alternative for small businesses" page, and a "why the obvious generic option falls short" page. Fast return.
3. **Claim and fill out your review-site profiles** and ask your first happy customers for reviews. Aim for a healthy handful before chasing anything paid.
4. **Join five to eight communities** where your buyers gather, and spend two weeks just helping before you post anything about your product.
5. **Record one 90-second demo** showing your product doing the thing, fast. Post it natively in those communities and on LinkedIn.
6. **Publish one point-of-view post per week** in plain language. Consistency matters more than virality. Generation is a drumbeat, not a one-off campaign.
7. **Set up Google Search Console** and log your branded search volume weekly. When people start Googling your name directly, generation is working. This is your north-star leading indicator.
8. **Run one tiny paid-search test** on only your highest-intent keywords, and measure cost per demo. Keep it small and ruthless.
9. **Pick one industry event** to attend, not exhibit, and talk to twenty potential customers. You'll walk away with relationships, research, and a month of content ideas.

## Conclusion

The one idea to keep: **capture harvests demand that already exists; generation creates demand that doesn't yet.** Most teams over-invest in the harvest and wonder why growth feels like a treadmill. The companies that compound do the slow, unglamorous work of planting in the 95% long before anyone is searching.

So here's the question worth sitting with. If only 5% of your market is shopping today, what are you doing to make sure the other 95% already know your name when their moment finally arrives? Start with the one-hour "How did you hear about us?" field, and within a few months you may be surprised which channel is quietly driving your best customers, and how invisible it was to your analytics the whole time.
