**This document is a step-by-step plan for getting new customers through Google search and free content — without paying for ads. It matters because Print-Flow-360 has no sales team, so the website itself has to do the selling. Content and SEO (search engine optimisation — getting found on Google) get cheaper over time, unlike ads that stop working the moment you stop paying.**

**The main parts explained simply:**

- **Finding the right search words** — Not all searches are equal. People who Google "OnPrintShop alternative" are ready to buy now; people who Google "how to start a print business" are just exploring. The plan targets both groups with different pages, and avoids fighting big competitors on their strongest searches.

- **Making Google trust your site (on-page basics)** — Each page needs a clear title, a plain description, one main heading, and real helpful content. The plan covers what to write and how to lay it out so Google (and AI tools like ChatGPT) can understand and recommend your pages.

- **Technical health** — Speed, mobile friendliness, and making sure Google can actually read your pages. There is a known bug where the storefront shows a blank page to search-engine crawlers — fixing that is the first priority because if Google can't read a page, that page can't rank.

- **Comparison and "alternative" pages** — Pages like "Print-Flow-360 vs OnPrintShop" or "OnPrintShop alternative" attract visitors who are already looking for software and ready to sign up. These are the highest-converting pages to build first.

- **Free tools that double as demos** — A public pricing calculator, a "is my file print-ready?" checker, and a free template gallery. These use engines already built into the product. People share and link to useful tools, which helps Google rank the site higher — and visitors who use the tool are already halfway to signing up.

- **Video** — Screen recordings of the design studio and store builder, posted on YouTube and embedded on the website. Video removes the biggest worry non-technical buyers have ("will I be able to figure this out?") and YouTube is its own search engine.

- **Content plan for a small team** — About two solid articles a week, each turned into social posts, emails, and short videos. Quality and depth beat publishing lots of thin articles.

- **What to avoid** — Buying links, mass-generating thin pages, publishing unedited AI text, or trying to rank for the most competitive searches right away. All of these can get the site penalised by Google.

**What to do with this:** Fix the SSR tenant-host bug so Google can crawl storefronts, then publish two or three competitor-comparison pages, and ship the public pricing calculator. Those three moves give the fastest return before anything else.
