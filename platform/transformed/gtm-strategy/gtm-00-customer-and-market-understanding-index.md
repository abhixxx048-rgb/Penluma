A brilliant marketing funnel pointed at the wrong customer just acquires the wrong people faster. A flawless sales pitch aimed at a market that isn't there just burns money more efficiently.

Most early-stage failures aren't execution problems. They're understanding problems. And the cure is research - but only if you do it in the right order, at the right moment, instead of drowning in a backlog of "things we should probably figure out."

## Why this matters

Before product-market fit, every lever you can pull is downstream of one thing: **how well you understand the customer and the market.**

Positioning, channels, pricing, packaging, what to build next, the story you tell investors - all of it rests on that foundation. Get the customer wrong and even a great product struggles. Get the market sizing wrong and you build for demand that doesn't exist.

Here's the trap most founders fall into. They treat customer research as a giant to-do list and try to grind through all of it. But your scarcest resource isn't time or money - it's **founder attention**. The point of a research plan isn't to do everything. It's to read the right thing at the right decision, so each hour of research actually changes a decision you're making this quarter.

This guide is a map. It walks through six research practices, what each one answers, and when to reach for it. Think of it as the table of contents for understanding your market without losing your mind.

## Meet your buyer: a quick example to anchor everything

It's easier to talk about research with a real person in mind, so let's borrow one.

Imagine **Maria, who owns a local print shop**. She runs a small business - somewhere between a handful and thirty employees. She's not technical. Her "system" is email, a few spreadsheets, and a generic website she set up herself.

Maria is a **one-person buying committee**. She won't convene a team to evaluate you. She won't tolerate clumsy onboarding or a wall of jargon. If your product confuses her in the first ten minutes, she's gone.

Two things follow from this, and they shape every research decision:

- **Misreading her job is the fastest way to lose her.** You have to know precisely what she's trying to get done, in her words, not yours.
- **You can't pretend your product gaps away.** If your software is missing something she needs, research has to surface how often that gap actually kills a deal - so you can price and position around it honestly.

Keep Maria in your head as we walk through the six practices. Each one is a different way of seeing her more clearly.

## The six research practices, in reading order

These aren't six chapters to read once. They're six lenses, and the order matters because each builds on the last.

### 1. Ideal customer profile and positioning - the foundation

This is where you define exactly **who you serve** and **what category you belong to**.

Who is the beachhead customer? (For Maria, it's small, non-technical local print shops of a certain size.) What persona are you really talking to? And what's your one-sentence category claim - the "we are an X, not a Y" that tells people how to think about you?

**Read this first, and re-read it every time you're tempted to chase a bigger or more technical buyer.** Everything else assumes this profile. If it shifts, the rest of your research needs re-grounding.

### 2. Jobs to be done - why she'd switch

A customer doesn't buy a product. They "hire" it to do a job, and they "fire" whatever they were using before.

This practice captures the real jobs Maria is hiring software to do - functional ("get orders online without hassle"), emotional ("stop feeling behind"), and social ("look professional to my customers"). It also names what she fires: the spreadsheets, the email chaos, the do-it-yourself website.

**Use this when you write copy, decide which feature to lead with, or design your first onboarding steps.** [Jobs-to-be-done](/blog/product-sense-empathy/04-jobs-to-be-done-people-hire-products-to-make-progress) is the raw material that turns vague positioning into something concrete. If your positioning feels mushy, it's almost always because this layer is thin.

### 3. Win/loss and churn interviews - the truth source

This is the reality check on everything above. You interview the deals you **won**, the ones you **lost**, and the customers who **churned**, then code the reasons.

**Start the moment you have any closed deals or cancellations - even two or three.** Before product-market fit, this is your single highest-signal feedback source. It tells you, faster than anything else:

- Whether your customer profile is actually right
- Which product gaps are deal-killers versus mere nice-to-haves
- Which competitor keeps showing up in the conversation

Think of it as the corrective layer. It can confirm or quietly demolish your assumptions, which is exactly why it's so valuable.

### 4. Pricing research - find willingness to pay

This is not about pulling a number out of the air. It's about learning what customers will actually pay and how to package it.

The methods here suit a tiny sample - simple willingness-to-pay probes inside sales calls, structured pricing questions, and choosing the right **value metric** (the thing you charge against, like per store or per order).

**Use it when you design or revise your tiers.** A good rule: get five to ten honest pricing conversations before you lock anything in. Pricing learned from real value beats pricing guessed in a vacuum.

### 5. Competitive intelligence - what she actually considers

Here's the subtle part. Maria's real alternatives aren't just the named competitors in your space. They include the generic website she already has, and the option to **do nothing at all**.

This practice is a standing habit of tracking those true alternatives - including the status quo. **Use it when you sharpen positioning, handle objections, and decide where your product needs to reach parity versus where it can genuinely win.**

It feeds directly off your win/loss work (every loss names a competitor) and loops back into your positioning.

### 6. Market sizing: TAM, SAM, and SOM - the big-picture layer

This is the market-size layer, and it's the one founders most often get wrong by doing it first. Build it **last**, from the bottom up, so it survives scrutiny.

Three numbers, in plain language:

- **TAM (Total Addressable Market):** everyone who could theoretically be a customer for your category.
- **SAM (Serviceable Addressable Market):** the slice you can actually serve given your product and constraints - for example, only the regions where your payment system works, and only the customer type you've defined.
- **SOM (Serviceable Obtainable Market):** the realistic share you can capture given your actual resources and [founder-led approach](/blog/sales-customer-development/15-building-the-muscle-process-pipeline-habit).

**Use TAM/SAM/SOM for your investor story and for grounding annual planning targets.** But it only means something once your customer profile and pricing are credible - which is why it comes at the end.

## How they connect

The biggest mindset shift: this is a set of **feedback loops, not a linear funnel**.

- **Jobs to be done feeds positioning.** The jobs and the "what gets fired" become the substance of your category claim.
- **Win/loss feeds everything.** It validates or breaks your customer profile, names the competitors to track, and surfaces which product gaps are really killing deals - which then drives what you build next.
- **Pricing research shapes packaging.** Willingness-to-pay and value-metric findings drive your tiers. But you can't set price without understanding value (jobs) and competitive anchors.
- **Competitive intelligence and positioning loop into each other.** Knowing where to claim difference versus reach parity sharpens both your messaging and your [objection-handling](/blog/sales-customer-development/11-handling-objections-with-honesty).
- **Market sizing rolls it all up.** The top-line number is the fundraising narrative; the obtainable number sets realistic targets - and it's only as credible as the customer profile and pricing feeding it.

In one breath: your customer profile **anchors**, jobs-to-be-done **supplies the substance**, win/loss is the **truth source that corrects the rest**, pricing and competitive work **shape your offer**, and market sizing **rolls it up** for investors and planning.

## Common misconceptions

**"Research means surveys and big reports."**
Not at this stage. Your highest-signal research is a founder having [direct conversations](/blog/sales-customer-development/04-the-mom-test-talk-to-customers-without-fooling-yourself) - win/loss interviews and jobs-to-be-done chats. A few honest conversations beat a hundred survey responses.

**"Market sizing should come first because it tells me if the market is big enough."**
It feels logical, but a TAM/SAM/SOM built before you understand your customer and pricing is just a spreadsheet of guesses. Build it last, when the inputs are real.

**"Competitors are only the other companies in my space."**
Your fiercest competitor is usually the status quo - the spreadsheet, the existing website, or doing nothing. If you only track named vendors, you'll keep losing to a "competitor" that doesn't have a logo.

**"Once I've done the research, I'm done."**
These are ongoing practices, not one-time projects. Customer conversations and win/loss never stop. Only market sizing is mostly one-and-done.

## How to use this: an operating cadence

Different practices run on different clocks. At an early stage, the honest answer to "who owns this?" is: **the founder owns all of it.** So the real question is how to spend your attention budget.

- **Continuous (the heartbeat):**
  1. Talk to customers and prospects, jobs-to-be-done style - several conversations a week. Never delegate or pause this.
  2. Run a win/loss or churn interview within days of every close, loss, or cancellation.
  3. Log competitors as they come up in calls.

- **Quarterly (scheduled stops):**
  4. Refresh competitive intelligence with a full sweep.
  5. Re-run pricing and willingness-to-pay research, or do it sooner before any packaging change.
  6. Review your customer profile and positioning against the quarter's signal.

- **One-time (rebuild only on big changes):**
  7. Build the TAM/SAM/SOM model once, and revise it only when your customer, your pricing, or your reachable market materially shifts.

### If you only do three things this quarter

When attention is your binding constraint, here's the short list:

1. **Interview every win, loss, and churn - no exceptions.** Highest signal, lowest cost. It tells you faster than anything whether your customer profile holds and which gap is the real deal-killer. Do this before building any new feature.
2. **Pin down the one job your customer is really hiring you for, and rewrite your positioning to match.** Until your message reflects how customers describe their own pain, your funnel will under-convert.
3. **Validate your pricing against real [willingness to pay](/blog/business-financial-literacy/08-pricing-fundamentals-cost-plus-vs-value-vs-competitive).** Get five to ten honest conversations before locking tiers, so packaging is grounded in value rather than guesswork.

Defer the full market-sizing rebuild and the deep competitive sweep to next quarter - unless fundraising forces the market numbers sooner.

## Conclusion

If you remember one thing, make it this: **win/loss and churn interviews are the truth source that keeps every other research effort honest.** Everything else can drift into wishful thinking. Talking to the deals you won and lost is the cheapest reality check you'll ever run.

Start there, anchor on a sharp customer profile, and build market sizing only once the foundation is solid. The order isn't bureaucracy - it's what keeps you from building a beautiful answer to the wrong question.

And here's the thread worth pulling next: that "one job" your customer is hiring you for is rarely the one written on your feature list. The gap between what you think you sell and what they're actually buying is where most positioning breakthroughs hide.
