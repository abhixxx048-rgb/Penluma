A print shop owner decides it's time to sell online. What do they do first?

They don't visit your website. They open Google and type "best web-to-print software." They land on a comparison listicle or a Capterra category page, skim the star ratings, and build a shortlist in about ninety seconds. By the time they ever see your homepage, the decision is half made.

That ninety seconds is where you win or lose. And most of it happens on pages you don't own.

## Why this matters

Your buyer is often non-technical. They don't read your developer docs, and they instinctively distrust a vendor's own marketing. So they outsource trust to third parties: software review sites, industry directories, and "best of" roundup posts.

If you're absent from those pages, you're not just lower on the list. You're invisible at the exact moment of consideration. The buyer eliminates you before the demo, and you never even know you were in the running.

The encouraging part: getting listed is **free almost everywhere**, the ranking systems reward reviews you can generate yourself, and the whole landscape recently got simpler. One focused effort now covers most of the discovery surface.

## The three surfaces where buyers actually look

Not all listings are equal. There are three distinct types of pages, and they attract buyers at different moments.

### 1. B2B software review sites

These are "Yelp for software": category pages, star ratings, badges, and side-by-side comparisons. Think **G2, Capterra, GetApp, Software Advice, TrustRadius, GoodFirms, and SourceForge**.

Buyer intent here is **high**. People on these pages are actively comparing tools and ready to pick.

A big shift happened in early 2026: **G2 acquired Capterra, GetApp, and Software Advice from Gartner.** Those three already shared one backend, meaning a single review you collect appears on all three. Now the entire dominant discovery layer sits under one roof. In practice you manage two relationships instead of five: the Capterra cluster and G2.

### 2. App marketplaces

These are stores *inside* another platform: the **Shopify App Store, the WordPress and WooCommerce plugin directories, Wix, BigCommerce**.

Buyer intent is **medium**. These shoppers have already committed to a host platform and want to extend it. That's a narrower, often more technical audience than someone just starting out.

### 3. Industry directories and listicles

These are trade-specific: an industry association's supplier directory, or a blogger's "7 best tools for X" post.

Buyer intent is **mixed**, but the audience is hyper-relevant. A roundup post titled with your exact category often ranks #1 in Google for the precise phrase your buyer searches. Getting written into it is some of the highest-leverage SEO you'll ever do, and it doesn't live on your site at all.

## How rankings and badges really work

This is the part most vendors get wrong, so it's worth slowing down.

**On G2**, your position on the famous "Grid" comes from a simple formula: satisfaction and market presence, averaged together. Satisfaction is driven by review volume, recency, and ratings. Presence is driven by company size, web mentions, and social following. Badges like "Leader" and "High Performer" fall out of where you land.

One catch changed in 2025: you now need a **paid plan (starting around $3,000 a year)** to publicly display G2's premium badges. But the entry-level **"Users Love Us"** badge, earned at 10 reviews, is still free.

**On Capterra and its sister sites**, listing is free, responding to reviews is free, and earning and displaying badges is free. Their paid layer is a **pay-per-click ad auction** with a roughly $2-per-click minimum and a budget floor around $500 a month. Sponsored profiles rank above free ones.

Here's the principle that ties it all together:

> Paid money buys **placement**, never **ratings**. You cannot buy a better score. You can only buy more visibility for the score you've already earned.

That's why, early on, your money is better spent generating reviews than renting a top slot. An organic listing with 20 real reviews at 4.7 stars will out-convert a sponsored slot with one review and a 3.8.

## The legal lines you must not cross

Review generation is powerful, which is exactly why it's regulated. The **FTC's Final Rule on fake reviews took effect October 21, 2024**, with civil penalties exceeding **$50,000 per violation**. Major review platforms mirror these rules in their own policies.

The rule bans three things:

- **Fake or fabricated reviews**, including AI-generated ones or reviews attributed to people who didn't write them.
- **Incentivizing a specific sentiment.** You cannot offer anything in exchange for a positive (or negative) review, whether you say it outright or merely imply it.
- **Review gating.** You cannot selectively ask only happy customers while quietly suppressing the unhappy ones.

For a small company, a single FTC complaint can be existential. Build the program clean from day one.

### Common misconceptions

**"I can ask my best customers for a 5-star review."** No. You can ask customers for *a* review. The moment you specify the rating, you've crossed the line.

**"Offering a gift card for reviews is illegal."** Not quite. You can reward the *act* of reviewing, as long as the same reward goes to everyone regardless of what they write, and you disclose it. A $25 to $50 gift card is the documented B2B sweet spot.

**"Routing unhappy customers to a private feedback form is just good UX."** It used to be a common SaaS tactic. It's now explicitly illegal. Don't use any tool that does it.

**"Paying for a badge makes me look more credible."** At an early stage, a profile full of real reviews builds far more trust than a rented badge. Buyers can smell the difference.

## Build vs. integrate: the standalone question

If you sell something that *is* a platform, you'll eventually face a fork: build an app inside a bigger marketplace, or stay standalone?

It's tempting to chase the marketplace. The Shopify App Store, for example, has generous terms (you keep 100% of your first $1M a year, then 85%) and a built-in audience of millions.

But marketplaces come with a hidden assumption: **the customer already lives on that platform.** A Shopify app only helps people who already run a Shopify store. If your whole pitch is "the simplest way to get online *without* hiring a developer," you'd be selling a bolt-on to people who don't yet have the thing you bolt onto.

There's a deeper risk too. If your product *is* the storefront, wrapping it as someone else's app means handing your core differentiator to the host platform and shrinking yourself into an add-on.

A useful way to think about it: some competitors are **built for reach** (dozens of integrations, present everywhere) and some are **built for depth** (one complete, all-in-one platform). Those are different strategies. If you're a depth player, don't dilute that by scattering yourself thinly across marketplaces.

**The pragmatic path:** stay standalone as your primary motion. Treat marketplace listings as a *later* lead-generation channel, a lightweight connector that earns you a free store listing for discovery and SEO, while your real product stays standalone. Build your reputation first. Integrations are channel number four, not number one.

## How to use this: a prioritized rollout

Don't try to be everywhere at once. Work in tiers.

### Tier 1 — this month (free, high intent)

1. **Claim your Capterra cluster profile.** One claim covers Capterra, GetApp, and Software Advice. Pick the most *specific* category that fits you, not a generic catch-all where you'll drown next to giants. Spend a full day making it great: real screenshots, a product demo, a plain-language feature list, and clear pricing.
2. **Claim your free G2 profile.** Complete it fully and aim for the free "Users Love Us" badge at 10 reviews. Skip the paid badge plan for now.
3. **Add free listings on GoodFirms and SourceForge.** Both have real category pages that rank in Google. Roughly two hours each.

### Tier 2 — next 60 days (industry and editorial)

4. **Join the relevant industry directory.** Trade association directories carry a credibility halo that generic sites can't match. Membership usually costs something, but the co-marketing and trust are often worth more than the listing alone.
5. **Get written into the "best of" listicles.** Find the roundup posts that rank for your exact buyer query. Email each author, offer a free account and a crisp two-line positioning blurb. Prioritize neutral or affiliate posts; skip the ones owned by competitors.

### Tier 3 — later (paid and integration plays)

6. **Add a plugin or marketplace listing** for the platforms your customers already use, starting with the most natural fit. Free listings here are SEO-valuable even if they're not your main motion.
7. **Only then consider paid ads or premium badges**, and only once you can prove that demos turn into closed deals. Until that's proven, paid placement just burns cash.

## The review engine that powers all of it

Reviews are the flywheel: more reviews lift your organic rank, which brings more leads, which produces more reviews. Run it like a repeatable process, not a one-off favor.

1. **Trigger on a real success moment, then ask everyone who hits it.** Send the request after a genuine win (the customer goes live, places a first order, hits the 30-day mark) but send it to *every* customer who reaches that point, not a hand-picked few.
2. **Offer the same small thank-you to all** for leaving a review of any kind. Say it plainly. Never tie it to a rating.
3. **Make it effortless.** Don't tell a non-technical customer to "go authenticate on Capterra." Send a direct deep link to the review form, walk through the two-minute steps with a screenshot, and offer to hop on a call and do it together.
4. **Seed your primary site first.** Reviews on the Capterra cluster automatically fan out to its sister sites, so that's the best return per ask.
5. **Respond to every review, especially the critical ones.** A calm, gracious public reply is both a ranking signal and free reputation insurance. Silence reads as guilt.
6. **Keep a steady cadence.** Aim for two to four fresh reviews a month. Because recency is weighted, a steady drip beats a single big push and keeps your momentum alive.

A handful of numbers worth watching: total reviews per site, how many landed in the last 90 days, your average star rating, your organic category rank, and how much referral traffic each directory actually sends you. That last one tells you which surface is really converting, so you can double down on it.

## Conclusion

The single idea to hold onto: **your buyers form their shortlist on pages you don't own, before they ever meet you.** Directories aren't vanity. They're the buyer's shortlist, and being absent means being eliminated in silence.

So the winning move isn't to spend big. It's to show up where buyers look, earn honest reviews on a clean and steady cadence, and let your reputation compound. Reviews are the one asset competitors can't buy out from under you.

Once that engine is humming, a sharper question surfaces: what makes a buyer who's comparing five near-identical tools pick *you* in particular? That's the work of positioning, the two-line hook that makes a stranger think "that's the one for me" in a single glance. Get the listings right first, then go win that line.
