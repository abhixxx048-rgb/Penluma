Kodak's own engineers invented the digital camera. Internal forecasts showed film was doomed. The company had the information, the talent, and the patents. It went bankrupt anyway.

The reason wasn't ignorance. It was a single belief running quietly underneath everything: *we are a film company.* That one assumption decided what counted as a threat and what counted as a distraction. This article is about that hidden layer of belief, because it turns out to be the most powerful place to change anything.

## Why this matters

The things that feel most concrete in any system — budgets, prices, rules, org charts — usually move it the least. The thing that feels most intangible, what people quietly assume to be true, usually moves it the most.

That's a strange and useful idea. It means the lever you're reaching for is probably the weak one. When a problem keeps coming back no matter how many times you fix it, you're almost certainly working at the wrong depth.

Learn to find the deeper layer and you stop fighting the same fire forever. You start changing the thing that produces the fire.

## What a mental model actually is

A **mental model** is an internal, simplified picture of how some part of the world works. It's the set of assumptions you use to make sense of what you see and to decide what to do.

You carry thousands of them: how a meeting should run, what a good employee looks like, whether strangers can be trusted, why the printer jams. The systems thinker Donella Meadows put it this way — mental models are "the images, assumptions, and stories which we carry in our minds of ourselves, other people, institutions, and every aspect of the world."

Here's the crucial part. Every human action is based on a mental model. You literally cannot act without one. So the problem is never that you *have* models. The problem is that every model is incomplete, often wrong, and we forget it's a model at all. We mistake the map for the territory and stop questioning it.

Think of mental models as prescription glasses. They make the world legible, but they also distort everything you see — and you never notice the distortion, because you're always looking *through* the lenses, never *at* them. Surfacing a model means taking the glasses off and studying the frame. For a moment the world goes blurry. That discomfort isn't a sign you're wrong. It's the sign you're finally seeing the frame.

## The iceberg: four levels of seeing

Most of an iceberg hides below the waterline, and so does most of any problem. Systems thinkers use the iceberg to show four levels, from what's obvious to what's hidden:

- **Events** — what just happened. A product recall, a missed deadline, a traffic jam.
- **Patterns** — what keeps happening. Recalls getting more frequent. Deadlines slipping again and again.
- **Structures** — the rules, incentives, and feedback loops that produce those patterns. Cost-cutting that quietly thins out quality checks. Scheduling norms that always lowball the work.
- **Mental models** — the beliefs that built and maintain those structures. "Cost is the only variable we control." "A schedule is a negotiation, not a commitment."

Most organisations fight fires at the top level, because the top is all they can see. They fire the manager after the recall and patch the hole after the breach. But if the structure and the underlying belief are untouched, the same root cause just produces a fresh event in a new disguise.

**The trap here is treating every event as unique.** Because events are dramatic, teams give each one its own post-mortem and its own fix, instead of seeing them as symptoms of one structure and one belief. That's how you end up with a permanent fire-fighting culture that never gets less busy.

## Paradigms: the belief a whole group breathes

A **paradigm** is a mental model shared by an entire society, organisation, or field. It's the set of unstated beliefs held so widely that they're *presumed true rather than believed.* Meadows described it as "the mindset out of which the system — its goals, power structure, rules, its culture — arises."

Some examples she named: "Nature is a stock of resources to be converted to human use." "One can own land." "Growth is always good." Notice these don't feel like opinions. They feel like facts. That's exactly what makes a paradigm so powerful and so invisible.

A paradigm is the water a fish swims in. The fish doesn't think *about* water; it thinks *with* water. The paradigm shapes which questions even get asked and which solutions seem possible. That's why paradigm shifts so often arrive from outsiders. Tesla wasn't a car company. Netflix wasn't a video store. An outsider isn't breathing the same water, so they can see it.

## Why the paradigm is near the top of the lever list

A **leverage point** is a place in a system where a small push produces a large change. Meadows ranked twelve of them, from weakest to strongest, and the ranking surprises almost everyone — because the things that feel most important have the least leverage.

| Rank | Leverage point | How it feels |
|---|---|---|
| 12 (weakest) | Numbers — subsidies, prices, taxes | Concrete, urgent, fought over |
| 5 | Rules of the system | Powerful |
| 4 | Power to self-organise | More powerful |
| 3 | Goals of the system | Deep |
| 2 | Paradigm — the mindset itself | Almost invisible |
| 1 (strongest) | Transcending paradigms | Intangible |

The numbers everyone argues about endlessly — budgets, prices, taxes — sit at the bottom. The paradigm sits at number two. The mindset from which the whole system arises is second only to one thing.

### The very top: holding your paradigm lightly

Meadows' number one leverage point is the ability to hold *any* paradigm lightly — to know that every model is partial and none is the final truth. She wrote that "mastery has less to do with pushing leverage points than it does with strategically, profoundly, madly letting go."

This is not the idea that all views are equally valid. It's **epistemic humility**: keeping your current best model firmly enough to act on it, while staying genuinely ready to revise it when the evidence demands.

The two traps to avoid are rigid certainty ("my model is reality") and paralysed scepticism ("no model is trustworthy, so I can't act"). A working scientist uses a paradigm every single day and also knows it's provisional. That's the balance.

## How mental models defend themselves

If models are so often wrong, why don't we just notice and update them? Because they protect themselves. Two ideas explain how.

### The ladder of inference

Chris Argyris built the **ladder of inference** to show how a belief becomes self-sealing. We climb it fast: from all the available data we *select* a slice, add meaning, make assumptions, draw conclusions, adopt beliefs, and act.

The sting is in the loop at the bottom. Our beliefs decide which data we notice in the first place. So the model grows *stronger* even when the world contradicts it, because the contradicting evidence never makes it onto the ladder.

Experts are especially exposed. Their fast, accurate pattern-matching inside their field becomes fast, *inaccurate* pattern-matching outside it — and the speed hides the intermediate steps even from themselves. This is close cousin to the **curse of knowledge**: once you know something, you can't un-know it, so you badly underestimate how opaque your model is to a beginner.

### Single-loop versus double-loop learning

Picture a thermostat set to 68°F. The room gets cold, so it turns on the heat. That's **single-loop learning** — adjusting your action to hit a goal.

It never asks, "Is 68°F even the right setting? Who chose it, and why?" That question is **double-loop learning** — questioning the goal itself.

Most organisations are excellent thermostats: fast, precise, consistent. But if the set-point came from a wrong assumption nobody revisits, they efficiently chase the wrong goal forever. Double-loop learning is how mental models actually get updated. It's blocked by fear of looking incompetent and by authority gradients that silence dissent — and enabled by psychological safety, devil's-advocate roles, pre-mortems, and anonymous input before group discussion.

## Common misconceptions

- **"Transcending a paradigm means having no convictions."** The opposite. It means acting on your best model while staying ready to revise it. Conviction without rigidity, not paralysis.
- **"You change a paradigm by winning the argument."** Almost never. People change their minds when the old model visibly fails *in their own experience*, not when they're out-argued. Whitepapers and persuasion workshops mostly bounce off.
- **"Announcing a new value changes the paradigm."** Saying "we now put customers first" changes nothing if the incentives still reward cost-cutting. Structure follows paradigm — so if the structure hasn't moved, neither has the real belief.
- **"The boiled frog is biology."** Real frogs jump out of slowly heated water. It's only a metaphor for how human threat-detection is tuned to sudden change, not slow drift. Use it as a metaphor, not a fact.

## Paradigms in the wild

**Kodak.** Its own engineers built the digital camera, and its own forecasts saw film fading. But the operating belief was "we are a film company." That belief decided how every piece of information got read. Embracing digital meant dismantling a hugely profitable film business, so protecting it felt rational right up to the 2012 bankruptcy filing. Blockbuster told the same story when it passed on buying Netflix around 2000 for roughly $50 million.

**Toyota versus Taylorism.** Frederick Taylor's scientific management assumed "workers are interchangeable units; engineers design the work." The Toyota Production System reversed it: "workers are thinking partners; improvement comes from the people doing the work." Toyota even lets any worker pull a cord to stop the entire production line over a quality problem. At the 1984 NUMMI plant, the *same* workers and equipment that had produced GM's worst quality became one of America's most productive plants within two years under Toyota's management. Nothing physical changed. Only the paradigm.

**The Theory of Constraints.** In *The Goal*, Eliyahu Goldratt attacked the belief that "a busy machine is a productive machine." His counter-paradigm: a system's output is limited by one constraint, so improving anything that *isn't* the constraint just piles up inventory and lengthens lead times. "A balanced plant is a bankrupt plant," he wrote. An hour lost at the bottleneck is an hour lost for the whole system. It overturned a belief that felt obviously true — which is what a real paradigm shift does.

## How to use this

You can't change a belief you can't see, so the work starts with dragging models into the light. Five reliable tools, then a question to keep asking:

1. **Run a pre-mortem.** Before a project starts, imagine it's six months later and has failed badly. Have everyone write down, independently, every plausible reason. This tests the model *before* it costs you anything.
2. **Use the left-hand column.** Split a page. On the right, write what was actually said in a hard conversation. On the left, write what you were thinking but didn't say. The gap exposes your real operating assumptions.
3. **Walk back down the ladder.** Trace a decision in reverse: "What data led to that conclusion? What meaning did I add? What assumption am I making?"
4. **Balance advocacy with inquiry.** For every opinion, add your reasoning and invite challenge: "Here's what I think and why — what's your view?" That makes the model visible and testable instead of just asserted.
5. **Collect anomalies.** Hunt for results your current model can't explain, and keep raising them with open-minded people. Accumulated anomalies, not clever arguments, are what eventually tip a paradigm.

One habit ties it all together: **watch behaviour, not what people say.** Argyris separated *espoused theory* (what we claim to believe) from *theory-in-use* (what our actions reveal). They diverge constantly. To find someone's real mental model — including your own — look at what actually gets done.

## Conclusion

The deepest leverage in any system isn't in its numbers, or even its rules. It's in the beliefs that produced those rules. Change what people take to be obviously true, and the goals, structures, and behaviours quietly reorganise themselves around the new belief.

So the next time a problem keeps returning no matter how hard you fix it, resist the patch. Ask instead: what would have to be true for smart people to keep building this? The answer is usually a belief nobody has said out loud — and that hidden assumption is where the real power has been hiding the whole time. It's also the same blind spot that lets a slow-moving threat sit in plain sight until it's too late, which is a story worth telling on its own.
