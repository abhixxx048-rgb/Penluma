Most people who say they "can't write" are actually doing two jobs at once: dreaming up ideas and polishing the words, both in the same breath. That is exhausting, and it is exactly why the blank page feels so heavy.

Here is the good news. The fix is not talent. It is a simple split: get the mess down first, then make it clear in a separate sitting. Do that, and any rough first attempt becomes something a stranger can understand.

## Why this matters

Unclear writing costs you in ways you rarely notice. The email that gets misread. The proposal nobody finishes. The good idea buried under so much padding that your reader gives up before they reach it.

The hidden cost is in your head, too. When you try to think and polish at the same time, you freeze. The page stays empty, the deadline creeps closer, and you start to believe writing just isn't your thing.

It is your thing. You have simply been doing one impossible job instead of two easy ones. Separating those jobs is the single highest-leverage writing skill you can learn, and you can learn it today.

## Write first, fix later: the two-stage rule

There are really only two stages to good writing, and they should never happen at the same moment.

- **Drafting** is getting your raw thoughts onto the page as fast as you can. You are allowed to be messy, repetitive, and ugly. The only goal is to exist.
- **Editing** is reshaping what already exists so a reader can follow it. No new thinking about *what* to say. Only fixing *how* it is said.

Why keep them apart? Your **working memory** is the small mental "desk" where you hold whatever you are thinking about right now. It can only juggle a few things at once. When you try to invent an idea, word it perfectly, and check the grammar all in one go, the desk overflows and you stall.

Think of a potter. Drafting is dumping all the clay onto the wheel. Editing is shaping the pot. You cannot shape clay you haven't put down yet, and you can't shape it while still hauling in more buckets. Two motions, two moments.

So write badly on purpose. A bad draft you can fix beats a perfect sentence you never wrote. Permission to be messy is what unlocks the page.

## The heart of editing: cut, then clarify

The most famous rule in English writing comes from Strunk and White's *The Elements of Style*: **"Omit needless words."** A sentence should contain no unnecessary words the way a machine has no unnecessary parts.

Most first drafts are 20 to 40 percent padding. So editing is mostly *deletion*, not addition. The fastest wins come from swapping bloated phrases for lean ones:

| Flabby phrase | Lean version |
| --- | --- |
| in order to | to |
| due to the fact that | because |
| at this point in time | now |
| has the ability to | can |
| a large number of | many |
| I think that maybe we should | we should |

Notice the pattern. **Hedges** ("I think that maybe"), **throat-clearing** ("at this point in time"), and **noun-puffs** ("has the ability to") all dissolve into shorter, stronger words. Once you can spot these three, half your editing is done.

## Reviving a sick sentence: a simple Paramedic Method

Some sentences aren't padded so much as tangled. Writing teacher Richard Lanham created the **Paramedic Method**, a fast, mechanical way to "revive" a sentence that has stopped making sense. Stripped to its useful core, here is what you do to any knotted sentence:

1. **Find the action.** Ask who is doing what. Make that person the subject and that action the verb.
2. **Kill weak verbs.** Replace "is, are, was, were" plus a noun with one strong verb. "Made a decision" becomes "decided." "Is dependent on" becomes "depends on."
3. **Cut the prepositional pile-up.** Strings of "of the... in the... for the..." usually hide a simpler sentence underneath.
4. **Start fast.** Delete slow openers like "It is important to note that" and "There are many reasons why."

Watch it work on a real sentence.

**Sick (24 words):** "It is important to note that the making of a decision by the committee was done in a way that was quite slow."

**Treated (4 words):** "The committee decided slowly."

We found the actor (committee), used a strong verb (decided), cut the prepositions, and deleted the slow opener. Same meaning, 80 percent lighter.

## The clarity checklist you can reuse forever

Run these passes **one at a time**. Do not try to catch everything in a single read. That just overloads the desk again. Each pass has exactly one job.

1. **One idea per sentence.** If a sentence has two "ands" or a stray "which," try splitting it in two. Short sentences are easier to hold.
2. **Cut needless words.** For each word, ask: "If I remove this, do I lose meaning?" If not, cut it.
3. **Replace abstract with concrete.** Swap vague nouns for things a reader can picture. "We saw improvements in performance" becomes "Pages loaded in 2 seconds instead of 6."
4. **Fix flabby openings.** Hunt for "There is," "There are," and "It is" at the start of sentences and rewrite around the real subject.
5. **Strong verbs, active voice.** Prefer "We shipped it" over "It was shipped by us." The doer comes first.
6. **Read it aloud.** Your ear catches what your eye skips: run-ons, missing words, awkward rhythm. If you run out of breath, the sentence is too long.
7. **The stranger test.** Imagine someone who knows nothing about your topic. Would they get it on the first read, without asking a question? If not, name the missing piece and add it.

## Watch it happen: a full before and after

Theory is nice, but the transformation is what convinces. Here is a real, bloated paragraph.

**Before (71 words):**

> It is important to note that there are a number of different reasons as to why our customers seem to be experiencing some level of difficulty in terms of the checkout process, and one of the main things that we think is maybe causing this is the fact that the page is loading in a way that is quite slow, which then leads to people abandoning their carts in many cases.

**After (26 words):**

> Customers struggle at checkout because the page loads slowly. Many give up and abandon their carts. Speeding up the page should fix it.

Here is what changed, mapped to the checklist: we cut the slow opener ("It is important to note"), removed the hedges ("seem to," "we think is maybe"), found the actors (customers, page), used strong verbs (struggle, loads, give up), replaced "some level of difficulty" with the concrete cause, and split one bloated sentence into three clear ones.

Look at the bonus. The edit didn't just polish the words. It surfaced a usable insight that was hiding in the fog: **slow page leads to lost sales.** Clear editing exposes the idea underneath.

## Common misconceptions

**"Good writers get it right the first time."** They don't. They write messy drafts like everyone else and then edit hard. The polish you admire is the *fifth* version, not the first.

**"Editing means adding more sophisticated words."** The opposite. Editing usually means removing words and choosing simpler ones. Clear beats clever every time.

**"Shorter writing sounds dumber."** Shorter writing sounds *confident.* Padding signals uncertainty. "We should ship this" lands harder than "I think that maybe we should consider shipping this."

**"I should fix each sentence before I move on."** This is the costliest myth. Editing while you draft stalls you and burns willpower on sentences you may later delete. Finish the ugly draft, *then* open the checklist.

## How to use this today

You don't need a writing course. You need four small reps:

1. **Cut by a third.** Take any paragraph you wrote recently, an email works fine. Count the words. Edit it down by at least 30 percent using passes 1 and 2. Read both versions aloud.
2. **Revive one sentence.** Find a long, ugly sentence and run the Paramedic Method on it: find the actor, pick a strong verb, cut the prepositions, start fast.
3. **Stranger test a message.** Before you send your next work message, ask whether someone with zero background would understand it on the first read. Fix the one part that fails.
4. **Run a two-stage drill.** Tomorrow, write a 150-word draft in 10 minutes with editing *banned.* The next day, edit it cold. Notice how much easier each job feels alone.

## Conclusion

If you remember one thing, remember this: editing is a series of single-purpose passes, not one heroic read-through. Draft messy, then cut words, split ideas, make it concrete, read aloud, and ask whether a stranger would get it.

But notice what just happened in that checkout example. Forcing the sentence to be clear *revealed* an idea you didn't know you had. That is the quiet secret of editing: the fog on the page is usually fog in the mind. So the next time a sentence refuses to come out clean, ask yourself a sharper question. Is the writing unclear, or is the *thought* not finished yet? Learn to tell those two apart, and you stop being someone who edits words and become someone who thinks on paper.
