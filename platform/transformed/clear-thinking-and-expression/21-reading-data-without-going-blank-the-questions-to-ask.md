You open a chart or a table, and your mind goes empty. You stare. Nothing comes. So you nod, mutter "interesting," and quietly hope nobody asks you what it means.

Here is the good news: this is almost never because you are "bad at numbers." It is because you have no **routine** for attacking data. Give yourself a fixed list of questions to ask every single time, and the blank screen turns into a short, calm conversation with the numbers.

## Why this matters

Data shows up everywhere now: a sales dashboard at work, a battery graph on your phone, a league table, your bank statement, a news infographic designed to alarm you. The people who can calmly read those and say "so the interesting thing is X" make better decisions and look sharper doing it.

The skill is not raw intelligence. It is having a method. And a method is something you can learn in ten minutes and keep for life.

## Why your mind goes blank (and why it's not your fault)

Your **working memory** is the small mental space that holds whatever you are thinking about right now. It is tiny. The psychologist George Miller famously estimated it at around seven items; later research suggests it is closer to four.

A chart throws far more than four things at you at once: numbers, labels, colours, two axes, a legend, a title. Your working memory overloads, and overload *feels* like blankness.

The fix is not "be smarter." The fix is to look at **one thing at a time, in a fixed order**. A checklist does the remembering for you, so your brain is freed up to actually think.

Pilots run a checklist before takeoff. Surgeons run one before cutting. Not because they are forgetful or slow, but because under pressure a checklist beats memory every time. You deserve the same tool.

## The six questions to ask, every time

Ask these in order, out loud or on paper, whenever you face a chart, table, or report. Do not skip ahead. Each question should produce just **one short sentence**.

1. **What am I looking at?** The units, the time period, the source. Dollars or counts? This month or this year? Who made it?
2. **What's the big picture?** The overall level. Is the number big or small, healthy or worrying?
3. **What's changing?** The trend. Is it going up, down, or staying flat over time?
4. **Compared to what?** The baseline or benchmark. Up versus last month? Better or worse than the target?
5. **What's surprising?** The one thing that breaks the pattern: a spike, a dip, an odd row.
6. **What's missing?** What the data does *not* show, but you would want to know.

A few of those words deserve plain definitions before we lean on them:

- **Units** — what each number actually measures: rupees, people, percent, orders. A "5" means nothing until you know it is 5 *what*.
- **Trend** — the direction over time: up, down, or flat.
- **Baseline** — a starting point you measure against: last week, last year, the day before a change.
- **Benchmark** — an outside standard you compare to: a target, an industry average, a competitor.
- **Base rate** — how common something normally is. "10 complaints" sounds bad until you learn it came from 10,000 orders, so it is 0.1%.

## Climb the ladder: describe, then compare, then explain

Beginners freeze because they leap straight to the hardest question: *why did this happen?* Skip the easy rungs and you fall.

Instead, climb the ladder in order. This mirrors how real analysts think.

### Layer 1 — Descriptive (easiest)

Just read it back in plain words, with no opinion yet. "Tuesday had 120 orders." That's it.

### Layer 2 — Comparative

Put two numbers side by side. "Tuesday's 120 is double Monday's 60." Now the number has meaning, because meaning lives in the comparison.

### Layer 3 — Causal (hardest)

Offer a possible reason, and clearly label it as a guess. "Maybe the Tuesday discount drove it." Never a fact, always a question to test.

Do the layers in that order and the blank feeling never gets a chance to start, because you are always doing the easy thing first.

## Reading a chart, step by step

For any chart, do this slow walk *before* you let yourself feel anything about it:

1. **Read the title.** It usually tells you the whole point in one line.
2. **Read the bottom axis (the X axis).** Usually time or categories. Ask "what is each bar or point?"
3. **Read the side axis (the Y axis).** The units and the scale. Crucially: does it start at zero?
4. **Find the highest and lowest points.** Now your eye has anchors.
5. **Trace the shape left to right.** Up, down, flat, bumpy? Say it in one sentence.
6. **Now run the six questions.**

## A tiny worked example

Here are one week of orders at a small print shop.

| Day | Orders |
| --- | ------ |
| Mon | 60     |
| Tue | 62     |
| Wed | 58     |
| Thu | 61     |
| Fri | 120    |

Now narrate the six questions, one sentence each:

1. **What am I looking at?** Daily order counts (units = orders) for one week, Monday to Friday.
2. **Big picture?** Most days sit around 60 orders, a steady, modest level.
3. **What's changing?** Monday to Thursday is flat. Friday jumps.
4. **Compared to what?** Friday's 120 is roughly double the ~60 baseline of every other day.
5. **What's surprising?** That Friday spike, breaking an otherwise flat week.
6. **What's missing?** I can't see *why* Friday spiked (no column for promotions, holidays, or order size), and I only have one week, so I can't tell if Friday is always big.

Look what happened. From a wall of numbers you now have a clear story, "steady week, big Friday, worth finding out why," plus a real next question. That is the exact opposite of going blank.

Notice the layers in action too: steps 1 to 3 were **descriptive**, step 4 was **comparative**, and the "why" you deliberately held back as a **causal** question to investigate. That restraint is what separates careful thinking from jumping to conclusions.

## Common misconceptions

**"I just need to be better with numbers."** No. You need a routine. The blankness is working-memory overload, not a maths gap. A checklist solves it instantly.

**"A big number is good news (or bad news)."** A number alone is just a number. "Sales were ₹40,000!" Good or bad? You cannot know until you ask "compared to what?" Meaning lives in the comparison, never in the raw figure.

**"If two things moved together, one caused the other."** Sales rose and the weather warmed, so the heat sold more? Maybe, maybe not. Things moving together is a clue, not proof. Always phrase it as "this *might* be because…" and treat it as something to investigate.

**"The chart shape tells me the truth."** Only if you checked the scale. A Y axis that starts at 95 instead of 0 can make a tiny rise look like a cliff. A scary-looking line is sometimes just a zoomed-in axis. Check the scale before you trust the shape.

**"A scary number is scary."** Not without its base rate. "500 people affected!" out of how many? Out of 600 it's a crisis; out of 60 million it's a rounding error.

## How to use this

Try this in the next ten minutes, then keep it as a habit.

1. **Pick any chart or table.** Your phone battery graph, a sports table, your bank statement, a news infographic. Anything.
2. **Write the six questions down the left of a page.** Leave room for an answer beside each.
3. **Write one full sentence for each**, even when it feels obvious. The discipline is the point.
4. **Check the Y axis** for whether it starts at zero before you trust any shape.
5. **Finish with one line:** "So the interesting thing here is ___, and I'd want to know ___."

Then keep your reps going:

- **Three days, three charts.** One chart a day for three days. Spreading practice across days (called **spaced practice**) beats one long cramming session, and it makes the routine fast and automatic.
- **Label a sentence.** Take any data claim you hear and tag it Descriptive, Comparative, or Causal. If it's Causal, rewrite it as a guess.
- **Catch a base rate.** Find one scary news number ("X complaints!", "Y% rise!") and ask: out of how many? Compared to when?
- **Write the "so what."** For one table this week, write a single action sentence beginning "So we should…". Turning a fact into a next step is the move that makes data actually valuable.

That last habit matters most. The whole point of reading data is to find something you can **act on**. The two richest questions for spotting opportunities are already in your checklist: *what's surprising?* (where the easy win usually hides) and *what's missing?* (what to measure next).

## Conclusion

Here is the one thing to carry away: **don't try to absorb data all at once.** Walk the six questions in order, climb describe → compare → explain, and end every read with "so what should we do or find out next?"

Think of it the way a good doctor reads test results. They don't panic at a single high number. They ask what it measures, what's normal, whether it's trending up over past visits, how it compares to the healthy range, what's odd, and what test is still missing. Same calm checklist, just pointed at your numbers instead of your blood.

And once a chart can no longer make you freeze, a sharper question waits: who designed it, and what did they *want* you to feel? Because the most persuasive charts in the world are built to bypass exactly the slow, careful reading you just learned. That is a story for another day.
