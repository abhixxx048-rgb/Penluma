**This document answers one question: how should Print-Flow-360 find and sign up customers - and does it need a sales team to do it? The short answer is no, not yet. It maps out exactly which customers can sign up on their own versus which ones need a human to help close the deal, and it explains why the math forces those choices.**

**The main parts explained simply:**

- **The "animal sizes" framework** - A researcher named Christoph Janz figured out that every software business hunts a certain size of customer. Small customers (called "Rabbits") pay a few hundred dollars a year; mid-size customers ("Deer") pay thousands. The key rule: the way you sell must match the size. You cannot afford to send a salesperson to sign up a small customer, because the salesperson's salary costs more than the customer pays.

- **The $5,000 line** - Research from the firm a16z found that a sales rep can only pay for themselves if the customer pays roughly $5,000 or more per year. Below that line, the only affordable way to get customers is to let them sign up on their own, with no human involved.

- **The five types of customers** - The document breaks Print-Flow-360's market into five groups: solo print shops (cheapest, sign up themselves), growing shops (also self-serve), multi-location or business accounts (big enough for a founder to help close), franchise brand headquarters (a rare, complex deal worth much more), and trade/wholesale printers (not a customer - they are a reseller partner, not a buyer).

- **Two selling approaches, kept separate** - Small and growing shops sign up on their own through the product. The founder steps in personally only for the bigger business accounts. These two approaches must never be mixed on the same customer.

- **Switching customers from competitors** - Nearly every customer is already using a rival product (like InkSoft or DecoNetwork). The document says the best way to win them is comparison web pages plus a self-serve tool that imports their existing product catalog, so switching feels easy and does not require human help.

- **The big unknown risk** - Setting up a print store (products, pricing, designer, payment) is complicated. If a non-technical owner truly cannot finish setup without help, the whole "let them sign up themselves" plan breaks down. This must be tested with real users before spending money on marketing.

- **No free trial with a credit card** - For an unknown brand, requiring a card upfront cuts signups by half. Dropping the card requirement brings in 3–4 times more signups, which is exactly what this type of self-serve business needs.

**What to do with this:** Run a test with 10–20 real, non-technical print shop owners to see if they can set up a store on their own without any help. Everything else in the plan depends on that answer. At the same time, lock in the no-card 14-day free trial - that decision is already settled.
