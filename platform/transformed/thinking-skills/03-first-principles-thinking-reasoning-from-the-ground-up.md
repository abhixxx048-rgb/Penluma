Two men looked at the same rocket and saw two completely different prices. The aerospace industry saw a $65 million machine. Elon Musk added up the raw metal and saw about 2% of that. Same object, same physics, wildly different answers.

The difference was not intelligence. It was a method. Musk refused to accept the price the industry had always charged and instead asked a stranger question: what is this thing actually made of, and what does that cost?

That question is the whole game. It is called **first-principles thinking**, and it is one of the few mental tools that can break you out of limits everyone else treats as permanent.

## Why this matters

Most of us solve problems by looking around at what already exists and copying it, tweaking it, or averaging it. That works most of the time. It is fast, and it lets you stand on everyone else's experience instead of starting from zero.

But it also quietly locks you inside a box built by everyone who came before you. If you only ever improve on the existing template, the best you can do is a slightly better version of it.

First-principles thinking is the way out of that box. It is how people find solutions that are 10 times better instead of 10% better, because they stop asking "how do others do this?" and start asking "what is actually true here?"

The core idea is simple: **strip a problem down to the most basic facts you know are true, then build your answer up from there.** You reason from the ground up, not from the pattern you inherited.

## Where the idea comes from

The phrase "first principles" is more than 2,000 years old. The Greek philosopher **Aristotle** used the word *archai* - meaning "origins" or "starting points" - to describe the fundamental truths that everything else rests on.

His definition was sharp: a first principle is something that cannot be derived from anything more basic. It is the bedrock. Every other claim in a field sits on top of it.

Aristotle argued that real understanding meant being able to trace any claim back to those bedrock truths. If you couldn't, you were just repeating what someone else said. You didn't truly know it.

**Physics later turned this into its core method.** Physicists refuse to accept a claim just because everyone believes it. They demand that you derive it from known laws and measured facts. That stubbornness is exactly why physics keeps overturning what "everybody knew" - that the Earth sat at the center of the universe, that light travels instantly, that time ticks the same for everyone. Each time, someone went back to first principles and found the inherited assumption was simply wrong.

Think of it like the foundation of a building. Before you add floors, you dig down to solid rock. First-principles thinking means refusing to build on sand just because the building next door was built that way.

## The contrast: reasoning by analogy

The opposite of first-principles thinking is **reasoning by analogy** - doing something because it resembles how something else was done. You look at an existing solution, or the way your industry has always operated, and you work from there.

This is not stupid. Analogy is fast, and it is how humans get through ordinary life. You don't rederive the laws of nutrition every morning before breakfast - you just eat roughly what worked yesterday. Analogy lets you borrow from all of human experience without starting over each time.

But analogy has a ceiling. When you reason from what already exists, you can only produce **incremental change** - small improvements on the existing template. You cannot see past the template itself.

Here is the difference side by side:

| Approach | Starting point | Output | Speed | Upper limit |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Reasoning by analogy | What others have done | Incremental improvement | Fast | Bounded by the original template |
| First-principles reasoning | Fundamental, verified truths | Potentially radical solutions | Slow, effortful | Bounded only by physical or logical law |

As Musk put it: *"The normal way we conduct our lives is we reason by analogy... it's mentally easier to reason by analogy rather than from first principles. First principles is kind of a physics way of looking at the world."*

In other words, analogy is fast and usually good enough. First principles is slow and hard - but it is the only way to break through limits the whole industry has accepted without question.

## Two famous examples: rockets and batteries

The clearest public demonstrations of this method come from two companies Musk built. You can watch the thinking working in real time.

### SpaceX: what is a rocket really made of?

When Musk started looking at rockets in the early 2000s, the industry consensus was that rockets were just expensive - launch prices ran as high as **$65 million** each. The analogy everyone used was simple: "Rockets cost a fortune. That's the nature of space hardware."

Musk refused to accept that and asked a first-principles question instead: **what is a rocket actually made of?**

He listed the physical materials - aerospace-grade aluminum alloys, titanium, copper, carbon fiber, and a few others. Then he looked up the commodity-market price of each. The finding stunned him: the raw materials in a rocket cost roughly **2% of the typical purchase price**. The other 98% was markup, overhead, supply-chain tradition, and decades of baked-in assumptions.

That single insight led him to found SpaceX and build rockets in-house from raw materials. The result: SpaceX cut Falcon 9 launch costs to around **$2,700 per kilogram** to low Earth orbit, against the Space Shuttle's roughly **$90,000 per kilogram** - a reduction of about 97%.

The two ways of reasoning look like this:

- **Industry logic:** "Rockets cost ~$65M because rockets have always cost that." → No progress possible. The template is accepted.
- **First-principles logic:** "What *is* a rocket? Aluminum, titanium, carbon fiber. What do those cost? About 2% of launch price. What can we build starting there?" → A radically cheaper rocket.

### Tesla: what is a battery really made of?

The second example hits even harder, because the "impossible" claim was backed by the entire electric-vehicle industry.

In the early 2010s, the accepted wisdom was that battery packs would always cost around **$600 per kilowatt-hour** (a kilowatt-hour is just a unit of stored energy - roughly what your laptop uses over about 50 hours). At that price, electric cars would always be too expensive for the mass market. The analogy was airtight: batteries are expensive, so electric cars are expensive, so mass adoption is far away.

Musk ran the same drill. He asked: **what are batteries made of?** The answer: cobalt, nickel, aluminum, carbon, some polymers, and a steel casing. He looked up what those raw materials cost on open commodity exchanges. The finding: bought directly, the constituent materials for a battery pack came to roughly **$80 per kilowatt-hour**.

That is a gap of more than 7x between the raw material cost and the market price. Where did the gap come from? From layers of convention, inefficient supply chains, and unquestioned assumptions stacked up over decades.

Tesla concluded that smart manufacturing - designing cells in-house, optimizing chemistry, building its own "Gigafactory" - could close most of that gap. It has since driven battery costs well under $200 per kilowatt-hour and keeps pushing lower.

Notice the move in plain steps:

1. **What are we sure is true?** The materials exist and have known prices.
2. **What are we assuming without proof?** That today's supply chains and methods are near-optimal.
3. **What happens when we challenge that?** A 7x gap appears - and it points straight at where value can be created.

The gap between raw-material cost and market price is a map of every assumption an industry has never questioned. First-principles thinking is what reveals that map.

## It works far beyond engineering

You do not need a rocket factory to use this. The method shows up wherever someone refused to copy and instead asked "but why, really?"

### Netflix questioned the disc

The video rental industry assumed people wanted to drive to a store, pick a physical disc, and return it by a due date. That was the whole template - it was how Blockbuster ran.

Reed Hastings went to first principles: what do people *actually* want? To watch the story they chose, when they choose, with no late fee and no trip across town. The physical disc was just an inherited delivery method, not a law of nature. Strip it out, and what's left is streaming on demand. Netflix didn't improve video rental - it replaced the assumption underneath it.

### Airbnb questioned the hotel

The hospitality industry assumed that offering accommodation meant owning buildings - hotels. Brian Chesky and Joe Gebbia asked the basic question: what does a traveler fundamentally need? A safe place to sleep.

What already exists that could provide that? Millions of spare rooms and empty apartments. The hotel building was a convention that arose before anyone could reliably match spare rooms to travelers. Strip the convention, and what's left is a matching platform. Airbnb didn't build a better hotel - it questioned whether hotels needed to exist at all for the core need to be met.

### Your budget and your career

The same drill works on your own life.

Suppose you believe you can't afford to save each month. The analogy is "I've always spent everything I earn; that's just how my budget works." The first-principles version: what do you *genuinely* have to spend on - rent, food, transport to work, utilities? What does each truly cost? Done honestly, most people find that "I can't save" isn't a physical law. It's a habit or unexamined spending, not a fundamental constraint.

Or take a career. Many people stay in a role because they've invested years in it: "I've done this for a decade, so I have to keep doing it." The first-principles questions cut through the sunk-cost story: What skills do I actually have? Which transfer? What does the market pay for? What do I actually want from work? Drill to those facts and entirely different paths come into view.

## Common misconceptions

A few myths get in the way of using this well.

- **Myth: it means ignoring experts.** Reality: it means being precise about which parts of expert knowledge are *established facts* and which are *conventions that hardened into assumptions*. The materials cost of a battery is a fact. The supply chain that inflates it 7x is a convention. First principles separates the two - it doesn't dismiss expertise.

- **Myth: just keep asking "but why?"** Reality: shallow questioning without real knowledge produces nonsense, not insight. You can't find the true first principles of battery chemistry if you don't understand chemistry. The method needs substance underneath it.

- **Myth: use it for everything.** Reality: using first principles to decide what to eat for lunch would be paralyzing. Analogy is the right tool for most everyday choices. First principles is for the high-stakes calls where the inherited template might be badly wrong.

- **Myth: it's a shortcut.** Reality: it's the opposite. It's slow and mentally taxing. The payoff is big, but so is the effort.

## How to use this: the three-step drill

The method isn't mysterious. It's a pattern you can learn and repeat.

1. **Name your assumptions.** Write down what you "know" about the problem. Be suspicious of anything that starts with "everyone knows" or "that's just how it works." Those phrases are flags, not facts.

2. **Break it down to fundamentals.** Ask what physical facts, measurable quantities, or logical truths must hold here regardless of current practice. Strip away convention until you hit bedrock - the things that would still be true even if the whole industry vanished.

3. **Reason back up.** Build a new solution using only what you confirmed in step two. Don't let the old template sneak back in until you have a clean answer from the fundamentals.

A simple way to remember the flow:

> What do I *assume*? → What do I *know* for certain? → What can I *build* from those facts alone?

One practical trigger: whenever you catch yourself saying "that's just how it's done" or "everyone knows X is impossible," treat it as an invitation. That sentence usually means someone has accepted a constraint that may not be real - and that's exactly where first-principles thinking pays off.

And respect the cost. This method is slow, it demands genuine domain knowledge, and most decisions simply don't need it. Reserve it for the moments where the stakes are high and the standard answer might be fundamentally wrong.

## Conclusion

Here's the one idea worth keeping: **the gap between what something is made of and what it costs is a map of assumptions nobody has questioned.** First-principles thinking is how you read that map. Everyone stares at the $65 million rocket. Only a few people ask what the metal costs.

You don't have to use it often. You just have to recognize the rare moments when the whole crowd is reasoning by analogy off a template that might be wrong - and be willing to dig down to bedrock while everyone else builds on sand.

There's a catch, though. Knowing the method isn't the same as knowing *when* a constraint is real versus invented. That judgment - telling a genuine physical limit from a convention dressed up as one - is its own skill. It's where mental models like inversion and second-order thinking come in, and it's where the most interesting thinking really begins.
