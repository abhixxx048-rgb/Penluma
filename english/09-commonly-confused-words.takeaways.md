**This document is about small word mix-ups that are very easy to make in English — things like writing "its" when you mean "it's", or "login" when you should write "log in". These are not big grammar rules — they are tiny word choices. But getting them wrong makes your writing look careless, even when your actual work is good.**

**The main parts explained simply:**

- **The apostrophe test (it's / its, you're / your, they're / their, who's / whose)** — When you see a word with an apostrophe like "it's", it is always short for two words ("it is" or "it has"). If you cannot expand it to two words, do not use an apostrophe. Example: "its config" means the config belongs to it — no apostrophe needed.

- **lose vs loose** — "Lose" (one "o") is the action of misplacing something: "don't lose the data." "Loose" means not tight: "a loose cable." These are spelled differently and mean different things.

- **then vs than** — "Than" is for comparisons: "faster than before." "Then" is for time or order: "first build, then deploy."

- **affect vs effect** — "Affect" is the action (the verb): "this will affect the cache." "Effect" is the result (the noun): "what is the effect on the cache?" A shortcut: Affect = Action, Effect = End result.

- **One word as a thing, two words as an action (login / log in, setup / set up, backup / back up)** — When you name a thing, it is one word: "the login screen", "the setup guide". When you describe doing the action, it is two words: "please log in", "set up the server."

- **fewer vs less** — If you can count the items (3 errors, 5 tickets), use "fewer." If you cannot count it (less memory, less time), use "less."

- **accept vs except** — "Accept" means to agree or receive something. "Except" means to leave something out: "all tests pass except one."

- **e.g. vs i.e.** — "e.g." means "for example." "i.e." means "that is" or "in other words." Both need a comma after them.

- **Practice drills and answer key** — The document ends with 14 short exercises and full answers so you can check yourself right away.

**What to do with this:** Before you send a commit message or Slack message, do one quick check — scan for "its/it's", "lose/loose", "then/than", and "login/log in". Those four catch most mistakes. If a word has an apostrophe, say it out loud as two words — if it sounds wrong, remove the apostrophe.
