**This document is about making your verb match your subject — the "doer" of the action in a sentence. In English, a single thing needs one form of the verb, and many things need a different form. Getting this right makes your writing look careful and professional, even in quick Slack messages or commit notes.**

**The main parts explained simply:**

- **The basic rule** — One thing uses "is / does / returns / passes." Many things use "are / do / return / pass." The tricky part: always look for the *real* doer, not just the nearest word.
- **Hidden subjects — the "of …" trap** — A phrase like "the list of files" might tempt you to write "the files are," but the real subject is "list," so the correct form is "the list is." Cross out the "of …" part to find the true subject.
- **Words that are always singular** — "Each," "every," "everyone," "someone," "nobody," and "one of …" always take the singular form, even when they feel like they refer to many people or things. Example: "Each of the services **needs** a restart."
- **"There is" vs "There are"** — Use "there is" for one thing and "there are" for more than one. Match the verb to what comes right after it.
- **Team / group / list (collective nouns)** — In everyday professional English, treat these as one single thing. Write "The team **is** ready," not "The team are ready."
- **"Many" vs "much"** — Use "many" with things you can count (many bugs, many mistakes). Use "much" with things you cannot count (much progress, much memory). This also affects whether your noun needs a plural "-s."
- **Everyday examples** — The document shows exactly how these rules apply to commit messages, pull-request titles, standup updates, code comments, and bug reports — the writing you do every day as a developer.
- **Drills and answer key** — Twelve practice sentences let you test yourself, with full corrections and plain explanations so you can see the pattern, not just memorise the answer.

**What to do with this:**

Before you send a message or write a commit, quickly ask: "Is my subject one thing or many?" Then pick the matching verb form. Pay special attention to "each / every / one of …" — these always take the singular form and are the most common source of mistakes.
