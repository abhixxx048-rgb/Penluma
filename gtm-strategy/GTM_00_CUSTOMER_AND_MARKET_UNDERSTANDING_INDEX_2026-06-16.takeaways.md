**This document is a map to six research guides that help you understand your customers and your market before you spend money on marketing or sales. If you don't know who your best customer is, what problem they really need solved, and how much they'll pay, any money spent on advertising or sales will mostly be wasted. This map tells you which guide to read and when.**

**The main parts explained simply:**

- **GTM_01 - Who your best customer is** - Describes the exact type of shop owner you should sell to first: a small, non-technical local print or sign shop, usually run by one person. Read this first and come back to it whenever you feel tempted to sell to bigger or more complicated businesses.

- **GTM_02 - Why they would buy (and why they'd switch)** - Explains what problems shop owners are actually trying to solve - things like getting rid of back-and-forth emails, looking professional online, and saving time on orders. Use this when writing website copy or deciding which features to show first.

- **GTM_03 - Learning from every win, loss, and cancellation** - A simple method for talking to customers you won, customers you lost, and customers who left. Even two or three of these conversations will tell you more than any report. Use this every time a deal closes or a customer cancels.

- **GTM_04 - Finding the right price** - Covers simple ways to find out how much shop owners will actually pay, and which pricing shape (per store, per order) makes sense. Use this before locking in your pricing tiers.

- **GTM_05 - Keeping an eye on competition** - A practice for tracking who else shop owners consider buying from - including doing nothing or sticking with a basic website. Use this when writing sales objection responses or deciding what to build next.

- **GTM_06 - How big is the market** - A realistic estimate of how many shops you could sell to, limited by payment methods available and the type of shop you're targeting. Use this when preparing for investor conversations or setting yearly targets.

**What to do with this:**

Talk to every customer who signs up, cancels, or walks away - this is the single most valuable thing you can do right now. Then make sure you are crystal clear on the one main problem your best customer is hiring your software to solve, and say that plainly on your website and in your first email to new users.
