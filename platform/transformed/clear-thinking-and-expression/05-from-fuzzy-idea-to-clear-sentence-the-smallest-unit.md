You know the feeling. You [open your mouth in a meeting](/blog/clear-thinking-and-expression/08-speaking-clearly-confidently-in-real-time), say the thing, and watch faces go blank. Or you stare at an email for ten minutes, sure that what you mean is simple, yet every sentence comes out like wet cardboard.

Here is the surprising part: you almost never have a "big writing problem." You have a small sentence problem, repeated many times. Fix the sentence, and the paragraph, the email, and the meeting point all get easier.

## Why this matters

People do not hear you in paragraphs. They hear you one sentence at a time. If each sentence is clear, the whole thing is clear. If one sentence tries to carry too much, understanding breaks right there.

Think of the sentence as the **atom** of an idea, the smallest piece you can actually control. When you speak and people look confused, it is usually because a single sentence was overloaded. When you read something and "nothing sticks," it is often because you never re-said the idea in one clean sentence of your own.

So this is not a grammar lesson. It is leverage. Get good at the sentence and you fix most of your "I just can't explain it" problem, because the sentence is the exact spot where understanding and expression meet.

## Rule 1: One idea per sentence

Your [**working memory**](/blog/clear-thinking-and-expression/02-working-memory-schemas-chunking-your-brain-s-real-limits) is the small mental "desk" where you hold thoughts for a few seconds. It can only juggle a few items at once. A sentence stuffed with three ideas overflows that desk for you and for your listener. So put one idea on the desk at a time.

**Before:** "I think the project is behind because the designer was sick and also we changed the scope which nobody wrote down, so now the client is upset and we might lose them unless we do something fast."

**After:** "The project is two weeks behind. There were two causes: the designer was out sick, and we changed the scope without writing it down. As a result, the client is upset. I want to send a recovery plan today."

The "after" is barely shorter. But each sentence does one job. That is the difference between a pile and a staircase.

## Rule 2: Say who does what, in that order

The clearest sentence shape in English is **who does what to what**: the doer, the action, the thing acted on. When a sentence feels muddy, find the doer and the action and put them up front.

> Sam → approved → the budget

**Before:** "A decision regarding the budget was made by the team." Who decided? Hidden. What happened? Buried.

**After:** "The team approved the budget." Three words shorter, and you instantly know who did what.

## Rule 3: Prefer active voice

**Active voice** puts the doer first: "Sam broke the build." **Passive voice** puts the thing first and lets the doer hide or vanish: "The build was broken."

Here is a quick test. If you can add "...by zombies" to the end and it still fits, the sentence is passive: "mistakes were made [by zombies]."

Passive is not wrong, but it hides *who* is responsible and adds words. "Errors were found in the report" sounds professional and tells nobody what to do. "I found three errors in the report" is honest, specific, and points at a next step.

## Rule 4: Choose concrete over abstract

**Abstract** words point to things you cannot see or touch: synergy, value, issues, leverage. **Concrete** words point to things you could photograph or count: a printer, three orders, Tuesday. Concrete words land because the brain can picture them.

| Vague / abstract | Concrete rewrite |
| --- | --- |
| "We had some issues." | "Two orders shipped to the wrong address." |
| "It's taking a while." | "It's taken nine days; we expected three." |
| "We should improve engagement." | "We should get customers to reorder within 30 days." |
| "The results were significant." | "Sales rose from 40 to 95 a week." |

An abstract word is like a blurry photo. The shape is there, but you cannot tell what it is. When someone squints at your sentence, you handed them a blur. Concrete words bring it into focus.

## Rule 5: Cut filler and hedging

**Filler** is words that add length but no meaning: "basically," "in order to," "the fact that," "at this point in time." **Hedging** is the soft words that weaken your point: "kind of," "I just think maybe," "sort of," "it seems like it might possibly."

A little hedging is polite. A pile of it makes you sound unsure of your own thought.

**Before:** "I just kind of feel like maybe we should possibly think about, at some point, perhaps revisiting the pricing, if that makes sense?"

**After:** "I think we should raise the price. Here's why."

A few swaps you can make on autopilot:

| Don't write | Write |
| --- | --- |
| in order to | to |
| due to the fact that | because |
| at this point in time | now |
| has the ability to | can |
| a large number of | many |

## The rescue tool: "I think ___ because ___"

This one template untangles almost any fuzzy thought. A fuzzy idea is usually a **claim** (what you believe) plus a **reason** (why), tangled together. Pull them apart:

> I think **[ your claim ]** because **[ your reason ].**

The first blank forces you to commit to a clear claim. The second forces you to give evidence. If you cannot fill the "because," you do not yet have an opinion. You have a mood, and now you know exactly what to go find out.

**Fuzzy feeling:** "Eh, this homepage feels off."

**Template:** "I think the homepage buries the main button, because I had to scroll twice to find 'Order Now.'"

Now it is a claim someone can act on or argue with.

## The "so what?" test

After writing a sentence, ask: **so what, why should the reader care?** If the sentence has no answer, either cut it or add the consequence. This turns flat facts into points.

"Our load time is 4 seconds." So what? "Our load time is 4 seconds, and customers leave after 3, so we're losing buyers before the page even appears."

## Common misconceptions

**Myth: big or technical words make you sound smart.** The opposite is true. The psychologist Daniel Oppenheimer ran a study with a deliberately overblown title, "Consequences of Erudite Vernacular Utilized Irrespective of Necessity," and found that needlessly complex wording makes readers judge the writer as *less* capable. Plain words win.

**Myth: jargon is precise.** Jargon ("synergize," "leverage the funnel," "circle back," "low-hanging fruit") feels like insider precision, but it often hides the fact that there is no clear thought underneath. Replace each jargon word with what you literally mean. A good gut check: would you say this out loud to a smart friend over coffee? If you would never tell a friend "let's leverage our core competencies," do not write it.

**Myth: clear means short.** Clear means one idea, well aimed. Sometimes that takes more words, not fewer. The goal is a staircase, not a smaller pile.

## How to use this

Take the last real message you sent: a text, an email, a Slack note. Then run this drill.

1. **Find your worst sentence.** Pick the longest or vaguest one. That is your patient.
2. **Split it.** Break it into one-idea sentences so each does a single job.
3. **Make it active.** Rewrite as "who does what," with the doer and action up front.
4. **Swap abstract for concrete.** Replace every vague word with a number, a name, or a date.
5. **Run the "so what?" pass.** After each sentence, ask why the reader should care. Cut or upgrade any sentence with no answer.
6. **Read it aloud.** Keep the version a friend would actually say out loud.

Here is the whole method on one ugly sentence:

**Before:** "It is felt by the team that there are a number of issues with the current onboarding flow which may potentially be impacting the overall user experience in a negative way, and it is suggested that some improvements could be explored at some point."

**After:** "New users quit during sign-up. We think the form is too long: it asks for 11 fields. I want to cut it to 4 this week and watch whether sign-ups rise."

One idea per sentence, active voice, concrete numbers, no filler, and a clear "so what." Same meaning, suddenly usable.

## Conclusion

If you remember one thing, remember this: most "I can't explain it" moments are not a thinking failure. They are an unwritten sentence. Force the thought into one clean line, say who does what, swap the blur for something you could photograph, and the fog usually clears.

The deeper trick is that writing a clear sentence often *reveals* [you did not have a clear thought yet](/blog/clear-thinking-and-expression/04-clear-writing-is-clear-thinking), and that is a gift, not a failure. The empty "because" is a map to the one fact you still need to go find. Which raises the next question worth chasing: once your sentences are clear, how do you [order them so a whole argument pulls a reader along](/blog/clear-thinking-and-expression/06-structure-frameworks-pyramid-principle-scqa-prep), step by step, without ever losing them?
