**This document helps you figure out exactly which type of print shop owner to focus on first — and how to explain your product so they immediately understand it and want to buy. Trying to sell to everyone produces a message so vague that nobody buys. Picking one clear type of customer, and framing your product the right way for them, is what makes your website copy, ads, and sales pitch actually work.**

**The main parts explained simply:**

- **Who to focus on first ("ICP")** — Instead of targeting "all print businesses," you choose the one type of customer most likely to buy quickly, stay long, and recommend you to others. For Print-Flow-360 that is: small independent print or sign shops with 1–30 staff, run by a non-technical owner who currently takes orders by phone, email, and walk-in.

- **Your bullseye customer — "Maria"** — She owns a small local print or sign shop. She quotes jobs by phone, receives wrong-sized files from customers, rebuilds the same artwork repeatedly, and loses younger customers to online giants. She wants customers to order, design, and pay online without needing her help every time.

- **Scoring which customers are worth chasing** — The document ranks different types of print businesses (local shops, franchise printers, trade printers, apparel decorators, etc.) by how urgent their pain is, how willing they are to pay, and how well the product fits today. Small local print and sign shops score highest by a clear margin.

- **Positioning — how to frame your product** — "Positioning" means choosing the right comparison so people instantly understand what you are. The key decision: describe Print-Flow-360 as an "online storefront and design studio for print shops" — NOT as "print management software." That one framing change turns your strengths into obvious strengths instead of making your weak spots look like deal-breakers.

- **Who to say no to** — Solo Etsy sellers who need a fulfilment network, large commercial printers who need heavy production software, and anyone who demands automated file-checking as a must-have. These customers will never close or will leave quickly.

- **Cheap tests before spending money** — Interview ten shop owners about their current problems (do not pitch). Show your headline to five people for five seconds and ask what it said. Set up two simple landing pages with different angles and see which one gets more sign-ups. Do all of this before running ads or redesigning the site.

**What to do with this:** Pick the small local print and sign shop as the single focus for now — do not split attention across multiple customer types yet. Make sure everyone on the team describes the product as an "online storefront and design studio for print shops," not as print management software. Run the free tests above before spending any money on advertising or a website redesign.
