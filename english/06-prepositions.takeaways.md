**This document is about the small connecting words called prepositions — words like "in," "on," "at," "to," and "for." These tiny words cause big mistakes because there is often no logic to them; you just have to learn which word goes with which verb. This guide gives you simple rules and a cheat table so you can stop guessing.**

**The main parts explained simply:**

- **The zoom rule (in / on / at)** — Think of it as going from big to small. Use "in" for large containers or long time periods (in the repo, in June). Use "on" for surfaces or specific days (on the branch, on Monday). Use "at" for exact points (at port 4000, at 9am). Each level has its own word.

- **Fixed pairs (collocations)** — Many verbs always go with one specific preposition. "Depend on," "based on," "responsible for," "good at," "interested in" — these never change. You cannot work them out from logic; you just memorize the whole pair as one unit.

- **Direction vs. location** — When something is moving somewhere, use "to" (deploy to production, push to remote, reply to a comment). When something is already sitting inside a place, use "in" or "on" (in the repo, on the branch).

- **Common mistakes table** — The document shows the exact errors that come up most often in daily developer writing — things like "depend to," "based of," "comment in my PR," and "listens in port" — and shows the correct version with a short reason.

- **Daily writing examples** — The guide shows how to apply the rules in real situations: commit messages, PR titles, Slack standups, code comments, and bug reports.

- **Drills with answers** — There are 12 practice sentences to fix, with a full answer key. Each answer also explains the other mistake in the sentence (spelling, capital letters, punctuation).

**What to do with this:**

Bookmark the cheat table and check it whenever you write a verb you are unsure about. When you learn a new verb, always learn it together with its preposition as one pair — never the verb alone.
