**This document answers one question: how do you find your first paying customers for a print-shop software business, without wasting money? It says pick just one or two ways to reach people, do them well, and ignore the rest until those two are working.**

**The main parts explained simply:**

- **Don't try everything at once** — Spreading your effort across many channels (ads, social, email, events) means none of them get enough attention to work. The research says one or two channels usually drive almost all early growth. Concentrate there first.

- **Bullseye Framework** — A step-by-step method for choosing which channels to focus on. You list all the ways to find customers, quickly test the most promising two or three, then pour everything into the one that works best. You repeat this as the business grows.

- **Founder-led outreach (the #1 bet)** — The founder personally finds 100–200 print shops, records a short 60-second video showing the shop's own logo already live on a working storefront, and sends it with a personal note: "I built you a demo store — want the keys?" This works because print shops can immediately see what they're getting, and the cost is just time, not money. Do this by hand, one shop at a time. Do not use mass-email blasts — they don't work here.

- **Print communities (tied for #1)** — Print shop owners already hang out in a handful of online groups (a subreddit, a Facebook group for screen printers). Join two of these, be genuinely helpful for weeks before mentioning your product, and earn trust. The same people you're emailing are in these groups — this reinforces the outreach.

- **SEO and a free design tool (secondary)** — Build a small number of web pages targeting searches like "OnPrintShop alternatives" or "online ordering software for print shops." Also publish your design studio as a free tool anyone can use. This brings in customers slowly but keeps working for years. Takes 9–12 months to show results, so it runs alongside the faster channels, not instead of them.

- **Reseller partnerships (later)** — Equipment dealers who already sell to print shops can bundle or recommend your software. High potential, but you need existing happy customers before any dealer will take you seriously. Start this after you have 10–20 stores.

- **Google Ads (skip for now)** — Too expensive for a low-priced niche product. The number of people searching for this type of software is small, the clicks cost a lot, and the math doesn't work at this stage.

- **Anti-patterns (mistakes to avoid)** — Don't automate the outreach emails, don't chase big brand-name equipment partners before you have customers, don't try to rank for popular terms like "print on demand" (huge brands own those), and don't give up on SEO at three months just because it hasn't produced sales yet.

**What to do with this:** Start by hand-picking 100–200 local print shops and sending each a short personalised video of their own branded storefront. At the same time, join two print-owner communities online and become a helpful regular. Write down every question and objection you hear — that language becomes your website copy and your SEO pages.
