**This document teaches the small punctuation marks and capital letters that make written English look polished and professional. Most non-native writers skip these without realising it — but they are what separate a message that looks rushed from one that looks confident and senior.**

**The main parts explained simply:**

- **Always write "I" as a capital** — The word for yourself is always a big letter "I", never a small "i". This one habit alone makes a huge difference to how your messages read.
- **Start every sentence with a capital letter** — The very first word of each sentence gets a big letter. If you forget this, sentences blur together and become hard to follow.
- **End every sentence with a mark** — Use a full stop (.) to finish a statement, a question mark (?) to finish a question, and an exclamation mark (!) only rarely for strong emphasis. Without these, the reader cannot tell where one idea stops and the next begins.
- **Capitalize product and tool names** — Names like GitHub, PostgreSQL, Slack, and Stripe have their own official spelling. Copy it exactly — never write them all in lowercase.
- **Commas give your sentences breathing room** — Use a comma to separate items in a list, after an opening phrase, and before words like "but", "and", or "so" that join two full sentences.
- **Colon vs full stop** — A colon (:) introduces a list or explanation. When in doubt, a full stop is always safe.
- **Backticks for code and commands** — Wrap any command, file name, or technical term in backtick marks so readers know it is code, not a spelling mistake.
- **Daily writing: commits, PRs, Slack, comments** — The document gives specific rules for each: commit titles start with a capital; PR descriptions use full sentences; Slack standups still need capital "I"; code comments are sentences too.
- **Practice drills with answers** — Twelve short exercises let you fix real mistakes and check your work immediately.

**What to do with this:**

Pick two habits to fix first: always write a capital "I" and always end each sentence with a full stop or question mark. These two changes alone will make your messages read as far more careful and professional.
