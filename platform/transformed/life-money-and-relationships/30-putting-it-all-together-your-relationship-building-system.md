Most people network in panic. They suddenly need a job, a customer, or an introduction, and only then do they scramble to "reach out" to people they ignored for years. It almost never works, and it always feels awful.

There is a calmer way. Instead of frantic bursts, you build a light routine you keep for years, the way you'd water a garden. That routine is what this article gives you: a simple, repeatable system you can run for the rest of your life without burning out or feeling fake.

## Why this matters

A great network is not built by trying harder. It is built by a small set of habits that happen on a schedule, so you stop relying on willpower or memory.

The payoff compounds quietly. The friend you check in with today becomes the person who [thinks of you for an opportunity](/blog/life-money-and-relationships/27-how-opportunities-hires-advice-really-flow) three years from now. The colleague you helped without keeping score becomes the one who vouches for you when it counts. None of it feels urgent on any single day, which is exactly why a **system** beats motivation. Motivation fades by Wednesday. A routine on your calendar does not.

Here is the core idea, and everything else hangs off it: **consistency beats intensity, every single time.**

## Start with two anchors: capacity and cadence

Before any routine, two realities shape everything.

First, **capacity**: you cannot be close to everyone. Second, **cadence**: different people need different amounts of contact. Get these two right and the routine almost writes itself.

### Capacity: your relationship budget

Anthropologist **Robin Dunbar** found that humans can sustain only about **150 stable relationships**, a figure now known as Dunbar's number. Treat 150 as a rough order of magnitude rather than an exact law; some researchers dispute the precise figure. What matters is the shape: those relationships sit in nested layers, each roughly three times bigger than the one inside it, and each costing about three times less time and emotional energy.

- **~5** people in your inner circle, the ones you'd call in a crisis.
- **~15** close friends, your "sympathy group."
- **~50** good friends you'd invite to a big party.
- **~150** meaningful relationships you actively maintain.
- **~500 and ~1,500** acquaintances and faces you can name. This is a *recognition* list, not a relationship list.

**Think of concentric ripples in a pond.** The center is close and intense. Each ring outward is calmer and wider. You cannot make the whole pond the center, and trying to is exactly why some people end up genuinely close to no one.

### Cadence: how often to reach out

Match your contact rhythm to the layer. These are rules of thumb, not laws, so bend them to your life:

| Tier | Who | Reach out roughly every... |
|------|-----|----------------------------|
| Inner circle | the ~5 to 15 | weekly to monthly |
| Good friends and warm contacts | the ~50 | monthly to quarterly |
| Wider network | the ~150+ | twice a year |

A clean way to remember this is investor **Judy Robinett's "5 + 50 + 100" rule** from her book *How to Be a Power Connector*: curate a **Top 5**, a **Key 50**, and a **Vital 100**, each with its own deliberate rhythm. Robinett herself touches the Top 5 daily, the Key 50 weekly, and the Vital 100 monthly.

The big mistake here is trying to keep up with everyone at the same intensity. That leads straight to burnout, then to abandoning the whole effort. Accept that your 150th contact gets a twice-a-year hello, and that is completely fine.

## Set two tiny goals, not "build my network"

"Build my network" is too vague to act on. Replace it with two concrete goals.

1. **Who to nurture (deepen).** List the 5 to 15 people who matter most for your life *and* your work right now. These get your best attention.
2. **Who to meet (reach).** Pick *one* recurring community to show up in: a class, a club, a co-working space, a niche online group. Aim for **two or three real conversations** per gathering, not a stack of business cards.

That second goal works because recurring contact creates familiarity, and familiarity creates liking. Showing up to the same place repeatedly does more for you than attending ten different events once each.

One more list is worth keeping: a short **"dormant ties to revive"** list of [people you've genuinely lost touch with](/blog/life-money-and-relationships/18-mapping-your-network-strong-weak-dormant-ties). Reconnecting with them is the single highest-return, lowest-effort move most people ignore. The trust is already there, and they carry fresh information from circles you've drifted away from.

## The personal CRM that makes it sustainable

A **personal CRM** sounds corporate, but it is simple. CRM stands for Customer Relationship Manager, a term borrowed from sales. Here it just means a place that does three jobs:

1. **Remembers details** about people.
2. **Reminds you** to reach out on schedule.
3. **Logs** your last conversation so you can pick up naturally.

It can be an app like Dex, Clay, Covve, or Monica. It can be a Notion or Airtable template. It can be a plain spreadsheet. And this matters: **a spreadsheet you actually open beats the fanciest tool you never touch.**

Start with these columns: *Name*, *Tier*, *Last contacted*, *Next contact*, *How we met*, and *Notes*. In Notes, capture the *human* things, not job facts. Their dog's surgery, the trip they mentioned, their kid's recital. Those are the details that make the next conversation feel like a friendship and not a transaction.

If even a spreadsheet feels like too much, use the low-tech version: each week, skim your email "Sent" folder and tag everyone you contacted into the right tier. That alone turns your memory into a system.

One caution: do not treat the CRM like a sales pipeline of "targets to convert." Frame it as *remembering you care*, not tracking leads, and be respectful and private with whatever you note about people.

## Your weekly and monthly routine

This is the heart of the whole thing. Block a recurring **"relationship hour"** on your calendar and run this.

**Weekly (about 30 to 60 minutes):**

- **Do one to three five-minute favors.** A *five-minute favor*, a term coined by super-connector **Adam Rifkin** and popularized by psychologist Adam Grant in *Give and Take*, is anything that takes five minutes or less and helps someone a lot: a warm introduction, quick feedback on a draft, a relevant article, a genuine endorsement, amplifying someone's launch.
- **Reconnect with one dormant tie, with no ask.** Lead with warmth or a specific memory, don't over-apologize for the gap, and offer an easy out ("no need to reply").
- **Update your notes** for anyone you spoke with this week.

**Monthly:**

- Review your **Key 50**: who have I not touched lately? Send a low-pressure, no-ask check-in.

**Quarterly:**

- Sweep the **Vital 100 and wider 150**: a "saw this and thought of you," a congrats on their news, a holiday note.

Notice the recurring phrase: *no ask*. Make most of your contact carry no request at all. A relationship where every message is a favor request becomes transactional fast, and people learn to dread your name. And here is a reassuring research finding worth holding onto: people are reliably *happier* to hear from you out of the blue than you fear they'll be.

## Common misconceptions

A few beliefs quietly sabotage people. Here is the myth versus the reality.

- **Myth: more connections means a stronger network.** Reality: a thousand LinkedIn connections is a recognition list, not a web of trust. Depth beats breadth.
- **Myth: networking is something you do when you need something.** Reality: the worst time to start is when you're job-hunting or fundraising. By then it reads as desperate. You build before you need it.
- **Myth: reaching out after a long silence is awkward and unwelcome.** Reality: dormant ties are usually glad to hear from you, and they're among the most valuable contacts you have.
- **Myth: keeping notes on people is calculating or fake.** Reality: remembering that someone's mother was ill is the opposite of fake. Your unaided memory simply can't hold 150 lives. The notes help you care, not pretend to.

## Metrics that aren't gross

Most "networking metrics" are vanity numbers: follower counts, connection counts, cards collected. They measure recognition, not trust, and chasing them quietly nudges you toward transactional behavior. Track *process* and *depth* instead.

| Vanity metric (avoid) | Healthy metric (track) |
|-----------------------|------------------------|
| Total LinkedIn connections | Five-minute favors given this month |
| Cards collected at events | Two or three real conversations per gathering |
| "Reach" of your posts | Dormant ties reconnected this quarter |
| People who owe you a favor | Did everyone in my Key 50 get a no-ask touch? |

**Vanity metrics are like weighing the seed packets you bought. Healthy metrics check whether you actually watered the garden.** Only one of them grows anything.

## Fold money and relationships into one life review

If you also run a [money review each week](/blog/life-money-and-relationships/02-budgeting-cash-flow-knowing-where-money-goes) (income, spending, saving, goals), you can combine both into a single **life operating system**: one recurring review that keeps the two things that compound most in your life on track.

The parallel is real. Both reward small, regular deposits. Both decay silently if neglected. Both pay off most when you started long before you needed them.

A simple version looks like this. On Sunday evening, you spend 30 minutes on money (check balances, log spending, plan the week) and 30 minutes on people (send two introductions, message one old colleague just to say hi, update your CRM). Neither feels urgent on any given Sunday, and that is precisely why a system, not motivation, is what keeps it running for ten years straight.

## How to use this: a five-step setup

You can stand up the whole system in an afternoon.

1. **List your tiers.** Write down your inner ~5 to 15, your Key 50, and a rough Vital 100. Don't overthink it; you'll refine over time.
2. **Build the simplest CRM you'll actually use.** A spreadsheet with six columns is plenty. Add a "dormant ties to revive" tab.
3. **Pick one recurring community** to show up in, and commit to two or three real conversations each time.
4. **Block a weekly "relationship hour"** on your calendar as a repeating event. Treat it like a meeting you can't skip.
5. **Run the loop:** one to three five-minute favors, one dormant reconnection with no ask, and a notes update. Every week. That's it.

Keep these five principles above your desk, because if you forget everything else, these carry you:

- **Give first, always.** Be the *otherish* giver, generous *and* protective of your own time. [Give without keeping score](/blog/life-money-and-relationships/24-giving-value-reciprocity-without-keeping-score) and let returns be diffuse and delayed.
- **Build before you need it.** Plant early, long before the job hunt or the launch.
- **Widen your luck surface area.** Entrepreneur Jason Roberts' model is *Luck = Doing × Telling*. Do real work *and* let people know about it. Because they multiply, if either is zero, your luck is zero.
- **Match effort to capacity.** Use the Dunbar layers. You physically cannot give everyone the same intensity.
- **Be a connector.** Introducing two people who should know each other is the highest-leverage social act there is. It creates value you didn't have to generate yourself, and it quietly makes you the hub.

## Conclusion

If you take one thing from all of this, take this: **you don't build relationships by trying harder; you build them with a light routine you keep for years.** Five-minute favors are [compound interest](/blog/life-money-and-relationships/06-compounding-the-time-value-of-money) for human connection, tiny regular deposits that grow into a fortune of trust.

The deepest reason this works is the same reason it's so easy to neglect: relationships, like money and health, move on a slow clock. The deposit you make today is almost invisible. The withdrawal you'll someday need is enormous. The people who thrive are simply the ones who started watering the garden before the dry season arrived.

So here's the question worth sitting with next: of the people who matter most to you, who have you not spoken to in over a year, and what would it cost you to send them a warm, no-strings hello this week? That single message is often where a whole new chapter quietly begins.
