# How to Pick the Right GTM Motion for Your Price Point

Here is a truth that quietly kills startups: the way you sell is not a matter of taste. It is arithmetic. If your product sells for $40 a month and you hire a salesperson to close those deals, you will lose money on every single customer - no matter how good your pitch is.

The good news is that the math runs both ways. Once you know your price, the math tells you exactly which selling motion you are allowed to run. This guide shows you how to read that math, so you stop fighting your own numbers and start working with them.

## Why this matters

Most go-to-market mistakes are not bad execution. They are a mismatch between **how much a customer is worth** and **how much effort you spend winning them**.

Spend salesperson money to land tiny accounts, and your costs swamp your revenue. Try to self-serve a complex six-figure deal, and the buyer walks because nobody answered their questions. Either way, the engine grinds.

Getting this right early decides three expensive things:

- **Whether you hire salespeople at all** - and when.
- **Where your marketing budget goes** - ads, content, or pure product.
- **Whether your [unit economics](/blog/how-to-make-money/13-unit-economics-does-each-sale-make-money) ever close** - the moment a customer earns back what they cost.

Pick the wrong motion and you can do everything else well and still run out of money. Pick the right one and a small team can punch far above its weight.

## The core idea: match the motion to the animal

The cleanest way to think about this comes from investor Christoph Janz and his essay **"Five Ways to Build a $100 Million Business."** He frames the whole question as a hunting metaphor.

To reach $100 million in revenue, you can hunt different sizes of "animal," where the size is the **annual revenue per customer**:

| Animal | Revenue per customer / year | Customers needed | Motion it demands |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Elephants** | $100,000+ | ~1,000 | Field sales, named accounts, long cycles |
| **Deer** | $10,000+ | ~10,000 | Inside sales, sales-assisted |
| **Rabbits** | $1,000+ | ~100,000 | Self-serve, low-touch, marketing-driven |
| **Mice** | $100+ | ~1,000,000 | Pure self-serve, viral, freemium |
| **Flies** | $10+ | ~10,000,000 | Massive-scale consumer, ad-funded |

The lesson is **not** "hunt the biggest animal." It is this: you can absolutely build a great business hunting Rabbits *or* Deer - but you must commit your machinery to **one**. You cannot catch both with the same apparatus.

Janz names the classic failure mode perfectly: **hunting Deer with Elephant-sized effort.** That is spending expensive field-sales energy to land mid-sized logos. The hunt costs more than the kill is worth, and the math never closes.

### An everyday analogy

Think of fishing. To catch one giant tuna, you take a boat, a crew, and a full day - and it is worth it, because one tuna is a feast. To catch a thousand sardines, you cast a wide net once. Nobody hires a tuna crew to catch sardines one at a time. That is the whole insight, dressed in a wetsuit.

## The hard floor: when can you afford a human?

Self-serve has no people in the loop, so its cost to win a customer trends toward zero. The moment you add a human, you add a salary - and that salary has to be paid back by the deals that person closes.

So how big does a deal need to be before a salesperson earns their keep? The venture firm **a16z** offers a widely used rule of thumb in its writing on adding top-down sales:

> You need roughly **$5,000 in annual revenue per account** before an inside-sales team can even pay for itself.

Below that line, a human-touch motion loses money on every deal, and self-serve is the only thing the math permits. Above it, a rep starts to make sense.

To feel why, look at what a human actually costs in 2025–2026:

- A **fully-loaded sales development rep (SDR)** - the person who books meetings - runs about **$100K–$170K a year** once you add benefits, tools, management, recruiting, and ramp-up time. That is roughly 1.7 to 2.5 times their base salary.
- A **mid-market account executive (AE)** - the person who closes - is even pricier once loaded, and typically needs to generate around **$800K–$1M in new revenue per year** just to be worth the seat.

Now do the division. If your product earns $1,500 a year per customer, a single AE would need to close *hundreds* of accounts every year to cover their own cost - at the careful, consultative touch a real sale requires. It cannot be done. Even the cheaper SDR cannot be repaid by sub-$2,000 customers.

The conclusion is blunt and freeing: **humans are only affordable on your highest tier.** Everywhere else, the product has to do the selling.

## A worked example: a web-to-print SaaS

Abstract rules are easy to nod along with and hard to apply. So here is the framework run end to end on a real type of business - software that lets print shops build an online store, take orders, and run jobs.

This vertical has **five economically distinct buyers**, not the three a first glance suggests. Two of them quietly change the motion.

| Segment | Who they are | Rough price / year | Animal | Motion |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Solo shop** | One owner, simple needs, often no website | ~$350–600 | Rabbit | Pure self-serve |
| **Growing shop** | 2–10 staff, real order volume, wants a branded store | ~$950–1,800 | Rabbit (upper) | Self-serve, help only on request |
| **Multi-location / B2B** | Pay-on-account, departments, multiple stores | ~$2,400–4,800 | Deer | Founder-led sales-assist |
| **Franchise / multi-unit HQ** | Corporate procurement buying for many units | $15,000+ | Deer→Elephant | The one true committee sale |
| **Trade / wholesale printer** | Resells print capacity to other shops | n/a | - | A channel, not a customer |

A few things fall out of this table immediately.

### Small shops are Rabbits, so they must self-serve

Solo and growing shops are the bulk of the market and sit far below the $5,000 human-sales floor. There is no version of the math where a person profitably onboards a $400-a-year customer. So the product, the demo data, and a short go-live checklist have to do **all** the onboarding work. The North Star becomes a self-serve event by design: *store live and first order within seven days.*

### Mid-sized accounts are Deer - but the founder sells, not a hire

The multi-location tier is the first one that approaches the $5,000 line. It deserves a human - but **the founder, not a hired rep.** When a self-serve signup raises its hand ("we need pay-on-account, we have five locations"), that signal pulls the founder in to close. You get the benefit of human touch without the unaffordable salary. You do **not** hire an SDR or AE at $2,400 a year per account.

### The franchise HQ is the one real "big game" deal

Corporate franchise procurement - buying software for hundreds of units - is the only deal here worth $15,000+ and the only one with a true [buying committee](/blog/sales-customer-development/08-sales-discovery-frameworks-spin-bant-meddic-and-friends) (procurement, marketing, operations, IT). It is a Deer edging toward Elephant: long cycle, custom pricing, reference-dependent. It is also **deferred.** You cannot win it without reference customers and a track record, so it comes *after* self-serve has produced proof, never before.

### Some "buyers" are actually channels

Trade printers do not buy a seat - they resell capacity to other shops. That makes them a **[distribution channel](/blog/how-to-make-money/15-distribution-beats-product)**, not a customer. Treating a channel partner like a self-serve user is a category error that wastes months. Naming this correctly is half the battle.

## Common misconceptions

A few myths cause more damage than any competitor.

- **"More salespeople always means more growth."** Only above the affordability floor. Below it, every hire deepens the hole. Salespeople are an amplifier, not an engine - they multiply working economics and multiply broken ones just as fast.
- **"A hybrid motion means selling every customer two ways."** No. A legitimate hybrid is **cleanly segmented**: self-serve for small accounts, a human for large ones. Layering a salesperson on top of a self-serve customer who would have converted anyway is pure waste - the Deer-with-Elephant-effort trap.
- **"Paid search is just how SaaS gets customers."** In a thin niche with low prices and expensive clicks, your cost to acquire a customer can exceed their entire first-year revenue. Ads are not a law of nature; they are one option that the math frequently vetoes.
- **"Free trials should require a credit card to filter for serious buyers."** For an unknown brand, usually the opposite. Skipping the card brings in three to four times more signups. A no-card trial converts a smaller *share* but produces far more paying customers - and that volume is the fuel a self-serve motion runs on. Card-required is the right call only once you are an established brand with word of mouth doing the filtering for you.

## How to use this

Walk these steps in order. Each one narrows your options until the right motion is the only one left standing.

1. **Write down your real annual revenue per customer**, by segment. Not your dream price - the one customers actually pay. If you do not have data yet, mark it as a placeholder and revisit once you do.
2. **Map each segment to an animal.** Under ~$2,000 a year is firmly Rabbit territory. Around $5,000+ opens the door to a human. Six figures is Elephant country.
3. **Apply the $5,000 floor.** Any segment below it must be won by self-serve. No exceptions, no "but our reps are really good."
4. **For segments above the floor, start [founder-led](/blog/sales-customer-development/15-building-the-muscle-process-pipeline-habit).** Let the founder sell before you hire. Hire your first rep only when repeatable volume clearly clears the economics - not when it feels exciting.
5. **Define the handoff signal.** Decide the exact trigger ("multi-location," "pay-on-account requested") that promotes a self-serve user into a human-assisted deal. Make it a flag in the product, not a vibe.
6. **Pick the no-card trial if you are unknown.** Maximize top-of-funnel volume to feed the Rabbit motion.
7. **Make the product absorb the hard parts.** Build self-serve import and setup tools so switching and onboarding are features, not human services. Reserve human help for tiers that can pay for it.
8. **Defer channels and big committee deals** until you have ten to twenty reference customers. They take 6–18 months and depend on proof you do not have on day one.
9. **Re-run this map quarterly** with real numbers. As your prices rise, segments may cross the $5,000 line and unlock motions you could not afford before.

## A risk worth naming out loud

There is one assumption that can quietly invalidate a self-serve plan: **maybe the product is simply too complex to set up alone.**

A print storefront needs a product catalog, print options, a pricing engine, a design tool, and a live payment gateway. If a non-technical owner genuinely cannot get live in one sitting, you are forced into hand-holding for *everyone* - which breaks the cheap economics that made self-serve work in the first place.

The fix is not to argue about it. It is to **test it cheaply**: put ten to twenty real, non-technical owners through unaided setup and measure how many reach the North Star without help. If they sail through, scale your self-serve spend with confidence. If they stall, you have just learned - for almost no cost - that you need to [raise prices](/blog/how-to-make-money/14-pricing-value-capture) and move upmarket *before* you pour money into a motion that cannot hold. Whatever your product, find your version of this test and run it before you scale.

## Conclusion

If you remember one thing, make it this: **your price point picks your motion, not the other way around.** Decide what a customer is worth, and the math will tell you whether a human is allowed in the room.

The teams that win are not the ones with the cleverest sales pitch. They are the ones honest enough to let the arithmetic choose - to hunt Rabbits with a net and Deer with a rifle, and never confuse the two.

So here is the question that comes next, the one that quietly decides whether your chosen motion actually fires: once you know *how* you will sell, **which channels feed that motion**, and which ones burn cash pretending to? That is where the real fun begins.
