The cast (our running example)

To keep every abstract point concrete, the whole summary uses one running story:

  • You = the founder of Print-Flow-360 (a "Shopify for print shops").
  • Tony = owner of "Tony's Tees," a one-person screen-print shop in Ohio. Takes orders by phone, text, and sticky notes. No real website. Tony is your perfect customer.
  • Maria = a different shop owner who already uses a competitor and is shopping to switch.
  • Dave = a regional printing-equipment dealer who already sells to 200 shops like Tony's.

For every channel, the only question that matters is: "Does this reach Tony — cheaply, in real numbers, and is it actually Tony we're reaching?"

The whole paper in one breath

Your buyer is findable and your product is visual, so win customers the cheap, direct way: personally hand-build demo stores and help shop owners in their own forums (fast money + learning), while slowly building SEO pages and a free design tool that compound for free — and never waste money on ads, automation, or scaling before you've earned proof.

Section-by-section summary

Each section below: a short explanation + a one-line summary in bold. Read just the bold lines for the 60-second version.

TL;DR / Intro

The paper answers one question: which 1–2 ways should we get customers? Answer: two cheap, compounding channels — founder outreach + communities (now), and BOFU SEO + a free tool (slow but lasting). Skip paid ads and affiliates for now.

📌 Pick two near-free channels that match your buyer; don't spray across all of them.

§0 — The core thesis ("why don't spray")

Most early startups die trying every channel at once. Three facts make Print-Flow-360's choice obvious:

  1. Enumerable buyer — you can list every Tony from Google Maps, Yelp, directories (~50,000 US print shops, ~54% single-owner). You don't need reach; you need direct outreach into a list you already have.
  2. Visual product — Tony's own logo live on a working store is an instant "wow" in a 60-second video, but impossible to convey in text. That makes personal, hand-made outreach unusually powerful.
  3. Small budget — slow, expensive, "rented" channels (paid ads) are structurally wrong; cheap, owned, compounding channels are right.
📌 Findable buyer + visual product + small budget = use direct, cheap channels.

BET 1 — Founder outreach fused with 2 communities

This is one motion, not two channels — the same Tonys are reachable both one-by-one and where they gather, so every hour builds both.

  • Story: You find Tony's Tees on Google Maps, spend 15 minutes building a demo with his logo and colors, record a 60-second Loom: "I built you a store — want the keys?" Tony's never seen his brand online; he replies. Meanwhile you're a helpful regular in his Facebook group, so he already trusts you.
  • Cost: Near-zero (your time, ~2–4 weeks part-time for 100–200 shops).
  • Speed: First demos in 1–2 weeks; first paying shops in 3–6 weeks.
  • Bonus (the real magic): It's a learning engine — every objection and "I already use X" becomes your website copy, onboarding, and SEO.
  • Guardrail: Never automate it into cold email — SaaS cold email replies at only 2–4% (worst vertical), and at low ACV the math breaks.
  • Kill-criterion: if manual can't beat ~8–10% positive replies, automation (which is worse) won't save it.
📌 Hand-build demos and earn trust in forums — fast, cheap, and never automated.

BET 2 — BOFU SEO + free tool (built in parallel)

Build a few one-time assets that pull free Google traffic for years:

  • 5–10 comparison pages ("InkSoft alternatives," "DecoNetwork vs Print-Flow-360"). Story: Maria already uses a competitor, Googles "InkSoft alternatives," lands on your page, signs up — while you were busy messaging Tony.
  • A free standalone design tool (free business-card / flyer / t-shirt maker) built on the design studio you already own. It's both a traffic magnet (huge search volume) and a live demo (people experience your product while using it). This is the highest-leverage move.
  • Reality: Slow — 9–12 months to rank. That's why it's SECONDARY, paired with fast outreach. But it's cheap, durable, on-brand for a visual product, and fed by what BET 1 teaches you.
📌 Build pages + a free tool that pull free Google traffic forever — slow but compounding.

§2 — The Bullseye Framework (the selection method)

How the bets were chosen. A dartboard with three rings, worked outside-in:

  • Outer ring — "what's possible": brainstorm an idea for all 19 channels (kills founder bias — most founders only consider the 2–3 they like).
  • Middle ring — "what's probable": cheaply test the top ~3 in parallel, judging each on CAC / Volume / Fit. Budget: "at most $1,000 and a month."
  • Inner ring — "what's working": pour everything into the single winner until it plateaus, then repeat the loop.
  • Companion tools: ICE scoring (Impact × Confidence × Ease, 1–10) to rank ideas objectively; the 50% rule (success is ~50% product, ~50% traction).
  • Key caveat: Bullseye picks where to play, not how to win. "Don't mistake completing the diagram for doing the work." It also allows 2 complementary channels — which is why BET 1 + BET 2 run together.
📌 Brainstorm wide, test the top 3 cheaply, double down on the one winner, repeat.

§3 — Channel-by-channel briefs (the evidence)

Each channel judged on Fit / Cost / Time / Tactics / Proof:

  • 3.1 Founder outreach (✅ fit 8): message Tony directly with his pre-built demo. Best for your first ~50 customers; near-zero cost; fast. SaaS cold email is the worst-replying vertical (2–4%) — that's why you stay manual.
  • 3.2 Communities (✅ fit 7): earn Tony's trust in his group; ~95/5 helpful-to-promo ratio; own 2 communities, not 10 (r/SCREENPRINTING + the pro-only "Screen Printing Q&A" FB group); ~6–8 week ramp before product mentions are welcome.
  • 3.3 SEO (🟡 fit 6): Maria finds you on Google while you sleep. Great for ready-to-switch buyers, but 9–12 months slow. Win narrow terms; never chase "print on demand" (owned by Printful/Shopify). BOFU pages convert ~25× better than blog posts.
  • 3.4 Partnerships (🕓 fit 7): Dave the dealer could resell you to 200 Tonys (the OnPrintShop × Ricoh model = 2,000+ shops). Highest ceiling, but needs 10–20 reference stores first. Do now: a referral program + a "Powered by Print-Flow-360" footer.
  • 3.5 Social (🕓 fit 6): strong content fit, wrong audience — 200k views but few Tonys. Only YouTube reaches owners (search-driven). Later, not now.
  • 3.6 Paid ads (❌ fit 5): perfect intent but thin volume + $8–14 CPCs + low ACV = it costs more to win Tony than Tony pays you. Revisit much later as a tiny brand-term layer only.
📌 Outreach + communities reach Tony directly now; the rest are slow, wrong-crowd, or lose money.

§4 — The 6–12 week plan (the calendar)

Turns the bets into a schedule:

  • Week 0: set kill-criteria + cheap tracking (UTM links, per-community codes).
  • Week 1: build the shop list free (Google Maps/Yelp/directories); join the 2 communities.
  • Weeks 1–4: run outreach manually, one Tony at a time; log every objection (the deliverable that feeds BET 2).
  • Weeks 1–8: help in communities with discipline; request a mod-approved AMA around week 4–6; recruit 3–5 free design partners.
  • Weeks 2–6: build BET 2's assets — comparison pages + the free design tool + JTBD guides using real objection language.
  • Weeks 4–6: first Bullseye read — is BET 1 clearing 8–10% replies? Judge BET 2 on rankings/indexing, not revenue yet.
  • Weeks 6–12: double down on the winner; pre-test the next channel (partnerships) before the current one plateaus.
📌 A week-by-week calendar: set goals, run both bets in parallel, read results at week 4–6.

§5 — Anti-patterns (the 8 don'ts)

  1. Don't run Google Ads as your "fast" channel — thin volume + high CPC + low ACV burns budget for the least durable result.
  2. Don't automate outreach into cold email — kills the personal touch that made it work; SaaS replies at 2–4%; legal risk (CAN-SPAM/GDPR).
  3. Don't launch a pure affiliate program early — 18–24 mo for B2B; use a "Powered by" footer + your own happy customers instead.
  4. Don't pitch big vendors (Ricoh/Canon/HP) before traction — earn 10–20 reference stores first, then approach ONE regional dealer.
  5. Don't chase saturated SEO terms ("print on demand") — owned by giants; stay on narrow BOFU comparison/category terms.
  6. Don't spray across every social platform — that's the exact spray-and-pray Bullseye prevents; if social later, YouTube only.
  7. Don't mistake vanity metrics for traction — views/followers ≠ customers; measure real shop-owner demos with UTMs.
  8. Don't quit SEO at 3 months — it ranks at 9–12 months; quitting early wastes the whole investment.

The pattern behind all 8: don't buy speed (1,2), don't scale before proof (3,4), don't chase what only looks good (5,6,7,8).

📌 Don't buy speed, don't scale before proof, don't chase vanity — stay cheap, manual, narrow, patient.
