You have a great product, a tiny team, and roughly nineteen ways to find customers. So you try a little of everything: a few ads, a blog post, a booth at one trade show, some cold emails. Three months later nothing has really moved, and you cannot even say which thing failed.

That scattered, exhausted feeling has a name. It is the most common way small teams die. And the Bullseye Framework exists to cure it.

## Why this matters

Most startups do not fail because they picked the wrong channel. They fail because they never pick at all. They spread a small budget across many channels, or they fixate on the two or three channels the founder personally likes, usually paid ads.

The result is that no single channel ever gets enough attention to actually work. Each one gets starved, shows weak numbers, and gets blamed.

The Bullseye Framework, from Gabriel Weinberg and Justin Mares' book *Traction*, is a discipline for the opposite behavior: **explore widely, test cheaply, then concentrate ruthlessly.** It is less about marketing tricks and more about how to make a focused bet when you cannot afford a wrong one.

And here is the part most people miss. The real lesson is not "do one channel." It is **resource concentration under uncertainty** - a way of thinking that applies to almost every hard call a small team faces, not just acquisition.

## The three rings, explained simply

Picture a dartboard with three rings. You work from the outside in.

### Outer ring: what's possible

Brainstorm at least one concrete, plausible test for **all 19 channels**. Not "would this work?" but "if I had to try this, what exactly would I do?"

The whole point of forcing all nineteen is to fight your own bias. You will be tempted to skip the channels you find boring or unfamiliar. Do not skip them. The channel that ends up carrying your business is often one you would never have chosen on instinct.

The 19 channels are: viral marketing, PR, unconventional PR, paid search, social and display ads, offline ads, SEO, content marketing, email marketing, engineering as marketing (free tools), targeting blogs, business development (partnerships), sales, affiliate programs, existing platforms, trade shows, offline events, speaking engagements, and community building.

### Middle ring: what's probable

Take your three most promising ideas and run **cheap, fast, parallel tests.** Each test answers three questions:

- **Cost** - what does it cost to acquire a customer here?
- **Volume** - how many customers can this channel actually reach?
- **Fit** - are these the customers you want *right now*?

The budget bar from *Traction* is blunt and useful: get a rough idea of a channel's effectiveness with **at most a thousand dollars and a month.** Growth expert Brian Balfour sharpens it into a memorable rule - *run four ads, not forty.* Test enough to read the signal, not so much that you have already committed before you have data.

### Inner ring: what's working

Find the single channel with the best mix of cost, volume, and fit, and pour everything into it until it saturates. Ignore the runners-up while it is working.

This is the hard part, emotionally. You will have a channel showing decent early numbers and it will feel wrong to abandon it. Abandon it anyway. A second channel run at half-effort almost always underperforms your best channel run at full effort.

### Then repeat

When the winner plateaus, run the whole loop again. Weinberg ran it *"six or seven times"* at DuckDuckGo.

This is where most teams quietly fail. Practitioners notice that teams tend to **drop off around the third channel** - they get one channel working, ride it, and then never re-open the outer ring. When that channel saturates, growth stalls and they have no replacement warming up.

## A real example: the print shop software

Abstract frameworks are easy to nod along to and hard to use. So here is a concrete case: a small software company building an online-ordering platform for print shops, with a tiny team and a long list of options.

They used the Bullseye logic not just for channels but for their hardest strategic question - **[who to sell to first](/blog/sales-customer-development/03-customer-development-get-out-of-the-building).**

- **Outer ring:** they listed every plausible customer segment - apparel and screen-print shops, sign makers, promotional-product sellers, large commercial printers, copy shops, photo and packaging specialists.
- **Middle ring:** they scored each on cost, volume, and fit. Big commercial printers had high volume but demanded deep features the product did not have yet (poor fit, high cost to win). Apparel and sign shops gathered in active online communities, felt the pain sharply, and could see the product's value in a 60-second demo.
- **Inner ring:** small apparel, screen-print, and sign shops with no online ordering won. They were the intersection of *reachable*, *enthusiastic*, and *a good fit today.* The big printers became an explicit "later" bet, not a "no."

Notice what the framework did here. It did not invent the answer. It made the team **say the bet out loud and rank it honestly**, which stopped them from chasing the impressive-but-wrong big-printer segment.

## The companion tool: ICE scoring

How do you make the cut from the outer ring to the middle ring without arguing forever? Use **ICE scoring** (from Sean Ellis and the GrowthHackers community).

Score each idea on three dimensions, each from 1 to 10:

- **Impact** - if this works, how much does it move the needle?
- **Confidence** - how sure are you it will work?
- **Ease** - how cheap and fast is it to try?

Multiply the three, then rank. It will not be perfect, but it turns a vague gut argument into a sortable list, and it forces "easy but low-impact" ideas to reveal themselves.

## Common misconceptions

**"You must run exactly one channel forever."** Not quite. *Traction* echoes Peter Thiel's claim that one channel usually dominates at each stage, and that is directionally true. But modern teams soften it: test about three in parallel, then often run **one or two complementary channels** - for example, SEO plus the community where that content naturally gets shared. The deeper rule survives intact: **[concentration beats diffusion](/blog/ten-disciplines/13-what-strategy-really-is-advantage-moats-and-positioning).**

**"Finishing the dartboard means I did the work."** No. Bullseye is channel *discovery*, not channel *execution.* Completing the diagram tells you where to dig. You still have to dig.

**"Cheap tests give me a final answer."** They give you a *signal*, not a verdict. The thousand-dollar test tells you whether a channel is worth a real investment, not whether it will scale. Treat early numbers as a green light to dig deeper, not as proof.

**"The framework is only for marketing channels."** This is the most expensive miss. The core mechanic - *list broadly, test cheaply in parallel, concentrate on the one winner, re-run when it saturates* - works on any decision where a small team faces a tempting menu of options. Which feature to build next. Which customer segment to chase. Whether to hire a sales team. The dartboard is a thinking pattern, not just a marketing tool.

## A worked decision: should you hire a sales team?

Here is the framework applied to a question that has nothing to do with channels: how should customers actually buy your product?

The print software company had four options on the outer ring: self-serve sign-up (product-led growth), founder doing sales by hand, a hired outbound sales team, or selling through resellers.

The deciding test was **[CAC payback](/blog/business-financial-literacy/06-unit-economics-do-you-make-money-on-each-sale)** - how long it takes to earn back the cost of acquiring a customer. Andreessen Horowitz's go-to-market benchmarks put healthy CAC payback for products sold to small businesses at roughly **6 to 12 months**, versus 18 to 24 months for enterprise. That window is the gate: if a sales motion cannot pay itself back inside it, you cannot afford that motion.

At a price of under a thousand dollars per customer per year, the loaded cost of a salesperson simply cannot pay back in time. The math forces a self-serve engine. [Founder-led outreach](/blog/sales-customer-development/13-founder-led-outreach-getting-the-conversations) still has a role - but as a **cold-start primer** to land the first reference customers and learn the objections, not as a scalable sales org.

The lesson generalizes: when a decision has a tempting "hire people and push harder" option, run it through the rings. Often the honest cost-and-fit test rules it out before you waste a year on it.

## How to use this

Here is the framework as a repeatable process you can run this week.

1. **Brainstorm all 19 channels.** One concrete test idea each. No skipping the boring ones. Write the actual experiment, not just the channel name.
2. **Score with ICE.** Rate each idea on Impact, Confidence, and Ease (1 to 10), multiply, and sort. Pick the top three.
3. **Run three cheap tests in parallel.** Cap each at roughly $1,000 and one month. Measure cost per customer, reachable volume, and fit with the customers you want now.
4. **Pick the one winner.** Choose the best mix of cost, volume, and fit. Resist the urge to keep two or three alive at half-effort.
5. **Concentrate until it saturates.** Pour your time and money into the winner. Push it until growth from that channel genuinely flattens.
6. **Set a tripwire, then re-run the loop.** Decide in advance what "saturated" looks like, and start warming up the next ring before the current one runs dry. Re-running quarterly is a reasonable default.

The single most important habit is step 6. Getting one channel working is the milestone everyone celebrates. Re-opening the dartboard before that channel stalls is the discipline that separates teams that keep growing from teams that plateau.

## Conclusion

The Bullseye Framework is not really about darts or channels. It is about resisting the urge to do a little of everything when you can only afford to do one thing well. **Explore widely, test cheaply, then concentrate ruthlessly - and re-run the loop before your winner runs out.**

If that pattern feels familiar, it is because it is the same logic behind a focused product roadmap, a single beachhead market, and a clear value proposition. The next question worth asking is the one *Traction* quietly sets up: once you have found your one channel, how do you know when it has truly saturated - and what is the early signal that tells you to start hunting for the next one before growth ever dips?
