**This document explains how to measure the health of a subscription software business in honest, accurate numbers. It matters because using the wrong definition — even once — causes every other number (growth, retention, valuation) to lie, and decisions based on wrong numbers lead the business in the wrong direction.**

**The main parts explained simply:**

- **MRR and ARR (what "recurring revenue" actually means)** — MRR is the monthly subscription fee income, nothing else. An annual plan is spread evenly: a $600/year plan counts as $50/month, not $600 in one month. ARR is just MRR multiplied by 12. Print order revenue, setup fees, and one-time charges must never be added in — doing so makes the business look bigger than it is and breaks every other calculation.

- **The five movements of MRR (where growth actually comes from)** — Each month, MRR changes for five reasons: new customers joining, existing customers upgrading, old customers coming back, existing customers downgrading, and customers cancelling. Tracking all five separately shows exactly which problem to fix. Most teams only watch "new" and "cancelled" and miss the warning signs in between.

- **Contraction as an early warning sign** — When a customer drops to a cheaper plan, that often means they are about to cancel entirely. Watching this number weekly gives time to reach out and save the relationship before the full cancellation happens.

- **NRR — how well you keep the money you already have** — Net Revenue Retention measures how much subscription income stays from existing customers, including any upgrades. Above 100% means existing customers are spending more over time. Below 100% means you must keep signing new customers just to stay flat. For small-business software tools like this one, around 97% is the normal, healthy level — not 120% (that is an enterprise-software target and does not apply here).

- **GRR — the honest leak test** — Gross Revenue Retention measures the same thing but ignores upgrades, showing only the damage from cancellations and downgrades. It can never be above 100%. If NRR looks fine but GRR is falling, it means a few big upgrades are hiding a lot of cancellations — a sign of real trouble underneath.

- **GMV — a health signal, not revenue** — GMV is the total value of print orders that flow through the platform. It is not the platform's revenue. But it is the single best sign that a store is active and unlikely to cancel. Track it separately as a health indicator, never mix it into subscription revenue numbers.

- **Annual plan accounting** — When a customer pays a full year upfront, that cash cannot all count as income in one month. It must be spread across 12 months in the books. The upfront cash is still a good thing (better for cash flow and customers cancel less), but the revenue recognition must stay spread out.

- **Common myths to ignore** — "Every SaaS should hit 120% NRR" is an enterprise benchmark that does not apply to small-business tools. "Quick Ratio of 4 is a must" is a rule of thumb that means nothing when the customer base is still small. Snowflake's 158%+ NRR figures are from a massive enterprise data-warehouse company — irrelevant as a target here.

**What to do with this:**

Track the five MRR movements every week (new, upgraded, reactivated, downgraded, cancelled) and keep subscription income completely separate from order/transaction income. Watch for stores that downgrade — they are likely about to cancel — and reach out early. Accept that ~97% retention is a healthy target for this type of business; do not chase enterprise benchmarks.
