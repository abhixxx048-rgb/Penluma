You send your business card design to a printer. They reply: "Need 1/8 bleed, this should be 4/0, your logo is RGB not CMYK, and the file is only 150 DPI — it'll print soft."

You understand maybe one word in three. So you nod, say "sure," and quietly hope for the best.

This glossary is the antidote. Every term a print shop might throw at you, explained the way a patient friend would explain it — no fluff, no circular definitions, just what it means and why it matters.

## Why this matters

Print is one of the last industries where a tiny vocabulary gap can cost you real money.

Approve a file with no bleed and your cards arrive with white slivers on the edges. Confuse text weight with cover weight and your "premium" flyer feels like a napkin. Miss that a job is 4/4 when you only paid for 4/0 and the quote suddenly doubles.

Knowing the language does three things for you:

- **You catch mistakes before they print** — when reprints are free, not expensive.
- **You ask for the right thing**, so the proof matches what's in your head.
- **You spot where the money goes**, which is the first step to spending less of it.

You don't need to memorize all of this. Skim it once, then come back when a quote or a printer's email leaves you puzzled. Below, the terms are grouped the way a job actually flows — from file, to color, to press, to finishing — so related ideas sit together.

## Getting your file ready (prepress)

Everything before the press touches paper is called **prepress**. This is where most avoidable errors get caught.

**Bleed** is extra artwork pushed past the final trim line, usually 1/8 inch or 3mm. Cutting blades drift by a hair, so bleed guarantees color reaches the very edge with no white gaps. Think of it as painting slightly past the wall so the corner never shows bare.

**Trim size** is the final size of your piece after it's cut down from the bigger press sheet. **Crop marks** (or trim marks) are the thin lines outside your artwork that tell the cutter exactly where to slice. The marks live in the bleed area and get trimmed away.

**DPI / PPI** — dots, or pixels, per inch — measures image resolution. The standard is **300 DPI at final size**. Below that, photos print blurry. A web image at 72 DPI looks crisp on screen and mushy on paper, which trips up almost everyone the first time.

**Vector vs raster** is the deeper version of that same problem:

- **Vector** art is built from math (lines and curves). It scales to any size with zero blur — perfect for logos and type.
- **Raster** art is built from pixels. Blow it up and it goes soft. Photos are always raster.

When a printer asks for "a vector logo," they want one that stays sharp on a banner *and* a business card.

**Preflight** is an automated check of your file before it goes anywhere — resolution, bleed, fonts, color mode, overprints. **Imposition** then arranges multiple pages on one large sheet, in the right positions and rotations, so that after printing and folding everything lands in reading order. **Make-ready** and **plates** belong to the press stage, but proofing closes out prepress.

**Proof** — your preview for sign-off. A **soft proof** is on-screen (a PDF). A **hard proof** is a physical printed sample. Approving a proof is your last chance to catch errors, so read it like a contract.

## Talking about color

**CMYK** is the four-ink recipe behind most color printing: **C**yan, **M**agenta, **Y**ellow, and **K**ey (black). Mix them as tiny dots and your eye blends them into thousands of colors. Anything built from these four is **process color**.

**Pantone (PMS) / spot color** is the opposite approach: one ink, pre-mixed to match an exact numbered swatch (like PMS 286). Brands use spot colors so their blue is *the same blue* on every job — and for shades, like bright orange or metallics, that CMYK simply can't hit.

**RGB** isn't in the original list, but here's the key fact: screens use red-green-blue light, presses use CMYK ink. RGB can show colors ink can't, so a glowing screen-blue may print duller. Convert to CMYK before you send, and there are no surprises.

**Color management** keeps color consistent from screen, to proof, to press — usually with **ICC profiles**, so what you approved is what comes off the machine. **Registration** is the precise stacking of each ink layer; when it slips, you get blurry, off-color edges. **Spread / choke (trapping)** are tiny deliberate overlaps between touching colors so a small registration slip doesn't reveal a white gap.

And the shorthand that confuses everyone: **4/0** ("four over zero") means full color on the front, nothing on the back. **4/4** is full color both sides. The first number is inks on the front, the second is inks on the back.

## Choosing how it gets printed

There's no single "printing." The method shapes your cost, quality, and minimum order.

- **Offset lithography** — the workhorse of commercial print. Ink goes from a **plate**, to a rubber blanket, to the paper. Pricey to set up, then very cheap per piece. Best for big runs.
- **Digital printing** — straight from the file with **toner** or inkjet, no plates. Near-zero setup, higher cost per page. Best for short runs and personalization.
- **Flexography (flexo)** — fast printing from flexible rubber plates on rolls, used for packaging, labels, and boxes.
- **Screen printing** — ink pushed through a fine mesh stencil, one color per screen. Bold and durable; great for apparel and large runs.
- **Pad printing** — a soft silicone pad lifts a small image and stamps it onto curved or odd objects: pens, mugs, golf balls.
- **Wide-format** — big inkjet machines for posters, banners, signs, and vehicle wraps.

For apparel specifically, two terms get mixed up constantly:

- **DTG (Direct-to-Garment)** inkjets a full-color design straight onto fabric (usually cotton). Perfect for detailed, one-off shirts.
- **DTF (Direct-to-Film)** prints onto a film, dusts it with adhesive powder, then heat-presses it onto the garment. Works on more fabric types than DTG.

Two more press distinctions worth knowing: **web vs sheet-fed** (a web press prints on a continuous roll at high speed, like newspapers; sheet-fed feeds pre-cut sheets for higher quality), and **variable data printing (VDP)**, where text or images change copy to copy — think personalized names, codes, or mailing addresses pulled from a spreadsheet.

## Understanding paper

Paper is graded by weight, coating, and grain — and the words sound interchangeable but aren't.

**GSM** (grams per square meter) is the clean international measure of weight and thickness. Copy paper is ~80 gsm; a business card is 300+ gsm. **Text weight vs cover weight** is the old US shorthand for the same idea — "text" is lighter inside-page paper, "cover" is heavier card for covers and postcards.

**Coating** is any clear layer that makes paper smoother and images sharper. **C1S / C2S** means "coated one side" or "coated two sides." **Substrate** is the umbrella word for whatever you're printing on — paper, card, vinyl, fabric, metal. If a printer says "what substrate?", they mean "what material?"

**Grain (direction)** is the way the paper fibers line up. Paper folds and tears cleanly *along* the grain and cracks *against* it. This is why a thick folded card sometimes cracks at the spine — wrong grain direction. **FSC** (Forest Stewardship Council) certification means the paper came from responsibly managed forests, a common eco selling point.

## Finishing: where it gets fancy (bindery)

Once the ink is dry, the job moves to the **bindery** for folding, cutting, binding, and the premium touches. This is where a flat sheet becomes a real object.

**Binding** — how pages hold together:

- **Saddle stitch** — staples through the spine fold. Cheap and clean for thin booklets and magazines.
- **Perfect binding** — pages glued into a square-backed cover, like a paperback or thick catalog.
- **Self-cover** uses the same stock for cover and inside pages; a "plus cover" uses heavier stock for the cover.

**Cutting and folding** — shaping the sheet:

- **Die-cut** — a sharp metal "die" cuts a custom shape (a window, a rounded label, a folder pocket), like a cookie cutter for paper.
- **Kiss-cut** — cuts a sticker's top layer but not its backing, so it peels off while the sheet stays whole.
- **Perforation (perf)** — a line of tiny cuts so a coupon or ticket tears off cleanly by hand.
- **Score** — a pressed crease that lets thick or coated stock fold without cracking.
- **Collate** — gathering pages into the right order (1, 2, 3) before binding.

**Premium finishes** — the touches people *feel*:

- **Foil stamping** — heat-pressing a thin metallic or colored foil for a shiny gold, silver, or holographic accent.
- **Emboss / deboss** — pressing the paper into a raised (emboss) or recessed (deboss) shape, no ink, pure texture.
- **Spot UV** — high-gloss coating on selected areas only, so a logo gleams against a matte background.
- **Lamination** — a thin plastic film bonded over the sheet for protection and a gloss, matte, or soft-touch feel. Tougher than a coating.

**Coatings, sheen to shine:**

- **Aqueous coating** — a fast, cheap water-based clear coat for light protection and sheen.
- **UV coating** — cured instantly with ultraviolet light for very high gloss and toughness.
- **Varnish** — an ink-like clear coat (gloss, matte, or satin) applied on press; lighter than UV.

A few finishing terms shape *cost*, not look: **gripper** (the mechanical fingers that pull each sheet through the press — they can't carry image, so they shrink your usable area) and **gutter** (the inner margin near a binding, kept clear so text isn't swallowed by the spine).

## Common misconceptions

A handful of myths cause most reprints. Here's the reality.

- **"My logo looks fine on screen, so it'll print fine."** Screens hide low resolution and RGB color. A file can look perfect at 72 DPI and print blurry. Check resolution at final size, in CMYK.
- **"Bleed is optional."** If your design touches the edge, bleed is mandatory. Without it you get white slivers — every time.
- **"GSM equals thickness."** Weight and thickness are related but not identical; a bulky, airy stock can weigh the same as a dense, thin one. Ask for a sample if feel matters.
- **"Digital is always cheaper than offset."** Only for short runs. Offset's setup cost spreads thin over large quantities, so past a few hundred pieces it usually wins.
- **"Pantone and CMYK are basically the same."** They're different systems. A CMYK approximation of a Pantone color can look noticeably off, which is exactly why brands pay for spot inks.

## How to use this

Next time you send a print job, run this checklist:

1. **Set your document to CMYK** before designing, not after. Late RGB-to-CMYK conversions shift colors.
2. **Add 1/8 inch (3mm) bleed** on any edge your artwork touches, and keep important text well inside the trim.
3. **Check images are 300 DPI at final size.** If you're scaling a photo up, its effective DPI drops — verify at the printed size.
4. **Decide 4/0 or 4/4** consciously. Printing the back doubles ink cost; make it a choice, not an accident.
5. **Match the method to the quantity.** Short run or personalized? Digital. Hundreds or thousands? Ask about offset and **gang runs**.
6. **Name your finish on purpose.** "Soft-touch lamination with spot UV on the logo" gets you a far better result than "make it look nice."
7. **Always read the proof carefully** — soft or hard. Approving it is your signature; errors you miss are errors you pay for.

## Conclusion

If you remember one thing, make it this: **print is a physical process with physical tolerances, and almost every term in this glossary exists to manage one of them.** Bleed covers a wandering blade. Trapping covers a shifting ink layer. Grain covers a cracking fold. Learn the vocabulary and you stop guessing — you start directing.

Here's the thread worth pulling next. Notice how many "cheap online print" deals lean on one quiet trick: the **gang run**, where your job rides a giant sheet alongside strangers' jobs. It's the reason 500 business cards can cost less than your lunch — and understanding how shops squeeze cost out of a single press sheet is where the real craft of print production begins.
