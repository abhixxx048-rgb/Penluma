You have a customer call in five minutes. Your notes are everywhere, your idea is buzzing in your head, and you really want them to say yes.

Here is the hard truth: the call will go well or badly based on a handful of small habits you can load into your brain right now. This is the page to skim before you dial - the whole playbook squeezed onto one screen.

## Why this matters

Most calls fail in a quiet, invisible way. The prospect smiles, says "great idea," and never buys. You walk away feeling encouraged and learn nothing.

That is the trap. **Compliments and "I'd totally buy that" are not data.** They are politeness. Past behavior and money already spent - that is data.

A good call gets you facts you can act on: what people actually do, what actually hurts, and whether they will actually move. A bad call gets you a warm feeling and a dead lead. The difference is mostly technique, and technique you can rehearse.

## The three rules that make people honest

This comes from *The Mom Test* by Rob Fitzpatrick - named for questions so well-built that even your mom could not lie to you to spare your feelings.

1. **Talk about their life, not your idea.** The moment you describe your product, they start being nice instead of being honest.
2. **Ask about the past and the specific, not opinions about the future.** "What did you do last time?" beats "What would you do?" every time. People are terrible predictors of their own future behavior and great reporters of their past.
3. **Talk less, listen more.** Aim to let them speak about 80 percent of the time. If you are talking, you are not learning.

A quick example. You are building a tool for print shops. The weak move is "I'm building software that handles rush orders - would that be useful?" They say yes to be kind. The strong move is "Walk me through the last rush order that went sideways." Now they are telling you a true story, with real costs and real frustration baked in.

## Good questions vs bad questions

Same goal, very different results. The left column invites a polite lie. The right column pulls out a fact.

| Bad (leading / hypothetical) | Good (real / past) |
| --- | --- |
| "Do you think this is a good idea?" | "Walk me through the last time you faced this." |
| "Would you pay for this?" | "What do you use today, and what does it cost you?" |
| "Wouldn't it be great if...?" | "How are you solving it right now?" |
| "Do you like it?" | "What have you already tried to fix this?" |

Notice the pattern: every good question points backward in time, toward something that really happened. There is nothing to make up, so there is nothing to fake.

## The shape of a discovery call

A discovery call is just a learning conversation with a spine. Keep these six beats in order and you will never feel lost mid-call.

1. **Open** - set the frame: "Mostly I want to learn how you work today. Cool if I ask some questions?"
2. **Situation** - understand their world and current process.
3. **Problem** - find the pains and frustrations.
4. **Impact** - what do those problems cost them? Time, money, stress.
5. **Goals** - where do they want to be instead?
6. **Next step** - agree on a concrete advance, with a date.

The order matters. You earn the right to talk about goals and next steps only after you have understood their situation and their pain. Skip ahead and you are pitching, not discovering.

### SPIN, one line each

Neil Rackham studied thousands of real sales calls and found the best reps follow a question arc he called **SPIN**. It maps neatly onto the call above.

- **S - Situation:** the facts. "How do you handle X today?"
- **P - Problem:** the pain. "Where does that process break down?"
- **I - Implication:** the cost of the pain. "When it breaks, what happens downstream?"
- **N - Need-payoff:** let them say the value out loud. "If that were solved, what would it mean for you?"

The magic is in **I** and **N**. When the prospect describes the cost themselves, and then names the payoff themselves, you do not have to sell. They have sold themselves.

## Handling objections without getting pushy

When someone pushes back, resist the urge to argue. Use the five **LAARC** steps:

1. **Listen** fully. No interrupting.
2. **Acknowledge:** "That's a fair concern."
3. **Assess / Ask:** "Help me understand - is it the price, or whether it'll actually work?"
4. **Respond** honestly to the real concern.
5. **Confirm:** "Does that put it to rest?"

The core idea: **treat every objection as a question in disguise.** "It's too expensive" usually means "I'm not yet sure it's worth it." Answer the question hiding underneath, not the sharp tone on the surface.

## Reaching out so people say yes

Whether cold or warm, a good outreach message has three parts and stays short:

- **Relevance** - why you, why now, specific to them.
- **Credibility** - one short reason to trust you.
- **Ask** - one tiny, clear request (15 minutes to learn).

Here it is in practice:

> "Hi Sam, I saw your shop does custom wedding invites. I'm researching how small print shops handle rush orders - no pitch. Could I ask you four quick questions for 15 minutes this week?"

Small ask, easy yes. The "no pitch" line lowers the guard, and "four quick questions" makes the time cost feel tiny.

## Lines you can actually say to move forward

You do not have to improvise the hard moments. Keep these in your pocket.

- **Trial close:** "How's this sounding so far?"
- **Trial close:** "Is there anything that would stop this from being a fit?"
- **Advance:** "Makes sense to take the next step - can we set up a pilot starting Monday?"
- **Close:** "I think we can solve this for you. Want to get started?"
- **Honest exit:** "Sounds like this isn't the right time - totally fine. Should I check back in a quarter?"

The honest exit is the underrated one. Walking away cleanly from a bad fit keeps your pipeline real and your reputation intact.

### Qualify fast with BANT

Before you invest hours, run a four-letter gut check:

- **Budget** - can they pay?
- **Authority** - can this person decide, or who can?
- **Need** - is the problem real and felt?
- **Timeline** - when will they actually act?

If three of four are missing, you have a nice conversation, not a deal. That is fine to know early.

## Common misconceptions

**"A call full of compliments went great."** It went pleasantly. Compliments are the sound of someone being polite, not someone reaching for a wallet. Push for the past instead.

**"I should pitch my product so they understand it."** On a learning call, pitching ends the learning. The second you sell, they stop being honest. If you came to learn, do not sell - and even when you came to sell, listen first.

**"Silence means I'm losing them."** Silence is where the real answers live. Resist filling every pause; let the quiet pull a deeper thought out of them.

**"Listing more features will win them over."** Features they did not ask about are noise. Sell the value - what it does for *them* - and only the parts they actually care about.

## How to use this

Five minutes before your next call, do exactly this:

1. **Pick three past-focused questions** from the good-questions table and write them at the top of your notes.
2. **Memorize the six-beat shape** - open, situation, problem, impact, goals, next step. That is your map.
3. **Decide your one concrete ask** for the end of the call, with a date attached.
4. **Pre-load one objection line** so a hard moment does not throw you.
5. **Write down their exact words** during the call - not paraphrases. Quotes are gold; your memory after the call is not.

The Do / Never table to keep beside you:

| Do | Never do |
| --- | --- |
| Ask about the past and real behavior | Ask if they "like the idea" |
| Let silence work for you | Fill every pause or talk over them |
| Sell value (what it does for them) | List features they didn't ask about |
| End with a dated next step | Leave with "I'll think about it" |
| Write down their exact words | Trust your memory after the call |
| Say "no, not yet" honestly | Promise features you don't have |
| Walk away from bad-fit prospects | Chase everyone equally |

## Conclusion

If you remember one thing, remember this: **the past is data, the future is fiction.** Every strong move on this page comes back to pulling out what people really did, not what they say they might do.

Master that, and a question worth chewing on opens up: once you have collected ten honest stories, how do you tell a real pattern from a loud coincidence? That - turning raw conversations into a decision you can bet on - is where customer development gets genuinely interesting.
