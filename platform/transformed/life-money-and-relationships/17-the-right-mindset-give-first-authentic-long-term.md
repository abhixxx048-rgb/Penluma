Two people can send the exact same message, attend the exact same dinner, and walk away with opposite results. One is quietly trying to *extract* something. The other is genuinely trying to *connect*. People feel the difference even when they cannot name it, and that feeling decides everything that follows.

Before any clever technique for meeting people or staying in touch, there is one thing that silently determines whether any of it works: your **mindset**. Get that right and everything you build on top of it rings true.

## Why this matters

Most advice about networking teaches you tactics. Better openers, smoother follow-ups, the perfect coffee-chat script. None of it matters if your underlying posture is "What can this person do for me?"

Here is the uncomfortable truth: humans are remarkably good at detecting a hidden agenda. The second a conversation starts tilting toward your gain, trust quietly drains out of the room. You can be the smartest person there and still leave forgettable.

The good news is the opposite is just as true. A genuine give-first posture compounds over years into something tactics can never buy: a reputation that opens doors before you knock. This article is about installing that posture so it becomes your default, not a performance.

Let's name the core idea up front. **Mindset** here just means the automatic question your brain asks when you meet someone. The wrong default is *"What can I get?"* The right default is *"How can I help, and is there a real human here I'd enjoy knowing?"* Everything below is about making that second question automatic.

## Relationships are a garden, not a vending machine

A **vending machine** gives you a predictable result the instant you pay. Insert coin, press B4, get a snack. A lot of beginners treat networking exactly like this. They do one nice thing, expect an immediate and specific payback, feel cheated when it does not arrive, and quit.

A **garden** works nothing like that. You plant seeds, water them, pull weeds, and wait, often through a whole season with nothing to show. You cannot yank a plant up to make it grow faster. But tend it consistently and it eventually feeds you in ways and amounts you could never have predicted.

Relationships are gardens. The payoff is **delayed, diffuse, and arrives from unexpected directions**, frequently from someone other than the person you actually helped.

> Think of it this way: transactional networking is fishing with one line. You only catch what bites that single hook. Give-first networking is stocking the whole pond. You cannot say which fish you will catch or when, but the water is full of life you put there.

## Give first, and stop keeping score

The single most powerful shift you can make is to **give before you ask, and give without tracking who owes you**.

In his book *Give and Take* (2013), researcher Adam Grant describes three ways people approach exchange:

| Style | Default question | How it feels to others |
|-------|------------------|------------------------|
| **Taker** | "What can you do for me?" | Self-serving; people eventually avoid them |
| **Matcher** | "I'll help if you help me back" | Fair but transactional; keeps a mental ledger |
| **Giver** | "How can I help?" | Generous; builds goodwill that compounds |

Now the surprising part, and it is the most important finding here. Across many fields, from engineers to students to salespeople, Grant found that **givers show up at both the bottom and the top of the success ladder**. Takers and matchers cluster in the unremarkable middle.

Picture the curve:

- **Top performers:** givers
- **The crowded middle:** takers and matchers (most people)
- **The bottom:** givers again, the burned-out ones

So giving is what statisticians call a **high-variance strategy**. It can take you to the top or the bottom. The whole game is learning to give in the way that lands you at the top, instead of leaving you an exhausted doormat.

The question is never *whether* to give. It is *how*.

## How to give without burning out: be "otherish"

What separates the top givers from the doormats? Grant's answer is a word he coined: **otherish**.

- **Selfless givers** have high concern for others and *zero* concern for themselves. They say yes to everything, protect no time, and get drained and exploited. They perform worst.
- **Otherish givers** have high concern for others *and* high concern for their own goals, time, and energy. They are generous *and* boundaried. They perform best.

In plain language: **otherish means generous with boundaries.** This is not selfishness. It is the thing that lets your generosity last for decades instead of burning out in months.

The easiest way to give generously without running dry is the **five-minute favor**, an idea from entrepreneur Adam Rifkin that Grant features in *Give and Take*. Commit to doing anything that takes five minutes or less for anyone:

- A quick introduction between two people who should know each other
- A short piece of honest feedback
- Sharing or amplifying someone's work
- A genuine recommendation or endorsement

High value to them, tiny cost to you. Do one or two a week and you build enormous goodwill while staying fresh.

The trap to avoid is saying yes to every *large* request out of guilt until you are resentful and exhausted. That is selfless over-giving, and it is exactly how good people slide to the bottom of the curve. Protect your core work and your energy. You cannot pour from an empty cup.

## Stay generous, but don't be naive

Being a giver does not mean being a sucker. Successful givers stay generous with *everyone by default*. But when they notice that one specific person keeps taking and never contributing, they quietly switch to **matcher mode** with that person, helping only in proportion to what comes back.

This is sometimes called "[generous tit-for-tat](/blog/life-money-and-relationships/24-giving-value-reciprocity-without-keeping-score)": assume the best, but adjust once someone has proven they are a taker.

> A persistent taker is a leaky bucket. Pour in all the water you want and it never fills, and meanwhile you go dry. Save your water for the buckets that actually hold it.

## Authenticity beats using people

**Authenticity** means your outside matches your inside. You are genuinely curious about and helpful to people, not performing curiosity to get a result. The opposite is treating people as *means to an end*: collecting contacts to mine later, faking interest, or being warm only to those who seem useful.

Why does this matter so much in practice? Because of [how trust actually forms](/blog/life-money-and-relationships/19-how-trust-rapport-actually-form). Your trustworthiness goes *up* with your competence, your reliability, and how safe people feel around you. But it gets *divided down* by how self-focused you appear.

That self-focus, sometimes called **self-orientation**, is the single fastest way to destroy trust. The moment a conversation feels like you are steering everything toward your own product or your own gain, trust collapses, no matter how impressive you are.

So the rule is simple: **show that you care before you show how smart you are.** The most common trust-killer is making every conversation secretly about yourself.

## Common misconceptions

A few beliefs quietly sabotage people who are otherwise doing the right things.

**"A favor is a debt they now owe me."** This is matching disguised as giving, and people smell it instantly. Real generosity gives *and releases* the expectation. The return often comes anyway, just not on your schedule or from the person you expect.

**"Helping them means less for me."** This is the **scarcity mindset**, and it makes you hoard, guard, and keep score. Its opposite, the **abundance mindset**, says there is enough opportunity and goodwill to go around, so another person's win is not your loss. Abundance lets you connect people, share what you know, and celebrate others, all of which make *you* the trusted hub everyone wants to be near.

**"Reaching out would be a bother."** Research on relationships consistently finds people are *happier* to hear from you than you expect, and that we routinely underestimate how much others enjoyed talking with us. Your instinct that "I'd be imposing" is usually just wrong.

**"My big contact list is my network."** Thousands of contacts you never genuinely help is a *recognition list*, not a network. Almost no trust flows through it. A small set of [real, well-tended relationships](/blog/life-money-and-relationships/16-why-relationships-networks-truly-matter) beats it every time.

Here is the abundance mindset in action. Imagine you meet another founder working in the same broad space as you. The scarcity reflex is to stay guarded. The abundance move is: "You should really talk to my old colleague who solved exactly this." That one intro costs you nothing, makes you memorable to two people at once, and is the kind of act that [circles back years later in ways you will never trace](/blog/life-money-and-relationships/27-how-opportunities-hires-advice-really-flow), maybe a referral, maybe a hire, maybe just a warm reputation that opens a door.

## Play the long game

Give-first relationships are **planting, not harvesting**. The hardest part for anyone ambitious is that the timeline is long and the early returns are invisible. You will help people who never directly repay you, and that is fine, because you are filling a **reservoir of goodwill** and a [reputation that pays out from unexpected places over years](/blog/life-money-and-relationships/29-community-reputation-long-term-social-capital).

LinkedIn co-founder Reid Hoffman put the logic plainly: the more genuinely you set out to help others, the more you reinforce your own reputation and expand your "universe of possibilities." Matchers cap their upside; they only ever get back what they specifically traded for. A giver's upside is open-ended.

Which leads to the most practical timing rule there is: **build your network before you need it.** The worst time to start helping people is the week you are job-hunting or fundraising, because every message then screams "I need something." Generosity offered when you want nothing is what makes the eventual ask land as welcome rather than weird.

## How to use this

You do not change a mindset by reading about it. You change it by doing small things until the new default takes hold. Start this week:

1. **Line up three five-minute favors.** Write down three people you could help this week with an intro, a useful link, or a genuine endorsement, with no ask attached. Then actually send them.
2. **Reconnect with one person.** Pick someone you have lost touch with and send a short, warm, no-ask message: "Was thinking about you, what are you working on these days?"
3. **Ask more than you tell.** In your next conversation, set one goal: leave knowing one real thing about the other person's life. Be interested in *them*, not in what they can do for you.
4. **Catch your inner question.** When you meet someone, notice the automatic question. If it is "What can I get?", consciously swap it for "How can I help, and would I like to know this person?"

Do these for a few weeks and the posture stops being a checklist. It becomes who you are.

## Conclusion

If you remember one thing, remember this: **generosity offered freely, with boundaries, and without a scoreboard is the highest-return habit in any relationship.** It is high-variance, so the skill is giving in the otherish way that lands you at the top of the curve, not the bottom.

But mindset is only the soil. Once the soil is right, the question becomes mechanical: how does trust actually get built, conversation by conversation, in a way you can practice on purpose? There is a surprisingly precise formula for it, and the variable that breaks trust fastest is the one almost nobody is watching. That is where this goes next.
