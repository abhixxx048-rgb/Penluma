There's a seductive idea in startup circles: don't compete in a market, invent your own. Be the one company that defines a whole new category, and you'll own it forever.

It's a great story. It's also a quiet killer for most early-stage companies.

The truth is that inventing a category is roughly a $100 million, 6-to-10-year project. Most startups don't have that. The good news: you can get almost all of the upside - the bold story, the gravitational pull, the feeling of standing for something - without the ruinous bill. This is how.

## Why this matters

Positioning decides who shows up to buy before they ever compare your features. Get it wrong and you spend years explaining what you even are. Get it right and the right customers feel like you were built for them, because you were.

The single biggest positioning mistake is choosing the wrong *game*. If you try to create a new category when you should be owning a niche, you'll burn cash teaching the market a new word it didn't ask for - and a better-funded competitor will quietly capture the demand you spent money creating.

So the real question isn't "what's our tagline?" It's "which game are we even playing?" Let's make that choice clear.

## First, three words that get confused

A little vocabulary saves a lot of pain.

- **A product** is the thing you build.
- **A category** is the mental shelf a buyer puts it on *before* they compare features. When someone thinks "I need project management software," that phrase is the shelf, and they'll reach for whatever's on it.
- **Positioning** is deciding which shelf you want to be on and why you're the obvious pick once you're there.

Most of strategy lives in that last word. You're not just building a better product. You're choosing the shelf where your strengths look like superpowers.

## The two schools at war

There are two respected schools of thought here, and they pull in opposite directions.

### School A: Design a brand-new category

The book *Play Bigger* makes the bold case. The biggest winners, it argues, don't win existing markets - they **create new ones** and then dominate them. The headline statistic everyone quotes: the category "king" captures roughly **76% of the total market value**, leaving everyone else to fight over the scraps.

Their playbook has three signature moves:

- **A Point of View (POV):** a manifesto that names a problem, the cost of ignoring it, a vision of a better future, and your unique answer.
- **The named enemy and the old-way-vs-new-way:** you attack the customer's status quo, show it's now unwinnable, and name the "new game."
- **The Lightning Strike:** one concentrated, all-in campaign that makes your POV the talk of the industry.

Real examples land hard here. Drift named **Conversational Marketing** and cast lead-capture forms as the enemy. Gong named **Revenue Intelligence** and cast guesswork sales calls as the enemy. Both made their company feel inevitable.

### School B: Own a segment inside a category that already exists

The counter-school says category creation is a trap for almost everyone, and they have receipts.

**April Dunford**, author of *Obviously Awesome*, favors **niching down**. Use what customers already understand about a market, then narrow the field until you're clearly the best option for one particular kind of buyer. Her classic example: don't sell "a database," sell "an embeddable database for mobile devices." Create a brand-new category, she warns, and you first have to make that category *mean something* in the buyer's mind - slow, costly, risky.

**Louis Grenier** is blunter. True category creation, he argues, is impractical for about 99% of B2B companies. It needs $100M+ and 6 to 10 years, and real new demand is created by tectonic shifts - new technology, new regulation, cultural change - not by a company declaring a category into existence. Instead, **own a segment**. His test: the segment must be (1) specific enough to win, (2) painful enough to drive a purchase, and (3) connected enough that buyers talk to each other.

**Aaron Ross and Jason Lemkin**, in *From Impossible to Inevitable*, call "**Nail a Niche**" the number-one missing growth ingredient. Salesforce started with sales-force automation. Amazon started with books. Facebook started with Ivy League schools. Win one niche completely, *then* expand.

### The twist: they agree more than they look like they do

Here's the resolution most people miss. These schools aren't really enemies.

The smart early-stage move is to **borrow category design's storytelling weapons** - the named enemy, the old-vs-new contrast, the promised land - **while playing the niche-ownership game with your budget.**

You sound like a category creator. You spend like a niche dominator. Call it *category-design-lite*: 80% of the narrative power for about 1% of the cost.

## A worked example: web-to-print software

Abstractions are easy to nod along to and hard to use. So let's run one real case all the way through.

Imagine a company - call it Print-Flow-360 - selling software that lets print shops take orders online. The market is called **web-to-print**, it's already worth well over a billion dollars, it's growing, and most print companies have already adopted *something* in it. The buyer is a non-technical owner of a small, independent print shop.

Should Print-Flow-360 invent a new category, or own a segment? Run the tests.

- **Does demand already exist?** Yes - shop owners already know they need to "sell online." So don't pay to create demand; capture it.
- **Does the buyer already know the category word?** Yes - they search for "web-to-print" by name. Inventing a new word would *hurt* discoverability, not help it.
- **Do we have $100M and a decade?** No. Category creation is off the table financially.
- **Is there an ignored, painful sub-segment?** Yes. The big incumbents all target enterprise printers, trade printers, brokers, and IT departments - people who can configure and integrate software. The non-technical solo shop owner is underserved and frankly intimidated by those tools.

The decision makes itself. Print-Flow-360 fails every test for category *creation* and passes every test for segment *ownership*. The segment is clear: **web-to-print for non-technical, owner-run print shops.**

And here's the category-design-lite magic: they can *brand* that segment as if it were a category. Give it a name. Give it a POV. Give it an enemy. They get the loud, confident story without the war-chest.

## How the narrative comes together

Once you've picked your segment, the storytelling tools from School A do the heavy lifting. Here's how each one looks in practice, using our print example.

### Name the enemy (but never a competitor)

The instinct is to point at the market leader and say "we're the anti-them." Resist it. Naming a competitor is salesy, gives them free advertising, and starts a feature war you may not win.

The stronger enemy is the **belief the buyer already carries**. For the shop owner, the enemy is the assumption that "going online means hiring a developer, paying an agency, or surrendering my customers to a faceless giant." That belief is the villain. Your product is what defeats it.

### Contrast the old way with the new way

A simple two-column story is one of the most persuasive tools in marketing, because it lets the buyer feel the upgrade.

| The old way (the enemy) | The new way (you) |
|---|---|
| "Going online means hiring a developer" | Set it up yourself, in an afternoon |
| Tools built for IT teams and big printers | Built for the owner who runs the shop |
| Weeks of onboarding and config screens | Plain-language setup with smart defaults |
| Lose local jobs to a giant's scale | Keep your customers, brand, and margins |

### Paint the promised land

End on the future the buyer wants, not on your feature list. For the shop owner: *your own branded online store where customers design and order their own jobs, and you wake up to print-ready work - no code, no agency, no IT hire, competing with the giants on your turf: local trust.*

Only after the promised land do you introduce the product as the bridge that gets them there.

## Common misconceptions

**"The category king always wins, so I should create a category."** The 76% statistic is real, but it describes mature categories *after* they exist. It says nothing about your odds of surviving the years and millions it takes to build one. Most companies that try to create a category simply run out of money first.

**"Owning a niche means staying small forever."** The opposite. Nailing one niche is how you *earn the right* to expand. Amazon didn't stay in books. The niche is a beachhead, not a ceiling.

**"A category is the same as a tagline."** A clever tagline on your homepage is not positioning. The POV has to live in your pricing, your onboarding, your sales script, and your product. If the story and the experience disagree, customers believe the experience.

**"More marketing spend equals a Lightning Strike."** With a limited budget, your "strike" is a sharp argument repeated everywhere, not a paid saturation blitz. Concentrate your *message*, not your *spend*.

## How to use this

Here's a concrete sequence to apply this to your own product.

1. **Decide the game first.** Run the four tests: Does demand exist? Does the buyer know the category word? Can you afford 6 to 10 years? Is there an ignored, painful sub-segment? If demand and the word already exist, own a segment - don't invent one.
2. **Pick the segment, sharply.** Name one specific buyer with a struggle that's specific, painful, and connected. "Embeddable database for mobile devices," not "a database."
3. **Keep the known category word, modify the front.** Use the term buyers already search, then plant your flag in the adjective. "Web-to-print *for shops, not developers*."
4. **Write a one-paragraph POV.** Name the problem, the cost of ignoring it, the better future, and your unique answer. This becomes your manifesto.
5. **Name the enemy as a belief, not a brand.** Identify the status-quo assumption your buyer has quietly accepted, and make *that* the villain.
6. **Build the old-way-vs-new-way table.** Three to five rows. Each row should make the reader feel the relief of the upgrade.
7. **Lead with one wedge use-case.** Don't pitch the whole platform. Pitch the single most intuitive job that proves the story, then expand once you've won it.
8. **Make the product prove the POV.** If you promise "no developer needed," the first config wall or cryptic error code exposes the story as a lie. The narrative and the user experience are the same project.

## Conclusion

The one thing to remember: **most companies should own a segment loudly, not create a category quietly.** Borrow the bold storytelling of category design, but spend like someone determined to dominate one specific niche. You get the gravitational pull without the fatal bill, because the demand and the category word already exist. Your job isn't to invent the shelf - it's to become the only obvious choice on it.

Which raises the harder question waiting on the other side of a good positioning bet: once you've won your niche and earned the right to expand, how do you grow into the *next* segment without blurring the sharp identity that won you the first one? That's where the most interesting second acts in business get written.
