**This document is about figuring out how new customers find your business — and whether you really need expensive, complicated tracking tools to do it. The short answer is: no, you don't. For a small print-software business like this one, a simple "How did you hear about us?" question on the signup form does the job better than the fancy systems that big companies pay for.**

**The main parts explained simply:**

- **The tracking-method menu** — There are many ways to track where customers come from, ranging from simple ("credit the first place they found us") to very complex ("split credit across every step of the journey using a machine-learning model"). This document lists all of them and explains which ones are actually worth using for a small business.

- **Why the complicated methods are overkill for us** — The big tracking systems work only when you have hundreds of new customers every month. If your numbers are smaller, those systems just show you confident-looking guesses, not real answers. They also can't see customers who heard about you from a friend, a Facebook group, or a community forum — which is exactly how most of our best customers find us.

- **Self-reported attribution (the survey field)** — This just means asking customers directly, "How did you hear about us?" with a short dropdown list. It costs almost nothing to build, and it captures word-of-mouth and community referrals that no tracking software can ever see. About half of people give a useful answer.

- **UTM tracking links** — When you share a link (on social media, in an email, anywhere), you can add a small label to the end of the URL. When someone clicks it and signs up, you know where they came from. Store that label once at sign-up and you have reliable first-party data that doesn't depend on cookies or third-party trackers.

- **Incrementality testing (the one experiment worth running)** — Instead of tracking every click, pause one channel (say, stop posting in a particular forum) for a few weeks and see if sign-ups drop. That tells you whether that channel is actually bringing in customers or just taking credit. This is the only method that proves cause-and-effect.

- **Using both methods together** — When the survey says "a friend told me" but the tracking says "Google," trust the survey. The tracking misses the real reason; the survey surfaces it.

**What to do with this:** Add a "How did you hear about us?" dropdown to the sign-up form right away — it is the single highest-return action described here. At the same time, tag every shared link with a UTM label and save that label when someone signs up. Skip every other tracking tool until the business is much larger.
