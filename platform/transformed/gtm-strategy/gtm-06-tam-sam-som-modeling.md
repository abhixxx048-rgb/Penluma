A founder puts "$26B market, we'll capture 1%" on a slide. Ten seconds later, the investor stops listening. Not because the market is too small, but because that single line proves no real work was done.

Market sizing has a reputation as a chore you do for the pitch deck. That's the trap. The number on the slide is the least useful thing the exercise produces. The model underneath it, the one that tells you how many customers you can actually reach this quarter, is the part that runs your business.

## Why this matters

Before product-market fit, sizing feels like homework for investors. But the same math answers questions you urgently need answered for yourself.

Done right, a sizing model tells you whether your idea is a $2M lifestyle business or a $50M company. It tells you whether to add new countries or just go deeper in one. And it tells you, concretely, how many sales conversations you must have this quarter to hit your plan.

Done wrong, it tells you nothing, and it quietly signals to anyone experienced that you skipped the thinking. Get the math right once and you reuse the same spreadsheet forever.

The good news: the method is simple, and most people get it wrong in the same few predictable ways. Avoid those and you're ahead of most decks.

## The three circles, in plain language

TAM, SAM, and SOM are three nested circles, biggest to smallest. The rule is absolute: each one sits entirely inside the one above it. And each is an **annual revenue figure** (dollars per year), not a count of companies and not lifetime value.

- **TAM (Total Addressable Market)** is the total yearly revenue if you sold to 100% of everyone on Earth who could conceivably use a product like yours, ignoring competition and your own limits. It answers: *is the prize big enough to build a company around?*

- **SAM (Serviceable Available Market)** is the slice of TAM you can actually serve today, given your languages, your payment options, and the segment you focus on. It answers: *of that prize, how much is even reachable by this product as it exists?*

- **SOM (Serviceable Obtainable Market)** is the slice of SAM you can realistically win in a set window, given your team, your sales motion, and churn. It answers: *how much can we, specifically, capture in 12 to 18 months?*

A simple analogy. TAM is every fish in the ocean. SAM is the fish in the part of the ocean your boat can legally and physically reach. SOM is the fish you can actually catch this season with the crew and nets you have.

## Why bottom-up beats top-down (almost always)

There are two ways to reach a number. Most beginners pick the wrong one.

**Top-down** starts from a big published market figure and multiplies it down: *the market is $X, my region is 40% of that, my segment is half of that...* It's quick. It's also the weakest evidence you can offer, because research firms define "the market" wildly differently and you can't inspect their methods.

Here's how bad the spread gets. For "web-to-print," published figures range from about **$1.2 billion** (software licenses only) to **$26 billion** (including all the ink, paper, and print services). Same two words, a 20x difference. If you put "$26B" on a slide and you sell *software*, a sharp investor will ask if you're counting the paper. You are. That's the trap.

**Bottom-up** starts from things you can count and price:

> **Bottom-up TAM = number of target accounts × average annual contract value (ACV)**

This is what serious investors want, because every input is inspectable. You defend the account count with a census or industry source. You defend the ACV with your own price list. There's no "trust the analyst" step anywhere.

So the right posture is: lead with bottom-up, and use a top-down figure only as a cross-check. If your bottom-up TAM lands wildly outside the published market, one of the two is wrong, and you go find out which before anyone else does.

## A worked example: sizing software for small print shops

Let's make this concrete with a real-world style case study: a SaaS storefront tool sold to small print and sign shops. The same steps apply to any business.

### Step 1: Find your real ACV (not the sticker price)

You can't do bottom-up without an ACV, and the sticker price is *not* the ACV. ACV is what an **average** customer pays per year, blended across pricing tiers, after annual-billing discounts, including any usage add-ons.

Say the product has three tiers, and you estimate the mix of paying customers:

| Tier | Annual price | Share of customers |
|------|-------------|--------------------|
| Starter | ~$490 | 45% |
| Growth | ~$1,290 | 40% |
| Pro / Multi-store | ~$2,990 | 15% |

Blend them: (0.45 × $490) + (0.40 × $1,290) + (0.15 × $2,990) = **$1,185/yr**. Add a realistic uplift for usage and add-ons (say +18%) and you land near a **base ACV of $1,400/yr**.

Then carry three versions for later, because one number always looks like a guess:

- **Low: $900** (mostly Starter, heavy discounting)
- **Base: $1,400** (the mix above)
- **High: $2,400** (more upmarket, healthy add-on attach)

### Step 2: Count the accounts, from real sources

The account count is where bottom-up earns or loses credibility. Source it from citable data and show the filtering out loud.

For the US, public government data (the Census County Business Patterns series) puts printing establishments at roughly **21,400**, and about **71% of them have 1 to 9 staff**, which is the small-shop segment. So:

- 21,400 total × 71% small = **~15,200** small printing shops
- Add nearby sign and quick-print shops not captured in the printing code (an estimate, so label it): **~20,500**
- Keep only those that fit the product (small, non-technical, storefront-needing): **~15,400 US target accounts**

Repeat for other reachable markets (UK, Canada, Australia, and so on), each filtered the same way. Add them up and you get a SAM account count of roughly **29,000 well-sourced accounts**, plus a large but data-thin upside market you label clearly as an assumption rather than a fact.

Notice the discipline: every step is either a cited source or an explicitly flagged estimate. Nothing hides.

### Step 3: Multiply for TAM and SAM

- **SAM** = 29,000 accounts × $1,400 ACV ≈ **$40 million/year**
- **TAM** (scaling the account base up to all small print shops globally, say ~250,000) = 250,000 × $1,400 ≈ **$350 million/year**

Now the cross-check from earlier. The published software-only market was about $1.1 to $1.5 billion. A $350M bottom-up TAM means small shops are roughly 25 to 30% of all that software spend, with big enterprises taking the rest. That's plausible. The two methods sit in the same order of magnitude, so the cross-check passes. If your bottom-up TAM had come out at $5 billion, you'd know an assumption was broken.

### Step 4: Build SOM from capacity, never a percentage

This is the section that makes or breaks a pitch. **Do not write "we'll get 2% of SAM."** Build SOM from what your team can physically do.

Suppose a founder can run **15 real sales conversations per week**. Over 18 months that's about 66 productive weeks, so ~990 conversations. Apply a realistic close rate, add other channels, then subtract churn:

- 15 demos/week × 66 weeks = **990 demos**
- × 22% close rate = **~218 customers** from founder selling
- + 35% from inbound and other channels = **~294 customers**
- − 30% churn over the period = **~206 net active customers**
- × $1,400 ACV = **~$288K in annual recurring revenue**

That's the honest 18-month obtainable revenue. As a share of SAM, 206 of 29,000 is about **0.7%**. The key thing: you arrive at "less than 1% of SAM" as an *output* of the capacity math, not as an input assumption. That ordering is the entire point.

It also reveals the real pre-PMF truth. The market is plenty big. The constraint is founder throughput and close rate. You physically cannot demo your way past roughly $300K solo. So the strategic questions become: raise the close rate, add a salesperson, or lean harder on inbound. The model just handed you your to-do list.

## A third lens: size by value created

There's a powerful sanity check that pressure-tests your price from the *buyer's* side:

> **Value-based TAM = number of accounts × annual value you create per account × value-capture %**

Estimate the real economic value your product creates for one customer. For our print shop, that might be: hours saved fixing wrong-sized artwork, faster quotes that win jobs, after-hours online orders captured. Suppose it totals about **$13,000/year of value created**.

Your $1,400 ACV is then about **11% of the value you create**. SaaS commonly captures 10 to 25%, so 11% sits comfortably in the healthy range, which means your price is, if anything, conservative and has room to grow.

Use this lens to corroborate your ACV, never as your headline number. It's the easiest method to inflate, since every "hour saved" is a guess. But when three methods (bottom-up units, top-down cross-check, and value created) all agree in order of magnitude, that's the strongest market-sizing claim you can make.

## Common misconceptions

**"TAM/SAM/SOM tells my sales team who to call."** No. It's investment math, it tells you whether the opportunity justifies the effort and capital. *Who* to call Monday is your ideal customer profile, a different (and narrower) tool that lives inside SAM.

**"A bigger TAM is always better."** A defensible $40M SAM with visible math beats a confident $4B TAM with none. The single number is the least important output; the model, the sources, and the assumptions are the deliverable.

**"These are just big numbers."** They're specifically *annual revenue*, kept consistent. Slip a one-time fee into an annual figure, or lifetime value into ACV, and everything downstream silently inflates.

**"Market size is my biggest risk."** Usually not, at the start. In the example, SOM swings about 24x from low to high, driven by close rate, churn, channel mix, and ACV, while SAM barely moves 2x. The risk is conversion and retention, not the size of the ocean.

## How to use this

1. **Pull your free anchors first.** Find the citable count for your core market (government statistics offices and industry data series are gold, and often free). This alone makes your headline defensible.
2. **Derive your real ACV.** Build the blended-tier worksheet with your actual prices, discounts, and add-ons. Never size off the sticker price.
3. **Build bottom-up, cross-check top-down.** Compute accounts × ACV, then compare to a published industry figure. If they're orders of magnitude apart, hunt down the broken assumption.
4. **Size SOM from capacity.** Conversations per week × weeks × close rate, plus other channels, minus churn, times ACV. Let the "percent of SAM" fall out as a result.
5. **Always ship three numbers.** Low, base, and high, in a sensitivity table. A single magic figure reads as guessing; a visible range reads as rigor.
6. **Label every estimate.** Tag each input as either SOURCED or ASSUMPTION, with a confidence level and a note on how you'll validate it. That register is your live planning dashboard.
7. **Validate the driver, not the decimals.** The model says your constraint is close rate and churn, so spend the week booking demos, not refining the third digit of your TAM.

## Conclusion

The single takeaway: **market sizing is not a number you present, it's a model you operate.** The figure on the slide should be the byproduct of a chain you can defend line by line, where every count is sourced and every guess is labeled.

Get this right and you'll notice something uncomfortable and useful: the market is almost never the thing holding you back. Your weekly sales throughput is. Which raises the next question worth obsessing over: of all the small shops you *could* reach, which exact one should you call first? That's not a sizing question at all, it's the ideal customer profile, and it's where the real growth work begins.
