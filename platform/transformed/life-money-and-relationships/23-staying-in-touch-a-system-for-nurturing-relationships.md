Here is the quiet truth about networking: the hard part isn't meeting people. It's not losing them.

You go to an event, have a great chat, swap details, feel that little spark of "we should stay in touch." Then life happens. Three years later you need a favor, open your contacts, and realize you haven't spoken to that person since the day you met. The relationship didn't end in a fight. It just quietly faded.

This is about building a simple system so that doesn't happen. The good news: it's one of the most learnable, lowest-effort, highest-payoff skills there is. You don't need to be charismatic. You just need a list and a rhythm.

## Why this matters

Relationships rarely die from conflict. They die from neglect.

That matters because your network is where your best opportunities, introductions, advice, and support actually come from, and almost all of it depends on ties staying warm. A cold contact you met once can't help you. A warm one often will, gladly.

A staying-in-touch system turns "I really should call them" (guilt) into a scheduled, frictionless habit (action). It does the remembering so you don't have to.

## Why most connections die after the first conversation

Two forces work against you. Naming them helps you beat them.

**Silent decay.** A connection has no alarm that goes off when it's getting cold. Unlike a bill or a deadline, nobody reminds you to message someone. So the tie slides, not because you stopped caring, but because nothing prompted you.

**Capacity limits.** Anthropologist Robin Dunbar found that humans can only maintain roughly **150 meaningful relationships** at once. They sit in nested layers: about 5 of your closest people, 15 good friends, 50 friends, and 150 you actively keep up with. Each outer ring is roughly three times larger than the one inside it, and each person in it costs you less time and emotion. Treat 150 as a rough order of magnitude, not an exact law, since researchers debate the precise number.

Put those together and you get the core problem: **you literally cannot keep up with everyone equally.** Try to message all 150 people every week and you'll burn out and quit.

The skill isn't doing more. It's deciding *who* gets *what* level of attention, and letting a system carry the load your memory can't.

> Think of it as a gym log for friendship. What gets tracked gets done. Relying on memory to stay in touch is exactly *why* people lose touch without ever meaning to.

## The personal CRM: your relationship memory

A **personal CRM** is just a lightweight system that does three jobs. (CRM stands for Customer Relationship Management, a term borrowed from sales, but here it's purely personal.)

1. **Remembers details** about people: how you met, their kid's name, the trip they mentioned, what they're working on right now.
2. **Reminds you to reach out** on a schedule, so ties don't quietly fade.
3. **Logs your interactions** so next time you can pick up exactly where you left off ("How did that conference talk go?").

It can be a fancy app or a plain spreadsheet. **The tool is not the point.** A spreadsheet you actually open beats the slickest app you never touch.

Here's a starter layout you can build in five minutes:

| Name | Tier | Last talk | Next touch | Notes |
|------|------|-----------|------------|-------|
| Sara Lee | 15 | 2026-05-02 | 2026-06-15 | dog surgery |
| Tom Ray | 50 | 2026-03-10 | 2026-06-10 | new job @X |
| Old prof | 150 | 2024-11-01 | 2026-07-01 | reconnect! |

One warning: don't treat your CRM like a sales pipeline, logging people as "targets to convert." That mindset leaks into your messages, and people can smell it. Frame the system as *remembering that I care*, not *tracking who owes me*.

## Cadence and tiers: how often to reach out

**Cadence** just means rhythm, how often you touch base. The trick is to tier your people using Dunbar's layers and give each tier its own rhythm. Tag each contact, then let the system nudge you on schedule.

| Tier | Who they are | Reach-out rhythm |
|------|--------------|------------------|
| Inner circle (~5–15) | Closest people, key collaborators | Every 30–60 days (and naturally more) |
| Warm friends (~50) | People you genuinely like and enjoy | About every 90 days (quarterly) |
| Wider network (~150+) | Acquaintances, weak ties, dormant ties | Roughly every 6 months |

These numbers are heuristics, not rules. Don't get clinical. A real relationship that's flowing doesn't need a stopwatch. The cadence exists to catch the people who'd *otherwise drift away unnoticed*, especially in that wide outer ring.

## No-ask touches: the heart of the whole system

Slow down here, because this is the most important idea.

The fastest way to make people dread your name is to **only ever message them when you want something.** Every contact starts to feel like a bill landing in their inbox.

The fix is the **no-ask touch**: a short, warm message that contains no request at all and is about *them*, not you.

Here's the counterintuitive part, and it's backed by research: people are reliably **happier to hear from you than you expect.** An out-of-the-blue message lands as a small gift, not an imposition. We almost always overestimate how much we're bothering someone.

Good no-ask touches, all of which take under five minutes:

- **"Saw this and thought of you"** — an article, tool, or opportunity relevant to *their* current project, not a generic forward.
- **A congratulations** on a job change, launch, award, or work anniversary. (LinkedIn surfaces these for free.)
- **A specific memory:** "Was just remembering that talk we had about pricing. Hope you're doing well."
- **A genuine, specific thank-you** for something they did, even long ago.
- **A check-in on a human detail** they mentioned: "How did your daughter's recital go?"

> **Mini case:** A founder keeps a note that an old colleague is training for a marathon. Six months later, with zero agenda, she texts: "Saw the city marathon is this weekend. Were you running it? Rooting for you!" That 20-second message keeps the tie warm far better than a polished pitch ever could. When she later needs an introduction, the relationship is alive, not awkwardly resurrected.

## Reviving dormant ties: the move you're not making

First, two quick definitions.

A **weak tie** is someone you know only loosely: an acquaintance, a friend of a friend, a person you've met a few times but aren't close to.

A **dormant tie** is a once-active relationship that's gone quiet: an old classmate, a former boss, a collaborator from two startups ago.

Research by Levin, Walter, and Murnighan found that dormant ties are goldmines. There's **leftover trust**, so reconnecting is easy, *plus* **fresh information**, because you've both moved into new circles since you last spoke. In short, a dormant tie gives you a weak tie's fresh information with a close tie's trust already in place.

How to reconnect well:

- **Don't over-apologize for the gap.** A profuse "I'm so sorry it's been forever" centers *your* guilt. Most people aren't keeping score. Acknowledge it briefly, then move to warmth: "I know it's been a while! You popped into my head because [specific reason] and I wanted to say hi."
- **Lead with catching up or giving, never an ask.** "What are you working on these days?" beats "Can you intro me to..."
- **Offer an easy out:** "No need to reply, just sending a good thought your way."

A relationship doesn't "expire" because you went silent. Trust residue lasts for years, and the awkwardness you're imagining is almost always overblown. Reconnections are usually received warmly.

## Common misconceptions

**"Staying in touch means being a great networker."** Reality: it means having a list and a rhythm. The system does the heavy lifting that charisma is supposed to.

**"If I reach out with no reason, I'm bothering people."** Reality: people are gladder to hear from you than you predict. An unprompted, warm message reliably brightens someone's day.

**"We've lost touch, so the relationship is gone."** Reality: dormant ties are among your most valuable contacts. The trust is still there; you just have to wake it up.

**"I need the perfect app first."** Reality: a spreadsheet you actually open beats a perfect system you abandon. Tools don't build relationships; rhythm does.

**"A long, polished message shows I care."** Reality: a short, specific one does. "Hope you finally got that fishing trip in!" beats a paragraph, because it proves you remember them as a person.

## How to use this: your weekly routine

Block **one "relationship hour" a week** on your calendar. That single recurring slot is the engine of the whole system.

1. **Start small.** Add just 20 people to a spreadsheet with five columns: name, tier, last talk, next touch, notes. Don't over-engineer it.
2. **Tag each person with a tier** (inner circle, warm, wider) so you know their cadence at a glance.
3. **Weekly (30–60 min):** jot fresh notes for anyone you spoke to this week, send 1 dormant-tie reconnect with no ask, and send 1–2 no-ask touches to people who are due.
4. **Monthly:** scan your warm ~50. Who haven't you touched lately? Send a low-pressure check-in.
5. **Quarterly:** sweep the wider 150 with a congrats, a "thought of you," or a holiday note.
6. **Use the calendar's free triggers.** Birthdays, job changes, launches, and anniversaries are built-in, never-forced reasons to reach out. Make the note personal.
7. **Try the Sent-folder shortcut** (popularized by writer Nat Eliason): once a week, scan your email Sent folder and tag everyone you contacted into the right tier. It turns memory into a system with almost no extra effort.

When you're ready for more automation, pick one tool and don't agonize. A plain spreadsheet or Notion table is free and fully yours. Dex sets a cadence per person and sends reminders. Clay enriches contacts and surfaces job changes. Covve is mobile-first and keeps phone contacts fresh. Monica is open-source and privacy-friendly. (Features and prices change yearly, so verify before committing.)

## Conclusion

If you remember one thing, make it this: **relationships die from neglect, not conflict, and a simple system beats willpower every time.** The people who seem effortlessly well-connected aren't more charming than you. They just refuse to rely on memory.

So start this week. Twenty names, a spreadsheet, one no-ask message.

And here's the thread worth pulling next: the single most valuable contact in your network is probably someone you haven't spoken to in years. That dormant tie carries old trust *and* brand-new information. The question is which forgotten name, sitting quietly in your phone right now, is worth waking up first.
