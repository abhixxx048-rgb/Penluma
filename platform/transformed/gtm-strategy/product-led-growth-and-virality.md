Most software companies spend a fortune teaching strangers to want their product. The smartest ones do something else entirely: they let the product sell itself, and they let happy customers quietly hand them the next batch of customers for free.

That is the whole idea behind **product-led growth** and **virality**. Done well, your product becomes your best salesperson and your cheapest marketing channel at the same time. Done badly, you give away the farm and wonder why nobody pays.

This guide explains how both actually work, in plain language, with the honest math most growth pitches leave out.

## Why this matters

If you sell a tool people can try on their own, your single biggest lever is not your ad budget. It is how fast a new user reaches the moment they think *"oh, this is genuinely useful."*

Get someone there quickly and they stick, they upgrade, and some of them pull friends in behind them. Fail to get them there and no amount of clever email or paid traffic saves the relationship. Studies of trial users repeatedly find that people who never reach real value churn within a couple of weeks, almost without exception.

So the payoff is concrete:

- **Lower cost to acquire customers**, because the product and existing users do part of the selling.
- **Higher conversion**, because people buy after feeling the value, not before.
- **Compounding growth**, because each new customer can seed the next one.

The catch is that every piece of this has a wrong way to do it that looks reasonable on a whiteboard. Let us walk through the right way.

## What product-led growth really means

Product-led growth (PLG for short) means the **product itself** is the main engine for getting, converting, and expanding customers. Instead of a salesperson walking you through a demo, you sign up, start using it, and decide for yourself.

Think of how you probably started using tools like Dropbox, Calendly, or Slack. Nobody called you. You tried it, it solved a problem, and at some point you hit a limit or wanted a premium feature, so you paid.

PLG works best when three things are true:

- **The price is low enough** that a single person can decide to buy without a committee.
- **One person can feel the value** without needing six teammates onboard first.
- **The value is obvious quickly**, ideally in minutes.

### The PQL: a lead that has actually used the thing

In a sales-led company, a "lead" is someone who filled out a form. In a product-led company, the signal that matters is the **product qualified lead**, or PQL.

A PQL is someone who has done something inside your product that proves real intent. Not "logged in three times," which is vanity. Something meaningful, like a store owner whose first customer just placed a real order. That single event tells you more than any form ever could, because it means their business is working *because of you*.

The practical rule: **define your PQL as a real value event, then build everything around helping people reach it.**

## Activation: get to the "aha" fast, before anything else

Here is a distinction that trips up almost everyone. There is a difference between **setup** and the **aha moment**.

- **Setup** is configuration: adding your logo, picking a theme, connecting a payment method.
- **The aha moment** is the emotional click where the user feels the value land.

A classic mistake is to build an onboarding checklist that ends at setup. "You previewed your store! All done!" But previewing a store is not the value. The value is a real customer placing a real order. If your checklist celebrates the preview and goes quiet after that, your users high-five themselves and then drift away, because they never reached the part that actually matters.

### A simple model: Setup, Aha, Habit

Think of onboarding in three stages:

1. **Setup** - the first configuration to get going.
2. **Aha** - the first real taste of value (the order lands, the report generates, the meeting gets booked).
3. **Habit** - value happening again and again, so the product becomes part of the routine.

Most products engineer stage one beautifully and forget the other two. The fix is to make sure your onboarding pushes people all the way to a genuine aha, then hands them off to something that brings them back.

### Cut friction before you add motivation

There is a useful behavior model that says any action requires three things at once: **motivation, ability, and a prompt.** When someone is highly motivated but struggling with the steps, the answer is almost never a more persuasive popup. It is **removing steps.**

Picture a small print-shop owner who desperately wants an online store but is not technical. She has all the motivation in the world. What stops her is fiddly configuration. So the winning move is to pre-load sensible defaults, a sample catalog, a working offline payment option, so the store can take an order on day one with zero setup, and then simply *tell her that*: "You can already take orders."

Pre-loaded sample data has, in several documented cases, roughly doubled activation. People do not start from a blank page; they start from something that already works.

### Measure activation, do not guess it

A famous early-Facebook insight was that users who added "7 friends in 10 days" tended to stick. It became gospel. The honest footnote is that this was largely **correlation**, not a proven cause.

The lesson is not "find your magic number and hard-code it." The lesson is: **instrument your funnel, watch which early actions actually predict people staying, and let the data tell you.** Treat "first order within 7 days" or any similar rule as a hypothesis to confirm, not a law.

## Virality: powerful, widely misunderstood, and usually under 1

Virality is when your users bring you more users. It is measured by the **viral coefficient**, usually written as **k**.

> **k = the number of invites each user sends × the share of those invites that convert.**

If k is above 1, every cohort spawns a bigger one and growth sustains itself with no ad spend. That is the dream. It is also extraordinarily rare. Most business software sits around **k ≈ 0.2**, and reaching k above 1 essentially requires the product to be useless alone, like a shared document or a team chat.

### Three flavors of viral loop

Not all virality is referral programs. There are three broad types:

- **Inherent virality** - using the product naturally exposes other people to it, with no extra effort. The classic example is the old Hotmail signature: *"PS: I love you. Get your free email at Hotmail."* Every email was a tiny ad.
- **Referral** - you deliberately ask users to invite others, often with a reward.
- **Word of mouth** - people talk about you because you are good.

Inherent virality is the cheapest of the three because it requires **zero behavior change**. The product does the spreading just by being used.

### The "Powered by" loop

You have seen this everywhere. A small "Powered by Shopify" in a store footer. A "Powered by Calendly" on a booking page. A subtle Canva credit on a shared design. Each one is a clickable little billboard sitting on a surface the company already operates for free.

This is the single most accessible viral move for many products, because:

- It rides on traffic you already have.
- It costs the user nothing and changes none of their behavior.
- It doubles as a clean upgrade reason: **"Remove the badge - go premium."** That is exactly how Shopify, Wix, and countless others monetize it.

If you run any surface that your users' own audiences see, a storefront, an invoice, an exported file, a shared link, that surface is free advertising you are probably wasting right now.

### An honest k calculation

Let us be unromantic about it, because overselling virality is how growth plans lose credibility. Suppose each active customer's badge earns about 500 views a month. Say 1% click through to your site (5 people), and of those, maybe 1 to 2% are realistic prospects who eventually sign up. That is roughly 0.05 to 0.1 signups per customer per month. Stretched over a six-month decision window, you get something like **0.15 to 0.4 referred signups per customer**.

That is **k well below 1**. It is *sub-viral amplification*, not a self-sustaining engine. And that is fine. It quietly lowers your blended cost of acquisition and compounds your other channels. Just do not pitch it as a perpetual motion machine, because it is not.

## Common misconceptions

A few myths cause real damage, so let us clear them up.

- **Myth: a distribution loop is a network effect.** A "Powered by" badge is a one-way distribution loop. One customer showing your badge does not make the product more valuable to another customer. A **network effect** is different: more users make the product *better for everyone* (think a marketplace where more sellers mean more choice for buyers). Calling a badge a network effect sets false expectations.
- **Myth: freemium is always the way to grow.** Freemium only pays off at massive scale with near-zero cost per user, or when free users spread the product. Neither is guaranteed. Plenty of products lose money carrying free users who cost real infrastructure and never refer anyone.
- **Myth: more A/B tests equal better growth.** If your traffic is small, a "proper" A/B test on your core funnel can need tens of thousands of users per variant to reach a conclusion, which could take months. At low scale, cheap qualitative experiments beat slow statistical ones.
- **Myth: a finished checklist means an activated user.** A green checklist with zero real value delivered is a false positive. Setup is not aha.
- **Myth: hitting a limit should just block the user.** A hard wall that stops a non-technical user mid-task reads as *broken*, not as an upsell. More on that below.

## Freemium vs free trial vs reverse trial

This is where a lot of money is won or lost, so here is the plain comparison.

- **Freemium** - a free plan that lasts forever, with paid upgrades. Typically converts around **2 to 5%**. You carry roughly **20 to 50 free users per paying one**.
- **Opt-in free trial** (no card) - full product for a limited time, then it ends. Converts around **8 to 22%** depending on the product.
- **Reverse trial** - you start *every* new user on the full premium plan for a fixed window, then automatically downgrade them to a limited state if they do not pay.

The reverse trial is the quiet winner for many low-priced, self-serve tools. People experience the best version first, build habits around premium features, and feel the loss when the trial ends. Conservative estimates put it around **8 to 15%**, with optimistic best-case profiles claiming higher. Either way, treat any benchmark you read as a hypothesis to test against your own numbers, never a forecast.

### Give away the habit, charge for the scale

The cleanest way to design free-versus-paid is this principle: **let people keep the daily habit for free, and charge for the part that scales their success.**

For a store builder, that means the design studio, the saved designs, and the catalog stay available, that is the habit and the lock-in. What you charge for is the **live, publicly hosted, order-taking storefront**, the thing that actually makes them money. That is the fence worth paying to cross.

### When free actively hurts you

Free is only justified if it does one of these jobs: helps you acquire users, fuels virality, seeds a network effect, educates the market, or assists sales. If your free users do none of that and each one costs real money to host, store files for, and compute on, then a permanent free plan is a slow leak.

A smarter fallback than "open free forever" is a **dormant tier**: keep the user's data alive and editable so they can return in one click, but switch off the expensive live parts (the public, always-on storefront). Their work is safe, your costs drop to near zero, and the paid upgrade reason stays sharp. It is the cheapest way to keep a hesitant or churned customer warm.

A quick warning from the trenches: make sure your system's *default* for "no active plan" is **locked down, not wide open**. A common bug is enforcement code that "fails open," granting unlimited usage when it cannot find a plan. A free tier should be an explicit plan with explicit caps, never the accidental absence of one.

## Turn paywalls into helpful moments, not dead ends

When a user hits a limit, the worst thing you can do is throw up a blank wall. To a non-technical person, a hard block in the middle of a task does not say "time to upgrade." It says "this is broken."

Replace hard blocks with a **value-then-fence** pattern:

1. **Explain the value** they are about to unlock in one plain sentence.
2. **Offer a clear path** (an Upgrade button right there at the point of friction).
3. **Soft-warn before the wall**, not at it. As someone approaches a limit, gently let them know.

The same applies to trials. A quiet, non-nagging strip that says *"5 days left on your trial. Here is what you keep and what you lose"* respects the user and frames the decision honestly. Loss framing, what they would *lose*, tends to land harder than a list of features they would *gain*.

## How to use this

If you take nothing else away, here is a concrete order of operations. Notice that measurement comes first, because you cannot improve what you cannot see.

1. **Instrument your funnel before optimizing anything.** Track the real events: signup, first key action, the aha moment, trial start, upgrade, cancel. Without this, every "improvement" is a guess.
2. **Define your PQL as a genuine value event**, not a vanity action. Make it something that proves the user got real value.
3. **Engineer the path to aha, then celebrate it.** Pre-load sensible defaults, strip out skippable setup, and fire a real moment of delight when the user reaches actual value.
4. **Ship a "Powered by you" badge** on every surface your users' audiences already see. Tag each surface separately so you learn which one actually converts.
5. **Gate it with a single "remove branding" upgrade.** One flag both monetizes the loop and gives the clearest possible upgrade reason.
6. **Prefer a reverse trial over open freemium** for low-priced, self-serve products. If you need a free fallback, make it a dormant tier, not a free live product.
7. **Replace hard blocks with value-then-fence paywalls** shown at the exact point of friction.
8. **Run a 30-minute weekly growth meeting** off a simple backlog. Score ideas by Impact, Confidence, and Ease (the ICE method), and favor cheap "fake door" tests over slow A/B tests when traffic is low.
9. **Time your upgrade ask to the aha moment.** Asking for a card right after the user feels real value converts far better than asking on an arbitrary calendar day, because willingness to pay peaks exactly then.

A note on that last point: only trigger an upsell on a value moment you can actually deliver and actually charge for. Promising magic at a moment your product cannot yet fulfill just trains people to distrust you.

## Conclusion

The single idea worth carrying around is this: **growth is not something you bolt onto a product, it is something the product does when you remove the friction between a person and their first real win.** Everything else, the badges, the trials, the paywalls, is just making that first win arrive faster and spread further.

There is one move in this whole playbook that quietly outranks the rest, though. A "Powered by" badge amplifies. A reverse trial converts. But neither builds a *moat*. The one thing here that could make your product genuinely hard to leave is a true network effect, the kind where every new customer makes the product more valuable to all the others. That is a much harder thing to build, and a far better thing to own. Worth a closer look next.
