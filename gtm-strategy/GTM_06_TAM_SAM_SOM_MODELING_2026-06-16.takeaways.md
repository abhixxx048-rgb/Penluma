**This document answers one question: how big is the market for Print-Flow-360, really? It shows you how to count the number of small print shops you can actually reach, figure out how much each one would pay you per year, and estimate how many you can win in 18 months. Done right, this math tells you whether the business is worth building - and gives investors a number they can trust.**

**The main parts explained simply:**

- **TAM, SAM, SOM - the three circles** - Three nested estimates, each smaller than the last. TAM = every print shop in the world that could use your product. SAM = the shops you can actually reach today (right countries, right payment systems, right language). SOM = the shops you can realistically sign up in the next 18 months. Every number must fit inside the one above it.

- **Top-down sizing (industry reports)** - Start from a published "total market" figure and filter it down. Useful as a quick cross-check, but easy to get wrong. The document warns: the "$26B print market" number includes ink and paper, not just software. The honest software-only figure is $1.1–1.5 billion. Never lead with the big misleading number.

- **Bottom-up sizing (counting real shops)** - The method investors actually respect. You count real shops using government data (US Census, Australia's IBISWorld, UK's ONS), then multiply by what each shop pays you per year. Every step is visible and checkable, which is the whole point.

- **ACV - what one customer pays per year** - Not the sticker price. You blend your three pricing tiers by how many customers land on each one, add a little for extras, and arrive at roughly $1,400 per shop per year as a realistic average. The document shows the exact calculation.

- **SOM from capacity, not a percentage** - Never say "we'll get 1% of the market." Instead, count how many real demos a founder can run per week (about 15), how many close (roughly 22%), subtract customers who leave, and you get the honest 18-month number: about 206 paying shops and ~$288K in annual revenue. The math shows the constraint is your time and close rate, not the market size.

- **Value-theory check** - A cross-check from the shop owner's side. Add up real time and money the product saves a shop each year (roughly $13,000 in saved labor and won jobs). At a $1,400 price, you're capturing only about 11% of what you save them - meaning the price is fair and has room to grow.

- **Low / base / high sensitivity table** - The document always shows three scenarios, not one magic number. This shows investors you've thought through the risks, and it shows you where the real levers are (close rate and churn matter far more than market size).

- **Where to find trustworthy data** - US Census (free, citable), IBISWorld for Australia, ONS for the UK, Statistics Canada for Canada. India has no clean public data - always label India as an assumption with a wide range, never as a hard fact.

**What to do with this:** Build the spreadsheet using your real pricing tiers - do not use the illustrative numbers in the document as your final answer. Pull the free US Census data this week to anchor your US count. Show investors three numbers (low/base/high), never one, and always build your 18-month revenue target from how many demos you can actually run - not from a percentage of the market.
