Revision Cheat Sheet

Last-minute review of the Ten Disciplines. Each block gives the one-sentence big idea, the must-remember concepts, and the traps that catch most people. Scan top to bottom; the key boxes are the things you cannot afford to forget and the watch boxes are the errors examiners and real life punish hardest.

1. Systems Thinking

Big idea: A system's behaviour comes from its structure (how its parts connect and feed back), not from the parts in isolation or from outside events.
  • System = elements + interconnections + a purpose. Change the purpose or the connections and behaviour changes even if the parts stay the same. Purpose is revealed by behaviour, not by stated goals.
  • Stocks and flows: a stock is an accumulation (water in a bath, cash, trust); flows fill and drain it. Stocks change slowly and create momentum and inertia.
  • Feedback loops: balancing (negative) loops seek a goal and create stability; reinforcing (positive) loops amplify change and create exponential growth or collapse.
  • Delays between action and effect cause overshoot, oscillation, and over-correction — most policy failures are mis-timed responses to delayed feedback.
  • Leverage points (Meadows, low→high power): parameters < buffers < feedback-loop strength < information flows < rules < goals < paradigms. We instinctively push the weak, obvious ones (numbers/parameters).
  • System traps: tragedy of the commons, policy resistance, drift to low performance, escalation, success-to-the-successful, addiction/dependence, rule-beating, seeking the wrong goal.
  • Emergence & non-linearity: the whole has properties no part has; small inputs can cause huge outputs (and vice-versa). Bounded rationality means actors behave sensibly with local info yet produce bad global outcomes.
Watch out for: (1) Blaming events or individuals ("bad apple") when the structure would make almost anyone behave that way. (2) Pulling a leverage point in the right place but the wrong direction — and ignoring delays, so you keep adding pressure after the system has already turned.

2. Economics

Big idea: Resources are scarce, so every choice has a cost; prices and incentives coordinate millions of decentralized decisions and reveal what is valued.
  • Opportunity cost = the value of the next-best option given up. The real cost of anything is what you forgo, not the money spent. Sunk costs are gone — ignore them.
  • Marginal thinking: rational actors weigh the next unit's extra benefit vs extra cost, not totals or averages. Optimal point: MB = MC.
  • Supply & demand: price moves to clear the market; shifts in either curve move price and quantity. Elasticity = how much quantity responds to price (necessities/few-substitutes = inelastic).
  • Prices carry information (Hayek): a price signal compresses dispersed knowledge about scarcity and want — no planner could gather it all.
  • Incentives drive behaviour, including unintended ones. Comparative advantage means trade benefits both sides even if one is better at everything.
  • Market failures: externalities (pollution), public goods (free-rider), information asymmetry (lemons), monopoly/market power. These justify intervention — but government failure is also real.
  • Macro: GDP, inflation, unemployment. Money's value comes from trust + scarcity; inflation is too much money chasing too few goods. Central banks use interest rates; growth comes from productivity, not just more inputs.
Watch out for: (1) The lump-of-labour / fixed-pie fallacy and the broken-window fallacy — counting visible gains while ignoring the unseen opportunity cost. (2) Treating correlation in macro data as policy proof; the economy is a feedback system with long, variable lags.

3. Psychology

Big idea: The mind runs mostly on fast, automatic, error-prone shortcuts; reason is slower, lazier, and often justifies decisions the fast system already made.
  • System 1 vs System 2 (Kahneman): S1 = fast, intuitive, emotional, always-on; S2 = slow, effortful, deliberate, easily fatigued and avoided. Most of life runs on S1.
  • Attention is a bottleneck and memory is reconstructive — we rebuild memories each recall, so they're editable and unreliable (eyewitness problem).
  • Perception is inference: the brain predicts and fills gaps; we see expectations as much as reality.
  • Motivation: intrinsic (autonomy, mastery, purpose) vs extrinsic; extrinsic rewards can crowd out intrinsic motivation. Maslow-style needs are a rough ladder, not a law.
  • Social forces are powerful: conformity (Asch), obedience (Milgram), bystander effect, in-group/out-group bias — situation often beats personality (fundamental attribution error).
  • Emotion is not the enemy of reason — it tags options with value and is required for good decisions (Damasio); pure cold logic decides poorly.
  • Personality: the Big Five (OCEAN) is the best-validated model; traits are stable-ish but expressed through situations.
Watch out for: (1) The fundamental attribution error — explaining others' behaviour by character but your own by circumstance. (2) Believing memory is a recording; confident, vivid memories are routinely wrong.

4. Statistics & Data Literacy

Big idea: Data summarizes reality but always loses information; the summary you choose, and what it hides, decides whether you see truth or a flattering lie.
  • Data types: categorical (nominal/ordinal) vs numerical (discrete/continuous) — they determine which average, chart, and test are valid.
  • Centre: mean (sensitive to outliers), median (robust, use for skewed data like income), mode. Always ask which one and why.
  • Spread matters as much as centre: range, variance, standard deviation, IQR. "Average depth 1 m" can still drown you.
  • Distributions & shape: skew, outliers, and the difference between a normal bell curve and a fat-tailed one. Many real-world things are not normal.
  • Probability basics: independence, conditional probability, base rates. Bayes: update prior belief with evidence; a positive test on a rare condition is usually a false positive.
  • Sampling: a conclusion is only as good as the sample. Watch sample size, selection/survivorship bias, and the sampling frame.
  • Inference: confidence intervals beat single point estimates; a p-value is not the probability the hypothesis is true, and statistical significance ≠ practical importance.
Watch out for: (1) Correlation ≠ causation — confounders, reverse causation, and coincidence. (2) Chart and summary tricks: truncated/zoomed y-axes, cherry-picked windows, base-rate neglect, and Simpson's paradox (a trend reverses when groups are combined).

5. Business Strategy

Big idea: Strategy is a coherent set of choices to be different in a way competitors can't easily copy — not a goal, a vision, or "do everything well".
  • Strategy = where to play + how to win. It requires deliberate trade-offs and saying no; trying to please everyone is "stuck in the middle".
  • Competitive advantage = sustainably earning above-average returns, via lower cost or differentiation (Porter's generic strategies).
  • Moats protect advantage: network effects, switching costs, economies of scale, intangible assets (brand/patents), cost advantages. Without a moat, profits get competed away.
  • Porter's Five Forces set industry profitability: rivalry, new entrants, substitutes, buyer power, supplier power. Pick attractive industries and defensible positions.
  • Business model = how you create, deliver, and capture value. Value creation (making the pie bigger) is different from value capture (your slice).
  • Growth levers: more customers, more revenue per customer, retention. Compounding retention beats leaky acquisition.
  • Execution: strategy without execution is a wish; align resources, incentives, and structure to the chosen position. Most strategies fail in execution, not on paper.
Watch out for: (1) Confusing operational effectiveness (doing the same things better) with strategy (doing different things) — best practices spread and erase advantage. (2) Chasing "growth at all costs" with negative unit economics and no moat.

6. Finance

Big idea: Money has a time value and risk has a price; sound finance is about cash flow over time, weighed against risk — not about reported "profit".
  • Three statements: Income statement (profitability over a period), Balance sheet (what you own vs owe at a moment), Cash flow (actual cash in/out). They interlock.
  • Profit ≠ cash. Accrual accounting books revenue when earned; a profitable company can go bankrupt from running out of cash. "Cash is king."
  • Time value of money: a dollar today > a dollar tomorrow. Discount future cash flows to present value (DCF); compounding makes time the investor's biggest lever.
  • Risk and return are linked: higher expected return demands higher risk. Diversification reduces unsystematic (specific) risk for free; systematic (market) risk remains.
  • Valuation: a business is worth the present value of its future cash flows; multiples (P/E, EV/EBITDA) are shorthand, not truth. Price ≠ value.
  • Leverage amplifies returns and losses; debt is cheaper than equity but adds fragility and fixed obligations.
  • Personal finance: spend less than you earn, build an emergency buffer, start compounding early, low-cost diversified index investing beats most active picking.
Watch out for: (1) Reading the income statement and ignoring cash flow — earnings can be managed, cash is harder to fake. (2) Mistaking a low price for a bargain, or recent high returns for low risk; survivorship bias hides the losers.

7. Communication & Writing

Big idea: Writing is thinking made visible; clarity for the reader — not impressing them — is the whole job.
  • Lead with the point (BLUF / pyramid principle): conclusion first, then support. Don't make the reader dig for the "so what".
  • Structure for the reader: one idea per paragraph, logical flow, signposts. Reduce the reader's cognitive load — that's the writer's burden, not theirs.
  • Cut ruthlessly: prefer short words, active voice, concrete nouns and verbs. Delete jargon, hedges, and filler. If a word does no work, remove it.
  • Persuasion = ethos (credibility) + pathos (emotion) + logos (logic). All three; logic alone rarely moves people.
  • Stories beat bullet lists for memory and motivation — concrete, specific, with stakes and a character. Data convinces; stories move.
  • Know your audience & the curse of knowledge: you can't un-know what you know, so you forget what they don't. Write for what they actually know.
  • Difficult conversations: separate observation from interpretation, lead with curiosity, listen to understand; influence depends on the relationship, not just the argument.
Watch out for: (1) The curse of knowledge — assuming the reader shares your context, so you skip the bridge they need. (2) Using complexity and jargon to sound smart; it signals insecurity and loses the reader.

8. Technology (Computing, Software, Internet, Cloud, AI)

Big idea: Computers are layers of abstraction — from bits to programs to global networks to AI — each layer hiding complexity so the one above can be simpler.
  • Bits → logic → CPU: everything is 0s and 1s; the CPU fetches and executes instructions, RAM is fast/volatile working memory, disk is slow/persistent. Code is compiled or interpreted into instructions.
  • Software layers: hardware → OS → application. Abstraction and APIs let you use a layer without knowing its internals.
  • The Internet is packet-switched: data is split into packets, routed independently, reassembled. DNS maps names to IP addresses; HTTP(S) is the web's request/response protocol; client talks to server.
  • APIs are contracts for software to talk to software; databases store structured data — SQL (relational, ACID) vs NoSQL (flexible, scalable).
  • The Cloud = renting someone else's computers on demand; trade-offs of scalability, elasticity, and pay-per-use vs control. IaaS/PaaS/SaaS are levels of "how much they manage for you".
  • Machine learning learns patterns from data instead of being explicitly programmed; it optimizes against a loss function. Training (learn) vs inference (use).
  • AI / LLMs: neural networks; large models predict the next token from patterns in huge text corpora. They are powerful pattern matchers, not understanders — they hallucinate (state false things confidently) and reflect their training data's biases.
Watch out for: (1) Treating an LLM's fluent output as truth — verify facts; confidence ≠ correctness. (2) "Garbage in, garbage out" — a model is only as good, fair, and current as its training data, and it can't reason about what it never saw.

9. History

Big idea: History isn't a list of dates — it's the disciplined interpretation of incomplete evidence about why things happened, so we can read the present more wisely.
  • Sources: primary (first-hand) vs secondary (interpretation). Every source has a perspective, audience, and bias — ask who made it, when, why, and what's missing.
  • Forces that shaped the modern world: agriculture → cities/states, the printing press, the Scientific Revolution and Enlightenment, the Industrial Revolution, nation-states, colonialism, and globalization.
  • Cause is multi-layered: long-term structural causes, medium-term, and short-term triggers. Big events rarely have one cause.
  • Geography, technology, disease, and institutions shape outcomes as much as great individuals (guns, germs, steel; institutions matter).
  • Patterns & cycles: rise and fall of powers, recurring tensions (centralization vs freedom, debt cycles) — but recurrence is not destiny.
  • Contingency: outcomes weren't inevitable; small chances mattered. Avoid reading the present back as the only possible result.
  • Use of history: it gives base rates and analogies, sharpens judgment, and inoculates against "this time it's totally new" thinking.
Watch out for: (1) Presentism and hindsight bias — judging the past by today's values and assuming people then "should have known" what we now know. (2) Single-cause and "great man" explanations, and trusting a vivid source without checking its bias.

10. Decision Science

Big idea: Good decisions are about good process under uncertainty, judged before the outcome is known — not by whether you got lucky.
  • Separate decision quality from outcome quality ("resulting"): a sound decision can have a bad outcome and a reckless one a good outcome. Judge the process.
  • Expected value (EV) = Σ (probability × payoff). Decide by EV for repeated bets; for one-off, irreversible, ruin-risk bets, weigh downside heavily (don't risk ruin even at positive EV).
  • Tools: decision trees map options, probabilities, and payoffs; models simplify reality to compare choices. All models are wrong; some are useful.
  • Think in probabilities, not certainties — give ranges and confidence, update with evidence (Bayesian), and keep a forecasting track record (calibration).
  • Reversibility matters: for cheap, reversible "two-way doors", decide fast; for costly, irreversible "one-way doors", slow down and gather information.
  • Game theory: outcomes depend on others' choices — anticipate their best responses; prisoner's dilemma, Nash equilibrium, cooperation via repeated games.
  • Bias-proofing: use checklists, base rates (outside view), pre-mortems, devil's advocates, and decision journals to counter overconfidence and motivated reasoning.
Watch out for: (1) Resulting — rewarding/punishing decisions by their outcome and letting luck masquerade as skill. (2) Anchoring on the inside view and your first estimate; ignoring base rates and the cost of irreversibility.
One-line spine for all ten: See structure not events (Systems) · weigh trade-offs at the margin (Economics) · distrust your fast brain (Psychology) · ask what the number hides (Statistics) · be deliberately different and defended (Strategy) · follow the cash over time vs risk (Finance) · lighten the reader's load (Communication) · everything is layered abstraction (Technology) · interrogate the source and the cause (History) · judge the process, not the result (Decision Science).
