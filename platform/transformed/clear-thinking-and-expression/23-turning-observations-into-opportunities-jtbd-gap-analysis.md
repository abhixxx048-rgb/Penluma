You already notice things. A coworker keeps customer phone numbers on a sticky note. A bakery scribbles cake orders on paper and loses one now and then. A neighbor re-types the same lesson reminders every single evening.

The hard part isn't noticing. It's the next step: turning "huh, that's interesting" into "here's a real opportunity I could act on." This article hands you a repeatable bridge from one to the other.

## Why this matters

Most observations die quietly. You notice a problem, feel a flicker of annoyance, and move on. By lunchtime it's gone.

The thought evaporates because you have no **container** to put it in. Your brain needs a structure, a question to answer, or the idea just floats away.

The two tools below are containers. They catch a raw observation and force it to become something usable. Learn them and you stop wasting the dozens of small insights that drift past you every week. You start collecting opportunities other people never even write down.

First, two words worth keeping straight:

- **Observation** is a plain fact you noticed. "My coworker keeps a sticky note of customer numbers on her monitor."
- **Opportunity** is a described gap between how things are and how they could be, that someone would pay to close. Pay in money, time, or attention.

An opportunity is not a random idea. It's a **specific job someone is struggling to get done**, plus evidence the struggle is real. Find the struggle and you've found the opportunity.

## People "hire" things to do a job

[Jobs-To-Be-Done (JTBD)](/blog/product-sense-empathy/04-jobs-to-be-done-people-hire-products-to-make-progress) is a way of thinking made popular by Harvard professor Clayton Christensen. The core idea is simple: people don't really want a product. They "hire" it to make progress on a **job** in their life.

The famous example is his milkshake study. A fast-food chain wanted to sell more milkshakes. Surveys about flavor and thickness went nowhere. But when researchers watched *when* people actually bought them, they found something odd: many were sold early in the morning, to commuters.

The job? "Keep me occupied and full during a long, boring drive." A milkshake did that job better than a banana (gone too fast) or a bagel (messy and crumbly behind the wheel).

So the trick is to ask one question:

> **What is this person actually trying to accomplish?**

The product they use today is just their current best guess. If something does the job better, cheaper, or with less hassle, they switch.

**A small example.** You walk into a co-working space and see printed Wi-Fi passwords taped to the wall. The product is "tape and paper." The *job* is "let a new visitor get online in ten seconds without bothering staff." Once you see the job, you can picture a dozen better ways to do it: a QR code card, a tablet at the front desk, a printed welcome sheet. The job, not the tape, is the opportunity.

## Spotting jobs in the wild: look for friction and workarounds

You don't find jobs by asking people "what do you want?" People are surprisingly bad at answering that. You find jobs by watching for **signals of struggle**. These are gold:

- **Workarounds** (sticky notes, spreadsheets, duct tape) - the official tool isn't doing the job, so people patch it themselves. This is the single strongest signal there is.
- **Complaints** ("I hate that I have to...") - a job is being done badly, and the pain is named for you.
- **Repeated effort** (doing the same fiddly thing every week) - a job done often means high value if you [remove the friction](/blog/product-sense-empathy/09-reducing-friction-flows-steps-progressive-disclosure).
- **"Good enough" sighs** (people quietly settling) - an underserved job, tolerated but not solved.
- **Money and time spent** (hiring help, buying gadgets) - proof the job matters enough to pay for.

Two terms to lock in:

- **Friction** is anything that makes a task slower, harder, or more annoying than it needs to be.
- **Workaround** is a makeshift fix people invent because no good tool exists.

### Try this: keep a friction log

For the next two days, carry a "friction log." Every time you or someone near you grumbles, improvises, or does something twice, jot one line:

> *Who + what they were trying to do + the annoyance.*

Aim for ten entries. That's it. You're now collecting raw opportunities, and most people never write down a single one.

## Gap analysis: where things are vs. where they could be

Gap analysis is even simpler than JTBD. You name two things, and look at the space between them.

```
CURRENT STATE  ──────[ THE GAP ]──────►  DESIRED STATE
"how it is now"                          "how it could be"

The opportunity lives INSIDE the gap.
```

Write down where things stand today. Then write where the person *wishes* they stood. The space between is your opportunity. The wider and more painful the gap, the bigger the prize.

**An example.** Current state: a small bakery takes cake orders by phone, writes them on paper, and occasionally loses one. Desired state: every order captured correctly, with a reminder before pickup, and zero lost. The gap (lost orders, no reminders) is the opportunity, whether the fix turns out to be a cheap form, a shared calendar, or a simple app.

## Common misconceptions

**"A good opportunity starts with a great idea."** Reality: it starts with a struggle. Ideas are cheap and most don't connect to anyone's real pain. Hunt for the job first, and the ideas come later, better aimed.

**"Just ask people what they want."** Reality: people describe the solution they already imagine, not the job underneath. Watch what they *do* (the workarounds, the sighs) rather than trusting what they *say*.

**"They need an app!"** Reality: jumping straight to a solution before naming the job and the gap is the most common mistake there is. If you lead with the solution, you'll build something nobody hired. Always go job → gap → *then* [brainstorm fixes](/blog/clear-thinking-and-expression/14-generating-ideas-on-demand-divergent-thinking-scamper-brainstorming).

**"If the problem were real, someone would've solved it already."** Reality: most good gaps have existed for years. What changes is the *timing* - a new tool, a falling cost, a shift in behavior that suddenly makes the gap solvable.

## The four-part filter: is this opportunity real?

Not every gap is worth chasing. Run each idea through four questions. If you can't answer one, that's your next thing to research, not a reason to quit.

1. **Problem** - what's the struggle, stated plainly? Can you describe it in one sentence a stranger would understand?
2. **Who** - exactly who has it? Aim for a specific group ("freelance bakers"), not "everyone."
3. **How big** - how many people, how painful, how often? Frequent plus painful plus many people equals strong.
4. **Why now** - what changed that makes this solvable or valuable today? A new trend, tool, cost drop, or rule that opens a door.

That last one, **"why now,"** is the question beginners skip and experts obsess over. Most good gaps existed for years. The real question is what recently shifted to make solving it possible or urgent.

## From trend to thesis: turning "what's happening" into "what becomes valuable"

A **[trend](/blog/clear-thinking-and-expression/22-spotting-patterns-trends-anomalies-signals)** is a direction the world is moving in. A **thesis** is a clear bet you make because of it. A thesis is a short, testable claim that links a cause (the trend) to an effect (new value).

Use this exact sentence shape:

```
"Because [X is happening],
 [Y] becomes more valuable,
 so [opportunity Z] is worth pursuing."
```

**An example.** "Because more small shops now sell online (trend), *looking professional cheaply* becomes more valuable (effect), so a tool that turns a phone photo into a clean product listing is worth pursuing (opportunity)." Notice the chain: a real-world change → a specific need → a concrete idea. That chain is what makes the idea persuasive when you say it out loud.

This is also how you sound sharp in conversation. Instead of "AI is big these days," you offer a thesis: "Because AI now writes decent first drafts, the bottleneck shifts to *editing judgment* - so people who can quickly tell good from bad become more valuable." Same fact, but you've connected it and made a point. That's [critical thinking made visible](/blog/clear-thinking-and-expression/17-critical-thinking-foundations-claims-evidence-assumptions).

## How to use this: one observation, end to end

Let's run the whole pipeline on a single small thing you might notice. Follow these steps in order.

1. **Observation.** A neighbor who tutors kids keeps re-typing the same lesson reminders into WhatsApp every evening.
2. **Job (JTBD).** "Make sure each student shows up prepared, without me spending my evening copy-pasting." The product they hired (manual WhatsApp) does the job badly.
3. **Friction / workaround.** Re-typing the same thing nightly is repeated effort plus a clear grumble. Strong signal.
4. **Gap.** Current: manual messages, 30 minutes a night, sometimes forgotten. Desired: reminders go out automatically, never missed, near-zero effort.
5. **Four-part filter.** Problem = wasted nightly time. Who = independent tutors. How big = many tutors, daily pain, hours per week. Why now = cheap messaging tools and automations are easy to set up today.
6. **Thesis.** "Because solo tutors now run real businesses from their phones, *saving their admin time* becomes valuable, so a simple scheduled-reminder helper is worth exploring."

You started with a neighbor's mild annoyance and ended with a defined, defensible opportunity, using only the structures above. That's the whole skill.

### Practice on your own

- **Job-finder.** Take one product you used today (an app, a tool, a snack). Fill in: "I hired ___ to ___." Then name one thing that could do that job better.
- **Gap pair.** Pick a situation that annoyed you this week. Write its current state and desired state in two lines. Name the gap in one phrase.
- **Run the filter.** Take one entry from your friction log and answer all four questions. Mark the answer you're least sure of - that's your next research step.
- **Write a thesis.** Pick any trend you've heard about lately and fill in the sentence shape. Say it out loud once.

## Conclusion

The pipeline never changes: **Observation → Job → Friction → Gap → Filter → Thesis.** Memorize that chain and you can manufacture opportunities on demand, from things you'd otherwise walk past.

Here's the quiet truth underneath all of it: opportunities aren't rare. They're everywhere, hiding inside every sticky note and workaround and tired sigh. What's rare is a person carrying the right container to catch them.

And once you can name a real opportunity, a new question shows up: how do you test whether it's actually worth building before you sink a month into it? That's where the [smallest possible experiment](/blog/how-to-make-money/11-finding-a-real-problem-validating-demand), the kind that gives you an answer in a day, becomes your next best tool.
