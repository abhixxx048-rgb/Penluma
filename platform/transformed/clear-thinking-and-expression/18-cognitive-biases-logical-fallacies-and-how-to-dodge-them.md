Your brain is fast. And fast usually means sloppy.

Right now, dozens of times a day, your mind is taking shortcuts that feel exactly like clear thinking, but quietly bend the truth. The unsettling part is that [smart people fall for them just as easily](/blog/economics-from-first-principles/31-behavioral-economics-how-psychology-shapes-the-economy) as everyone else. Sometimes more, because they're better at building convincing reasons for whatever they already believed.

This is a field guide to the most common shortcuts that fool people, and the most common tricks that fool people in arguments, plus a few simple moves to catch all of them.

## Why this matters

Every belief you hold, every decision you make, and every argument you find convincing passes through the same flawed equipment. When that equipment misfires, you don't feel it misfiring. You feel certain.

That's the trap. The error and the confidence arrive together.

The cost is real: you buy things you didn't need, you cling to projects that aren't working, you lose debates you should win, and you trust ideas that quietly let you down. The good news is that these errors are **predictable**. They have names. And once you can name the moment one is firing, you can stop it.

## Two thinking speeds, and why you slip

Psychologist Daniel Kahneman, in his book *Thinking, Fast and Slow*, described two modes your mind runs in. Think of them as two different employees sharing one desk.

- **System 1** is fast, automatic, and effortless. It guesses, jumps to conclusions, and runs on gut feeling. It's the part that yanks your hand off a hot stove before you've finished the thought "that's hot."
- **System 2** is slow, effortful, and deliberate. It checks, calculates, and [weighs evidence](/blog/clear-thinking-and-expression/17-critical-thinking-foundations-claims-evidence-assumptions). It's the part you use to work out whether a deal is actually a good price.

System 1 is on all day. System 2 is lazy and expensive to run, so your mind avoids waking it unless it has to.

Here's the key. **Most bad thinking is just System 1 answering a hard question before System 2 even wakes up.** A cognitive bias is a repeating, predictable error in how the fast system sees the world. A logical fallacy is a flaw in an argument, where the reasoning looks valid but isn't.

The simplest way to keep them apart: **biases happen inside one head, fallacies happen in the back-and-forth between people.**

You cannot delete biases. They're built in. But you can learn to feel the moment one fires and switch the slow system on by hand.

## The biases worth knowing

Here are the big ones. Read each name slowly, because naming a bias is the first real step to spotting it in the wild.

### Confirmation bias
You notice evidence that agrees with you and quietly ignore the rest. You decide a coworker is lazy, so you only remember the times they slacked off, never the times they stayed late. This is the engine behind most of the others.

### Anchoring
The first number you hear drags every later judgment toward it. A shirt tagged "₹3000, now ₹1200" feels like a steal, but only because [the ₹3000 anchored you](/blog/business-financial-literacy/09-pricing-psychology-pricing-with-confidence). Cover the original price and the ₹1200 might feel like plenty.

### Availability bias
Events that are easy to recall feel more common than they actually are. After a plane crash dominates the news, flying *feels* risky, even though driving to the airport was the dangerous part of the trip.

### Survivorship bias
You study the winners and never see the failures, because the failures vanished. "College dropouts get rich!" ignores the millions who dropped out and didn't. You only hear from the ones the story worked out for.

### Sunk cost fallacy
You keep going because of what you already spent, not because it's still worth it. Finishing a boring movie "because I paid for the ticket" is the harmless version. Pouring another year into a failing project is the expensive one. The money and time are already gone either way.

### Dunning-Kruger effect
Beginners overrate their own skill, because they don't yet know enough to see their gaps. After one tutorial you feel like an expert. Then reality, gently or otherwise, humbles you.

### Hindsight bias
Once something has happened, you feel you knew it all along. "That team was obviously going to lose," a thing nobody says until *after* the team loses. It quietly rewrites how predictable the past really was.

**Watch how they stack.** A friend says, "Successful founders all wake at 5 a.m., so I should too." That's survivorship bias, because they ignored every 5 a.m. riser who failed. It's also a hint of mistaking correlation for cause, since waking early may not *cause* anything. Two errors in one sentence, and both sounded perfectly reasonable.

## The fallacies worth knowing

These are flaws in arguments. Learn them and you can defend yourself in any debate, comment thread, or sales pitch.

### Straw man
Someone twists your view into a weaker, more extreme version, then attacks that version instead. You say "maybe some gun rules," they reply "so you want to ban all guns." They're fighting a scarecrow they built, not you.

### Ad hominem
Attacking the person instead of the argument. "You can't trust her data, she's young." Her age has nothing to do with whether the numbers are right.

### False dichotomy
Pretending only two choices exist when more do. "Either we cut salaries or we go bankrupt." Almost always there's a third path, and a fourth, that the speaker would rather you didn't notice.

### Correlation is not causation
Two things move together, so one supposedly causes the other. Ice cream sales and drownings rise at the same time, but ice cream isn't the killer. Hot weather drives both.

### Appeal to authority
"An expert said it," used as proof all by itself. A famous doctor endorsing a skincare cream is not evidence the cream works.

### Slippery slope
Claiming one small step must lead to an extreme end, with nothing to back the chain. "If we allow remote Fridays, soon nobody comes in at all." Maybe. But "maybe" is not "must."

## Common misconceptions

A few traps catch even careful people:

- **"Appeal to authority is always a fallacy."** Not true. It's only a fallacy when authority *replaces* evidence, or when the expert is speaking outside their field. A cardiologist on heart health is excellent evidence. The same cardiologist on cryptocurrency is just a person with an opinion.
- **"Spotting fallacies wins arguments."** Yelling "straw man!" at someone is not the same as making a point. The goal is clearer thinking, not a debate scoreboard.
- **"Biases only happen to other people."** This belief is itself a bias (it even has a name, the bias blind spot). The feeling that you're the rational one is the surest sign you should slow down.
- **"Smarter people are less biased."** Often the reverse. Intelligence mostly makes you better at *justifying* the conclusion your fast brain already picked.

## How to use this: your three defenses

You don't need to memorize twenty traps in the heat of the moment. You need three reliable moves that beat most of them.

1. **Slow down.** When a claim makes you feel instantly sure or excited, that rush is System 1 talking. Pause and ask, "Is this a quick gut answer, or have I actually checked?" The feeling of certainty is not evidence. It just feels like it.
2. **Seek disconfirming evidence.** Confirmation bias makes you hunt for "yes." Flip it. Ask, "What facts would prove me *wrong*?" Then go look for them on purpose. Find none, and your idea is stronger. Find plenty, and you just dodged a mistake.
3. **Consider the opposite.** Researchers Lord, Lepper, and colleagues found that simply asking people to argue the *other* side reduced biased judgment. Before you decide, spend two minutes [building the strongest possible case against your own view](/blog/clear-thinking-and-expression/19-reasoning-well-deduction-induction-abduction-steelmanning).

Think of biases as your brain's autocorrect. Usually fast and helpful, but every so often it confidently "fixes" something you never meant to say. You don't switch autocorrect off. You learn to glance back and re-read before you hit send.

### Turn it on your own ideas

Here's the move most people skip. When you read something and feel a warm "yes, exactly right," that glow is a confirmation-bias signal. The piece matched what you already believed, so you trusted it more.

So train yourself to **pause hardest on what you most agree with.** When you come up with an idea of your own, run it through the three defenses before you fall in love with it. Ideas that survive "what would prove this wrong?" are the ones worth speaking and writing about, and you'll defend them far more clearly, because you already know exactly where their weak spots are.

### Four drills to build the habit

1. **Bias-spotting feed.** Scroll your news or social feed for five minutes. Write down three claims and label the bias or fallacy each one leans on.
2. **Anchor test.** Next time you shop, cover the "original" price and ask, "What would I pay if I'd never seen that number?" The gap you feel was anchoring at work.
3. **Steel-man drill.** Pick a view you disagree with. Write the strongest, fairest version of it in three sentences. If you can't, you don't yet understand it well enough to argue against it.
4. **Slow-down catch.** For one whole day, every time you feel instant certainty, say "System 1" silently and re-check once. Tally how often the recheck changed your answer. The number tends to surprise people.

## Conclusion

If you remember one thing, remember this: **the feeling of being right and the fact of being right are two different things, and your fast brain serves up the feeling first.** Name the trap, slow down, and ask "what would prove me wrong?" Those three moves quietly defuse most biases and fallacies you'll ever meet.

There's a strange next layer to all this. Once you start catching your own biases, you may notice the people best at *persuading* you are the ones who know these shortcuts cold and aim them at your fast brain on purpose. The same map that protects your thinking is also the map a good salesperson, speechwriter, or negotiator works from. Worth knowing which side of that you want to be on.
