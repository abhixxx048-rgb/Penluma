**This document is a practical guide to understanding who Print-Flow-360 is really competing against - and how to keep track of that over time. It matters because if you don't know why a store owner chose someone else (or chose nothing at all), you'll keep losing deals for reasons you never see coming.**

**The main parts explained simply:**

- **Four types of competitors** - Your real competition is not just other print software. It falls into four groups: (1) full print platforms like OnPrintShop and DesignNBuy; (2) the "DIY stack" - store owners who use Shopify or Wix and email artwork manually; (3) old-school print management software (PrintSmith, Tharstern) that runs the shop floor; and (4) "do nothing" - the owner who sticks with email and spreadsheets. Group 4 is your biggest competitor. Most deals are lost there, not to a named rival.

- **Six things to watch** - Good competitive research looks at more than just features. You also need to track pricing, how competitors talk about themselves, how they find customers, what real users say in reviews, and whether they are hiring or raising money. This document tells you which of these to focus on first.

- **Where to get information (ethically)** - Free sources like Google Alerts, review sites (G2, Capterra, Trustpilot), competitor pricing pages, job postings, and Reddit print-owner groups give you most of what you need. The highest-value activity: read the 1–3 star reviews of competitors to find out what their own customers hate about them.

- **Mining reviews for ammunition** - Bad reviews of competitors are objections you haven't heard yet. The document gives you a simple tagging system to count complaints by theme (too hard to set up, bad support, clunky design tool, etc.). When 30+ reviews show the same pattern, that's a real weakness you can use in your sales conversations.

- **The feature comparison grid** - A simple table showing which platform does what. More importantly, it honestly marks where Print-Flow-360 has gaps today (preflight file checking, partial order fulfilment, live shipping tracking) so you know what to defend against.

- **Honest gap defense** - Every product has things it can't do yet. This section gives you a ready-made, honest answer for each known gap: acknowledge it plainly, explain why it usually doesn't matter for a small shop owner, state the plan, and redirect to what you do better. Pretending gaps don't exist breaks trust with non-technical buyers.

- **Battlecards** - A one-page cheat sheet for each competitor type. The format is: here is a true fact, here is why that matters to the store owner, here is what to say or show. Keep it to one page - long documents get ignored. Build four cards total, one for each competitor group.

- **A weekly/monthly/quarterly routine** - Competitive research only helps if you do it regularly. The document gives you a simple checklist: 15 minutes a week to clear alerts, 90 minutes a month to update the cards, and a half-day each quarter to check whether anything big has changed.

**What to do with this:** Start by reading the 1–3 star reviews of OnPrintShop and DesignNBuy on G2 or Capterra - count how many complain about being too hard to set up. If that theme dominates, your "easy for non-technical owners" message is validated by their own customers' words. Then spend one hour writing down an honest answer for each of your three known gaps (preflight, partial fulfilment, shipping tracking) before a prospect catches you off guard.
