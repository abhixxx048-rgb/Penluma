You pick a price. It feels about right. You build the pricing page, wire up the billing, write the sales script, and move on.

Months later you realize that "about right" cost you half your revenue, or worse, scared off the only customers who were ever going to say yes. The number was a guess wearing a confident smile.

Here's the good news: you don't have to guess. A handful of simple, well-tested methods let you ask real customers a few plain questions and read the answer straight off a chart. This is what pricing research actually is, and most of it fits in a spreadsheet.

## Why this matters

For an early product, pricing on a hunch is the most expensive mistake you can make.

The question is never "what is my software worth in some abstract sense." It's much more concrete: **what number makes a real buyer say yes without a long fight, and at what number do they walk away?**

At this stage you're not chasing precision to the dollar. You're hunting for two things:

- The **acceptable range** of prices your market will tolerate.
- The **value metric**, meaning the thing you charge per (per user? per order? per location?).

Get those two right and every other lever you'll pull later, like trials, bundles, and upsells, has room to work. Get them wrong and you either leave a fat slice of revenue on the table or price yourself out of the exact group that was willing to talk to you.

To keep this concrete, we'll follow a running example: a small software product sold to local print-shop owners, the kind of non-technical business owner who today pays maybe thirty to fifty dollars a month for a generic website and juggles the rest in spreadsheets. Call our typical buyer Maria. Everything below is a tool for figuring out what Maria will pay.

## The single best pricing question costs nothing

Before any survey, remember this: **what people say they'll pay is the weakest evidence there is.** People over-promise in surveys because saying yes is free.

A real click on a priced "start trial" button, a real credit card, or a real "that's reasonable" in a live conversation is worth ten survey answers. So lean on **revealed** behavior first, and use surveys to fill in the picture.

With that warning in place, let's look at the methods, from the lightest to the heaviest.

## Van Westendorp: find the acceptable range

The Van Westendorp Price Sensitivity Meter, named after the Dutch economist who created it, is the workhorse for early-stage pricing. It asks each person just four questions about price.

What makes it clever is that it treats **price as a signal of quality**, not just cost. That catches the "too cheap to be any good" effect that plain "what would you pay?" surveys miss entirely. A print storefront priced at nine dollars a month would read as a toy to a serious shop owner, and this method picks that up.

### The four questions

Ask each person, about your product:

1. **Too expensive:** At what monthly price would this be *so expensive* you wouldn't consider it?
2. **Too cheap:** At what monthly price would this be *so cheap* you'd doubt it could really do the job?
3. **Getting pricey:** At what price would it *start to feel expensive*, where you'd have to think hard before buying?
4. **A bargain:** At what price would it feel like a *great deal*?

A sensible person answers these in a logical order: Too Cheap is below Bargain, which is below Expensive, which is below Too Expensive. If someone's answers break that order, you re-ask or drop them. Expect to throw out a slice of raw responses this way, which is normal.

### How to read it

For each of the four questions, you build a running tally across the price axis: at each price, what share of people put their threshold there or beyond. Plot all four as lines on one chart and they cross at four meaningful spots.

- **Floor of the acceptable range:** below this, "too cheap to trust" starts to outweigh "getting pricey." Price below it and you signal *toy*.
- **Ceiling of the acceptable range:** above this, "too expensive" outweighs "bargain." Price above it and you start losing people on sticker shock.
- **Lowest-resistance point:** where equal shares reject the price as too cheap and too expensive. A common launch anchor.
- **Perceived-normal point:** where equal shares call it cheap and expensive. This is where the "median" buyer feels the price sits, and often where market leaders land.

The span between the floor and ceiling is your **range of acceptable pricing**. You price inside it.

### A worked example

Imagine twelve shop owners answer the four questions. After plotting, the crossings land roughly like this:

- **Floor:** about $76. Below this, people get suspicious.
- **Lowest-resistance:** about $84.
- **Perceived-normal:** about $92.
- **Ceiling:** about $102.

So the acceptable range is roughly **$76 to $102**, with the sweet spot near $90. That's a clean skeleton for three tiers:

- A **headline tier at $99**, a hair under the ceiling. The classic "anchor high, then offer a discount on annual" move.
- A cheaper **entry tier** below the floor, but *only* as a deliberate land-and-expand play, fenced with fewer features so it doesn't drag the brand down to "cheap and nasty."
- A **premium tier above the range**, sold on capability to buyers who shop on features, not price.

Notice the lowest-resistance point ($84) sits below the perceived-normal point ($92). That small detail tells you this market leans slightly cost-conscious, which fits a non-technical owner replacing a cheap website. The takeaway: lead your messaging with value and return on investment, not a feature list.

### The one thing Van Westendorp can't tell you

It reveals which prices are *acceptable*. It says **nothing about how many people will actually buy** or how much money you'll make. There's no demand or volume dimension at all.

That's exactly why you pair it with the next tool.

## Gabor-Granger: find the revenue-maximizing price

Gabor-Granger fills the hole Van Westendorp leaves. You show a person one concrete price and ask how likely they are to buy. If they're keen, you show a higher price. If not, a lower one. You ladder up and down until you find their personal ceiling.

Aggregate across everyone and you get two things:

- A **demand curve**: the share who'd buy at each price.
- A **revenue curve**: demand multiplied by price, whose peak is your revenue-maximizing price.

### A worked example

Suppose you ladder six candidate prices past 80 people, take the share who say "probably" or "definitely would buy," and then **cut those numbers in half** to correct for the fact that people over-claim in surveys. Against an imagined pool of 1,000 shops:

| Price/mo | Honest take-rate | Buyers | Relative revenue |
|---------:|-----------------:|-------:|-----------------:|
| $29  | 44% | 440 | $12,760 |
| $49  | 38% | 375 | $18,375 |
| $79  | 28% | 280 | $22,120 |
| **$99**  | **23%** | **225** | **$22,275** (peak) |
| $129 | 15% | 150 | $19,350 |
| $199 | 8%  | 80  | $15,920 |

Revenue peaks at **$99**, just edging out $79. That lines up beautifully with the Van Westendorp read.

There's a deeper signal here too. Between $79 and $99, demand barely softens while price jumps 25%, so you can raise the price and *gain* revenue. But push past $99 and demand falls off a cliff faster than price climbs, so you start *losing* money. That's a hard, quantitative argument for parking your headline tier right at $99, snug under the ceiling.

### The catch

Gabor-Granger only tests the prices *you* choose, so a badly chosen ladder hides the real best price. Bracket your ladder using the Van Westendorp range. And like all surveys, it measures stated intent, so trust the *shape* of the curve, not the exact take-rates.

## Conjoint analysis: the precise tool, for later

Conjoint analysis is the heavyweight. It shows people bundles of features at different levels (with price as one of those features) and forces them to choose between bundles. From the pattern of choices, it works out the dollar value of every single feature.

That makes it the gold standard for **packaging and feature-value** decisions. It can tell you something like "a corporate-accounts add-on is worth $60 a month to buyers, but a full design studio is worth $110." That's pure gold for deciding what to build and how to bundle it.

So why not use it now? Because it's expensive in exactly the things an early team is short on:

- It needs a **stable, well-understood feature set**, and you're still discovering which features close deals.
- It needs **200 to 300 or more** qualified respondents, which you can't reach yet.
- It needs real money and time to run and analyze.

Spending founder attention here before product-market fit is premature optimization. **Park it.** Once you have a few dozen paying customers and a settled feature menu, a price-friendly variant of conjoint is the right call. Until then, a lighter method called MaxDiff (which ranks features without involving price) is a cheaper way to learn what to *lead with* in your messaging.

## Common misconceptions

**"I'll just ask people what they'd pay."** A single "what would you pay?" question is the worst of all worlds. It ignores the too-cheap signal, invites lowball answers, and gives you one number with no range. The four-question structure exists precisely because the naive question fails.

**"More precise is always better."** Conjoint is more precise than Van Westendorp, but precision you can't act on, built from a sample you can't reach, is worthless. Match the method to your stage.

**"The survey number is the price."** No survey gives you a price to copy onto your checkout page. It gives you a range and a direction. You confirm with real behavior.

**"Cheaper always sells more."** Below the floor, lower prices actively *cost* you sales because buyers stop trusting the product. Cheap reads as toy, and toys don't replace a business's revenue engine.

**"The value metric is a detail."** It's at least as important as the number. Charging per order can feel like a tax on a customer's own success. Charging per user breaks when their team size swings. The right unit should match how the customer naturally thinks about their business.

## How to use this

Here's a concrete sequence for an early-stage product, lightest and cheapest first.

1. **This week, run a fake-door price test.** Put up a real pricing page with a live trial button and split your traffic across two or three prices (say $49, $99, $149). Watch which one holds clicks. If the $99 page keeps clicks within about 20% of the $49 page, you're underpricing at $49.

2. **This week, anchor ten sales calls.** Before stating your price, get the buyer to put a dollar figure on their pain ("how much is all that manual quoting costing you a month?"). Then state your price as a fraction of that number and listen. "That's nothing" means test higher. A thoughtful pause means you're near their ceiling, which is good. An immediate flinch means you're over the line or the value isn't landing. Log every reaction.

3. **Over the next few weeks, run a Van Westendorp survey.** You only need about 30 to 50 responses from real decision-makers for a directional read. Use open numeric entry, never a dropdown, because a dropdown anchors people to your numbers. Build the chart in a spreadsheet and read off your floor, ceiling, and sweet spot.

4. **Right after, run Gabor-Granger.** Use your Van Westendorp range to bracket the price ladder, then ladder past your respondents to find the revenue peak. Apply an honest haircut to the take-rates.

5. **Ask your early users the disappointment question.** "How would you feel if you could no longer use this?" If at least 40% say "very disappointed," that's a strong product-market-fit signal. Then ask that segment, and only that segment, what monthly price would feel fair. Their answers are the ones that matter.

6. **Price on value, not on your costs.** Anchor every conversation on the labor and lost sales you replace, never on your hosting bill. If a $99 plan saves a customer six hours a week of work, the price is a rounding error against what that time costs them.

7. **Don't hire a research vendor yet.** Spend the saved time and money closing your next five customers.

## Conclusion

If you remember one thing, make it this: **pricing research isn't about finding a perfect number. It's about finding the acceptable range and the right thing to charge per, then confirming with real behavior.** Van Westendorp draws the box, Gabor-Granger finds the peak inside it, and a live trial button keeps everyone honest.

The number you land on is only half the story, though. The other half is your **value metric**, the unit you charge per, because that quiet choice shapes how fair your price feels and how your revenue grows as your customers grow. Charge per the wrong thing and even a perfect price will feel like a penalty. That's the question worth chasing next.
