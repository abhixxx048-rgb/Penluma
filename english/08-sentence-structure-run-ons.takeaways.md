**This document teaches you how to write clear, complete sentences — no more long chains of words with no full stops. If you often write one long line where three or four sentences should be, this is for you. Fixing this makes your messages, notes, and updates much easier for others to read and understand.**

**The main parts explained simply:**

- **A complete sentence** — Every sentence needs three things: who or what (subject), what they do (verb), and a finished thought. "The build failed." is complete. "the build failed i pushed a fix" is two sentences squashed into one — that is a problem.

- **Run-on sentence** — When you write two full thoughts with no break between them, that is called a run-on. It is like speaking without pausing for breath. Example: "the build failed i pushed a fix" — needs a full stop in the middle.

- **Comma splice** — When you put two full sentences together with only a comma, that is also wrong. A comma alone is too weak to join two sentences. "the tests pass, the deploy is green" needs a full stop, not just a comma.

- **Four correct ways to join or split sentences** — You have four safe choices: (1) use a full stop to make two separate sentences, (2) use a semicolon when the two ideas are very close, (3) use a comma plus a joining word like "and", "but", or "so", (4) use a connecting word like "because", "when", or "if" to link the ideas.

- **Fragments** — A fragment is a sentence that is not finished. "Because the token expired." sounds like it is waiting for more. It needs to attach to another sentence: "The request failed because the token expired."

- **Daily writing practice** — The document shows how these rules apply to real work: writing commit messages, pull request descriptions, Slack standups, code comments, and bug reports. Short, clear sentences help people read faster and miss fewer things.

- **Drills and answer key** — The document ends with 12 practice exercises and full answers so you can test yourself and check your work right away.

**What to do with this:** When you finish writing a message or note, read it aloud. Every time you find two full ideas with no break between them, add a full stop or a joining word. Remember: a comma alone cannot join two sentences — always add "and", "but", or "so" after it.
