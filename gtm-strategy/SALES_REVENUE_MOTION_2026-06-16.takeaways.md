**This document explains the best way to turn print-shop owners who sign up for a trial into paying customers — without hiring a sales team. It matters because getting this wrong means spending money and time on the wrong things while most trial users quietly walk away.**

**The main parts explained simply:**

- **Who is buying and what that means** — The buyer is a busy, non-technical print-shop owner who decides alone, often on their phone between jobs. Because the monthly price is low (roughly $30–100/month), the math makes it impossible to hire a dedicated salesperson. Every rule in this document flows from that single fact.

- **Self-serve first, founder steps in only for hot leads** — The product itself does most of the selling. The founder only reaches out personally when a strong signal fires — for example, a shop owner's store just went live or they placed their first test order. Reaching out to everyone wastes the founder's time; reaching out to nobody loses the accounts that would have paid.

- **Product-Qualified Lead (PQL) — what it is and why it matters** — A PQL is a trial user who has already felt real value inside the product (store is live, first order placed). These people convert at 5–10 times the rate of someone who just opened a sign-up email. The PQL list is the only sales list a small team needs.

- **The first 72 hours decide everything** — Research across millions of trial users shows that 60–75% of whether someone eventually pays is decided in the first three days. If an owner hasn't set up a product and placed a test order by day three, they almost certainly never will. All onboarding effort should be front-loaded into that window.

- **How to talk to a shop owner on a call (discovery frameworks)** — The document covers several named selling approaches (SPIN, Sandler, Challenger). The plain-English version: ask the owner to describe how a quote or order works in their shop today, then help them add up what slow quotes and lost jobs cost them each year in their own numbers. Don't pitch features; let them talk themselves into the solution.

- **How to handle common objections** — A table covers the real worry hiding behind each pushback. "Too expensive" usually means "I don't see the value yet." "I'm not technical" means "I'm scared of setup." "Let me think about it" means there's an unsaid worry. The document gives a plain-language response for each.

- **Forecasting without a sales team** — Revenue prediction works as two simple stacks added together: (1) the self-serve run-rate based on recent months, and (2) a hand-written list of the few bigger deals the founder is personally helping. No complicated spreadsheet needed until there are at least 20 closed deals.

- **The reverse trial and why it needs a free tier** — The trial gives owners full access for 14 days, then steps them down to a limited (but still live) free plan instead of cutting them off. This keeps them reachable for a future upgrade. But it only works if a real free tier exists first — limited to roughly 3 products, with a "Powered by" footer badge.

- **Activation checklist and default pricing** — A short (5-step or fewer) setup checklist with a progress bar lifts activation by about 60% on its own. The checklist must end with "place a test order," not an admin task like tax settings. Pricing is the hardest step for non-technical owners, so the sample product should ship with sensible default prices already filled in.

- **Onboarding does not stop at "store published"** — Publishing the store is only the halfway point. The real goal is getting real orders flowing for 2–4 weeks until it becomes a habit. Stores that go live but receive zero real orders almost always churn when the trial ends.

**What to do with this:** Build the capped-but-alive free tier and add default pricing to the sample product — those two things unlock everything else. Then make sure the go-live checklist ends on "place a test order" and set up one automatic alert for when a trial reaches day three with no activity, so the founder can step in before the window closes.
