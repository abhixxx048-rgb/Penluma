A customer wants 12 photo-quality band shirts. Another wants 500 two-color event tees. A third wants their logo on 250 pens. If you printed all three the same way, you would lose money on two of them and probably ruin the third.

Putting a design onto a T-shirt, a mug, or a pen is its own craft, and the print industry calls it **decoration**. There is no single best way to do it. The right method depends on four things, and once you know them, the choice almost makes itself.

## Why this matters

Decoration is where most "custom merch" orders live. Get the method right and the same design can cost half as much and last twice as long.

Here is the core truth: **there is no one best method.** The right choice depends on four levers.

- **How many items** you are making
- **How many colors** the design has
- **What fabric or material** you are printing on
- **Where the design goes** on the product

A good print shop, and good software, picks whatever is cheapest and most durable for that exact combination. Pick by habit instead, and you either overcharge small orders or undercharge big ones.

Think of it like choosing how to travel. A bicycle is perfect for one short trip. A bus is wasteful for one person but the cheapest way to move a hundred people. A motorcycle is flexible on almost any road. And a specialized off-road vehicle is the only thing that can reach places the others physically cannot. Each printing method is one of those vehicles.

## The five ways to put art on stuff

Let's name the players before going deep. Each gets its own section below.

| Method | What it does, in plain words | Best for |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Screen printing** | Pushes thick ink through a stencil, one stencil per color | High-volume shirts and promo, few colors |
| **DTG** (Direct-to-Garment) | An inkjet printer that sprays ink right onto the shirt | Short runs, full-color photo art on cotton |
| **DTF** (Direct-to-Film) | Prints to a film, then heat-presses it onto the item | Short-to-medium runs on any fabric or color |
| **Pad printing** | A soft pad lifts ink and presses it onto curved objects | Pens, mugs, golf balls, hard goods |
| **Embroidery** | Stitches the design with thread, no ink | Logos on caps, polos, premium workwear |

## Screen printing: the volume workhorse

**Screen printing** (also called silkscreen) is the oldest and most common way to decorate apparel in bulk. The idea is simple. Ink is pushed through a fine mesh screen that holds a **stencil**, a mask that blocks ink everywhere except the shape of your design. Ink only reaches the shirt where you want it.

### The one-screen-per-color rule

This single fact drives all of screen-printing economics: **each ink color needs its own screen, its own setup, and its own pass across the shirt.** A four-color design means four screens, four setups, and four layers of ink. More colors means more cost and more time.

A one-color white logo on a navy shirt needs one screen. A four-color band logo needs four. If each screen costs roughly $20 to $75 to make and align, that four-color job starts with $80 to $300 of setup **before a single shirt is printed.** That is why screen printing is brutal for tiny orders and unbeatable for big ones.

### How it actually works

A few terms are worth knowing, because they explain why prints look the way they do.

- **Mesh count** is threads per inch on the screen. A low count like 110 has bigger holes, lays down more ink, and gives a bold, thick result with less fine detail. A high count like 230 or 305 lays down less ink and holds fine lines. Most T-shirt work lives at 110 to 160 mesh.
- **Photo-emulsion stencil**: the screen is coated with a light-sensitive liquid, your artwork on clear film is laid on top, and UV light hardens the coating everywhere except behind the dark artwork. Washing rinses out the soft part, leaving open mesh in the exact shape of your design.
- **Squeegee**: a rubber blade that drags ink across the screen and forces it through the open mesh.
- **Registration**: lining up each color's screen so the colors stack perfectly. Bad registration means blurry, ghosted prints.

### The dark-garment trick

On a dark shirt, colored ink sinks in and looks dull. So printers first lay down a **white underbase**, a layer of white ink shaped like the design that acts like primer and keeps the colors on top bright.

Between layers, a heater called a **flash cure** sets that white just enough to print on top of it. The trick is that the underbase must be flashed partway, not fully cured. Over-flash it and the next color will not stick.

### Two ink families

| | Plastisol (most common) | Water-based / discharge |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Where it sits | On top of the fabric | Soaks into the fibers |
| Feel | Thick, slightly plastic-y | Soft, almost no-feel |
| Look | Vibrant, opaque, bold | Soft, vintage, eco-friendlier |
| Difficulty | Forgiving | More technically demanding |

The make-or-break step is the **cure**. Under-cured ink cracks and washes out after a few washes; over-curing scorches the fabric. Done right, screen prints survive **50 to 100+ washes**, the most durable of all the apparel methods. Always run a wash test before a full run.

**The takeaway:** screen printing has high setup per color but a tiny per-unit cost once running. Expensive for one shirt, dirt-cheap at 100+.

## DTG: a printer for shirts

**DTG** is, in plain words, a printer for shirts. It is an inkjet that sprays water-based ink directly onto the garment in full color, unlimited colors, in a single pass, with no screens at all. If you can print it on paper, you can print it on a shirt.

The process runs in five stages: prep the artwork, **pretreat** the garment with a clear bonding spray, lay a white underbase on dark shirts, jet the color on top, then heat-cure it.

That pretreatment is the make-or-break step. Too little and the print washes out and looks dull. Too much and you get staining around the design.

The other hard limit: DTG only behaves on **cotton**, especially smooth ringspun cotton. On 100% polyester the water-based ink will not bond. Printing poly and expecting cotton results is a classic, costly mistake.

Watch what happens to the price. A typical four-color chest print runs about $12 a shirt at one unit, $10 at 25, and $8 at 100. The per-unit price barely drops, because there is no setup to spread out. That flat curve is the signature of DTG.

The feel is soft because the ink absorbs into the fabric, and prints last roughly **40 to 60 washes**, fading gracefully rather than cracking.

**The takeaway:** DTG is perfect for **1 to about 30 full-color cotton pieces**, samples, and print-on-demand stores with no minimums. It is the wrong choice for big runs (no volume discount) or for polyester.

## DTF: the fast-growing all-rounder

**DTF** flips DTG around. Instead of printing onto the shirt, you print the design, white ink included, onto a sheet of clear **PET film**, coat it with a special powder, melt the powder, then heat-press the film onto the garment and peel it away. The design is made first and applied second.

The steps:

1. Print the design plus white ink onto the PET film.
2. Sprinkle **hot-melt adhesive powder** onto the still-wet ink.
3. Melt the powder so it forms a sticky backing.
4. Heat-press the film onto the item (around 300 to 325°F for about 15 seconds), then peel the film away.

### Why DTF is exploding

- **It works on almost anything**: cotton, polyester, blends, nylon, leather, tote bags, hats, dark or light, with no pretreatment needed.
- **Low setup** compared to screen printing, so small orders are fine.
- **Gang sheets**: you can tile many different designs onto one big film sheet, slashing the cost per design.
- **Stock and apply later**: pre-print transfers, store them, and press on demand.

The finished transfer is thin but sits slightly on top of the fabric, so it has more texture than DTG and a softer feel than thick plastisol. Durability is roughly **30 to 50 washes**, with the main risk being edges cracking or peeling over time, especially if washed too hot.

**The takeaway:** DTF sits between DTG and screen printing on cost but beats DTG on fabric flexibility. Best for **short-to-medium runs under about 100 units** on mixed or non-cotton materials. Above 100 of the same simple design, screen printing wins again.

## Pad printing: for curved and odd objects

Screen printing, DTG, and DTF all need a roughly flat surface. So how do you put a logo on a pen, a golf ball, or the curved rim of a mug? That is **pad printing**. It is an indirect method: a soft silicone pad lifts a flat image and presses it onto a 3D or irregular surface.

It has three key parts:

- **Cliché**: a metal plate with the design etched into it. The recesses hold the ink.
- **Ink cup**: floods the plate with ink, then a blade scrapes the surface clean so ink stays only inside the etched design.
- **Silicone pad**: soft and squishy, it presses onto the plate, picks up the inked image, then presses onto the product, molding itself around the shape to lay the ink down.

Because the pad deforms, it can print on convex, concave, and uneven surfaces no flat method can touch: pens, golf balls, mug handles, bottle caps, electronics, toys, and automotive parts. The image area is small, and multi-color is possible, but like screen printing each color needs its own plate and its own pass.

**The takeaway:** pad printing is the dominant method for **promotional hard goods**. Often it is the only practical choice when the object cannot lie flat.

## Embroidery: stitched, not printed

**Embroidery** is the odd one out. The design is stitched into the fabric with thread, not printed with ink. It looks and feels premium, textured, and is extremely durable.

Its economics are the interesting part, because they are the **opposite** of screen printing.

- **Digitizing** is a one-time step that converts your artwork into a stitch file, the instructions telling the machine where each stitch goes. It costs around $20 to $150 and stays on file forever, so reorders skip it.
- **Cost is driven by stitch count, not color count.** Thread colors are essentially free once digitized, but a denser, larger design costs more because it has more stitches.

| | Embroidery wins | Screen printing wins |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Order size | Small orders, reorders | Large runs |
| Design type | Small logos on caps, polos, jackets | Big, colorful, photographic art |
| Color cost | Colors are free after digitizing | Every color is another screen |
| Look | Premium, raised, textured | Flat, smooth ink |

**The takeaway:** embroidery is unbeatable for premium logos on caps, polos, and uniforms. It cannot do gradients or photos, and heavy coverage gets stiff.

## One more: dye sublimation

You will hear about **dye sublimation**, so know what it is. Heat turns dye into a gas that bonds into polyester fibers, leaving zero hand feel, no cracking, and true edge-to-edge "all-over" prints.

The hard constraint: it only works on **light or white 100% polyester** (or poly-coated mugs). It cannot print on cotton or dark garments. Best for jerseys, sportswear, and all-over-print fashion.

## Common misconceptions

**"There is one best printing method."** No. The best method changes with every order. The four levers, quantity, colors, fabric, and placement, decide it.

**"More quantity always means a cheaper per-shirt price."** True for screen printing, not for DTG. DTG has no setup to spread out, so its per-unit price stays nearly flat no matter how many you order.

**"Embroidery costs more for more colors."** It costs more for more stitches. Colors are free once the design is digitized.

**"DTG works on any shirt."** Only on cotton. On polyester the ink will not bond, and you will get a faded, peeling mess.

## How to pick the right method

Decide in this order: **order size, then color count, then fabric, then placement.** Here is the practical guide.

1. **Big run, few colors?** Use screen printing. Roughly, it becomes the cheapest option at about 8 to 12 units for one color, 15 to 24 units for two or three colors, and 75 to 100+ units for full-color art.
2. **Small full-color order on cotton?** Use DTG. Think 1 to 30 shirts, samples, or print-on-demand.
3. **Mixed fabrics, polyester, or under 100 units?** Use DTF. One method covers almost any material with a low minimum.
4. **Printing on a curved or hard object?** Use pad printing. Pens, mugs, golf balls.
5. **Premium small logo you will reorder?** Use embroidery. Digitize once, reorder forever.
6. **Light polyester sportswear with all-over art?** Use dye sublimation.
7. **Supply the right artwork.** Vector or spot-color files for screen and pad printing; high-resolution full-color PNG or RGB for DTG and DTF.

A few worked examples make it concrete:

- **500 two-color event tees** → screen printing.
- **12 full-color band shirts on cotton** → DTG.
- **40 mixed-fabric hoodies plus poly bags, one logo** → DTF.
- **250 branded pens** → pad printing.
- **75 company polos and caps, chest logo** → embroidery.

A common shortcut: DTG is usually cheaper under about 36 shirts, screen printing above. Just adjust for color count and fabric.

## Conclusion

The single thing to remember: **match the method to the order, not to habit.** Quantity, colors, fabric, and placement, in that order, point straight at the cheapest, most durable choice every time.

And here is the quiet business angle worth chasing. Screen films and embroidery stitch files are reusable, which means a reorder can honestly skip the setup fee. Telling a customer "your reorder reuses your setup, so there is no setup charge this time" is both true and one of the most persuasive things a print shop can say. The next question is how you store that setup so a reorder a year later still knows exactly how the first one was made.
